## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

CRISIL Ltd, founded in 1987, is India's first and foremost credit rating agency, and a globally diversified analytics company. It is a **majority-owned subsidiary of S&P Global** (66.64% promoter holding), one of the world's three dominant credit rating agencies. This parentage is a critical piece of CRISIL's economic engine.

**The Business Model — A Toll Bridge with a Regulatory Moat:**

CRISIL operates through two segments:

1. **Ratings Services (~28% of revenue, ~51% of profits):** This is a classic toll bridge. SEBI mandates that every bond issuance in India must be rated by at least one credit rating agency. Every bank loan above a threshold requires a rating. CRISIL is the gatekeeper — it charges a fee for every rating mandate, and the mandate is recurring (annual surveillance fees). This segment has operating margins north of 50%, making it one of the most profitable businesses in India. CRISIL commands ~40% market share by revenue in Indian ratings.

2. **Research, Analytics & Solutions (~72% of revenue, ~49% of profits):** This segment includes CRISIL Intelligence (India-focused research), Coalition Greenwich (global research for capital markets firms, acquired in 2020), and the Global Analytics Centre (analytics outsourcing for S&P Global). This is a knowledge services business with a strong recurring revenue base (~70% subscription/multi-year contracts).

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. This is a toll bridge on India's credit markets, backed by regulatory mandate, with an additional knowledge services franchise. Buffett owns Moody's — the exact same business model globally. He would recognise CRISIL instantly.

> Buffett: *"The best business is a royalty on the growth of others, requiring little capital of its own."* CRISIL's Ratings business is precisely this — it collects a royalty every time an Indian company issues debt or borrows from a bank.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Moat Type: Regulatory + Switching Costs + Intangible Assets + Scale**

CRISIL possesses one of the widest and most durable moats of any Indian company. Here's why:

**1. Regulatory Moat (Ratings):** SEBI mandates credit ratings for bond issuances. The number of SEBI-registered rating agencies is limited (only 7 in India), and CRISIL has dominated with ~40% market share by revenue and ~68% by number of rated entities. This is a government-granted oligopoly. New entrants face years of regulatory approval, reputation building, and the chicken-and-egg problem of needing a track record to win mandates.

**2. Brand & Trust (Intangible Asset):** In credit ratings, reputation IS the product. A CRISIL rating carries weight precisely because of 38 years of track record. This cannot be replicated. Banks, investors, and regulators trust CRISIL ratings more than any competitor's. This brand premium allows CRISIL to charge higher fees than CARE Ratings or ICRA.

**3. Switching Costs:** Once a company is rated by CRISIL, switching costs are high — a rating is a long-term relationship with annual surveillance fees. Changing rating agencies creates uncertainty, disrupts existing relationships, and raises questions with investors about why the switch occurred.

**4. S&P Global Parentage (Scale & Knowledge Advantage):** With S&P Global (66.64%) as the majority owner, CRISIL has access to global best practices, proprietary analytical methodologies, technology platforms, and a steady flow of outsourced analytics work from S&P Global. This is a competitive advantage that ICRA (owned by Moody's but with much less integration) and CARE Ratings (no global parent) simply cannot match.

**Key Competitors:**

| Competitor | Market Share (Ratings Revenue) | Threat Level | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| ICRA (Moody's subsidiary) | ~20% | Manageable | Strong brand but smaller scale; less integrated with parent than CRISIL/S&P |
| CARE Ratings | ~15% | Manageable | Has been aggressive on pricing to gain share, but has had credibility issues post-IL&FS |
| India Ratings (Fitch) | ~10% | Manageable | Backed by Fitch but has struggled to gain market share in India |
| Acuité Ratings | ~8% | Manageable | Growing fast in SME/mid-market segment, but tiny relative to CRISIL |
| Fintech/AI disruptors | — | Low-to-Medium | Could disrupt the SME segment with faster, cheaper assessments, but unlikely to challenge large corporate/bank loan ratings where regulatory trust is paramount |

**Is the Moat Enduring?**

Yes, with high conviction. The credit rating business has existed globally for over 150 years (Moody's was founded in 1909, S&P in 1860) and the oligopoly structure has barely changed. India's bond market is still in early stages of development (India's bond-to-GDP ratio is ~20% vs 100%+ for the US), meaning CRISIL's TAM is expanding, not contracting. The business is NOT susceptible to rapid technological change — you cannot automate trust, regulatory track record, or institutional credibility.

**Susceptibility to Change:**

Very low. The way companies issue bonds, banks rate loans, and investors assess credit risk has barely changed in decades. The core product — an opinion on creditworthiness backed by 38 years of track record — is timeless. The research and analytics business may see some disruption from AI, but CRISIL is investing in GenAI capabilities and its proprietary data moats (Coalition Greenwich's benchmarking data, CRISIL's decades of Indian credit data) are defensible.

> Buffett: *"We do not get enthused about change… we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* Credit ratings are one of the most change-resistant businesses in the financial world.
