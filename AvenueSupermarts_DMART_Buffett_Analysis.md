## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### What Does DMart Do?

Avenue Supermarts operates **DMart**, a chain of hypermarket/supermarket stores across India, following an "Everyday Low Cost — Everyday Low Price" (EDLC-EDLP) strategy. The business is beautifully simple: buy groceries and household goods in bulk at the lowest possible cost, sell them at the lowest possible price in no-frills stores, make a small margin on enormous volumes, and repeat. As of March 2026, DMart operates **463 stores** across 12 states and union territories.

### Would Buffett Understand It?

Absolutely. This is a **toll bridge on India's consumption**. Every Indian family needs groceries, household goods, and basic apparel — and DMart positions itself as the lowest-price destination for these essentials. The model is nearly identical to early Walmart or Costco, both businesses Buffett has admired and invested in.

> *"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — Warren Buffett

DMart isn't quite "idiot-proof" — the operational execution required to maintain the lowest-cost position is demanding — but the business model itself is wonderfully simple: **buy cheap, sell cheap, own your real estate, keep costs rock-bottom, and let volume do the work.**

### Business Model Classification

DMart is a **cost-advantage franchise** with elements of a **switching-cost business**:

- **Cost advantage:** Bulk procurement, owned real estate (no rental inflation), lean operations, and a no-frills store format create a structural cost advantage that is difficult to replicate.
- **Switching cost (soft):** Once a household makes DMart their primary weekly shopping destination, the habit loop of driving to the nearest DMart, the consistently low prices, and the wide product range create meaningful behavioural stickiness.
- **NOT a commodity business:** While groceries are commoditised, DMart's cost structure and procurement relationships are differentiated. Competitors sell the same products but cannot match DMart's price-value equation at similar margins.

### The Economic Engine

Revenue is driven by three product categories:
1. **Foods** (groceries, staples, dairy, frozen, fruits & vegetables) — ~55-58% of revenue
2. **Non-Foods / FMCG** (home care, personal care, toiletries) — ~20-22% of revenue
3. **General Merchandise & Apparel** (bed/bath, toys, crockery, garments, appliances) — ~20-23% of revenue

The store-ownership model is critical. Unlike most Indian retailers who lease their stores, DMart **owns approximately 90%+ of its stores**. This eliminates rental escalation risk, provides balance sheet strength, and creates a competitive moat over time as rental costs for competitors keep rising.

Founder **Radhakishan Damani** — a legendary value investor himself — designed DMart with Buffett-like principles: low costs, high integrity, long-term thinking, and the discipline to say "no" to growth that doesn't meet return thresholds.

### Trend Narrative

**Revenue growth** has been consistently strong at a 10-year CAGR of ~25%, though it has decelerated meaningfully from the 30%+ growth era (FY2016-2019) to ~15-17% in FY2024-2025. This deceleration is partly natural (larger base) and partly structural (quick commerce competition in metros, staples deflation).

**Operating margins** have been remarkably stable in the 7-9% band — this is by design. DMart deliberately operates on thin margins to maintain its price leadership. The **best operating margin was FY2018 at 9.0%** and the **worst was FY2021 at 7.2%** (COVID disruption). Recent quarters show margins stabilising around 7.5-8.5%.

**Earnings growth is decelerating sharply.** Net profit grew at 29% CAGR over 10 years but has slowed to just 5-7% in the last two years. EPS growth of ~6-7% in FY2024-2025 is a far cry from the 30-60% growth investors enjoyed historically.

The **tax rate drop from ~35% to ~25%** post-2019 corporate tax cuts provided a one-time earnings boost. That tailwind is now fully reflected.

**Zero dividends** — DMart has never paid a dividend. Every rupee of profit is reinvested into new store construction. This is sensible for a business reinvesting at 13-18% ROE, but it means shareholder returns come entirely from share price appreciation.

> *"The best thing that happens to us is when a great company gets into temporary trouble. We want to buy them when they're on the operating table."* — Warren Buffett

DMart isn't on the operating table, but its growth deceleration and quick commerce headwinds have created the most pessimistic sentiment since its IPO. The question is whether this is temporary (cyclical) or permanent (structural).

### Buffett's Financial Fortress Assessment

**This balance sheet is a fortress.** DMart is essentially debt-free — the Debt/Equity ratio of 0.04 is negligible. The Debt/EBITDA of 0.15x means the company could pay off all its debt with less than 2 months of EBITDA. Interest coverage is ~53x.

> *"We will reject interesting opportunities rather than over-leverage our balance sheet."* — Warren Buffett

**DMart is FCF-negative or barely FCF-positive in most years** — and that is by design. ### Estimating Maintenance vs. Growth CapEx

For DMart, this split is more estimable than for most companies:

- **Maintenance CapEx** = spending to maintain existing stores (repairs, renovations, IT, supply chain maintenance). A reasonable estimate is **1.0-1.5x depreciation** for a physical retail business. Using 1.2x D&A as the proxy: ~₹1,044 Cr in FY2025.
- **Growth CapEx** = new store construction and land acquisition = Total CapEx minus Maintenance = ~₹3,100 - ~₹1,044 = **~₹2,056 Cr in FY2025**.

This means **~66% of CapEx is growth CapEx** — the company is investing roughly ₹2 of every ₹3 spent on CapEx into expanding its store base.

### Analysis

DMart's return profile tells an important story:

1. **ROCE has declined from 24-26% (FY2016-2019) to 18% (FY2025).** This is partly because the massive capital invested in new stores takes 3-5 years to mature to full profitability, temporarily depressing returns on total capital. Pre-IPO, with fewer new stores in the maturation pipeline, returns were higher.

2. **ROE of 13.4%** is decent but not exceptional. For a company trading at 10.7x book value, investors are paying a very steep premium for this level of return. To justify the current P/B, ROE would need to be 25%+ consistently.

3. **ROIC of ~13%** exceeds India's ~10-11% cost of capital, so the business IS creating value, but the spread is narrowing. In the peak years (FY2018-2019), ROIC was 18-19% — nearly double the cost of capital.

4. The **lowest point was FY2021 (COVID year)** when ROE dropped to ~9% and ROCE to 13%. The company recovered strongly in FY2023, proving the cyclical resilience of the model.

> *"The ideal business is one that earns very high returns on capital and that keeps using lots of capital at those high returns. That becomes a compounding machine."* — Warren Buffett

DMart IS a compounding machine — but the compounding rate has moderated. The business earns returns above its cost of capital and reinvests 100% of earnings. However, the declining trend in ROIC from ~18% to ~13% suggests that incremental returns on new stores may be lower than historical averages, likely because: (a) newer stores are in less mature markets, (b) competition is intensifying in metro areas, and (c) the large owned real estate base creates a drag on capital efficiency even though it provides long-term cost advantage.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Primary Moat: Cost Advantage (Strong)**

DMart's cost advantage comes from multiple interlocking sources:
- **Owned real estate:** ~90%+ of stores are owned, eliminating rental inflation that erodes competitor margins every year.
- **Bulk procurement:** Massive purchasing volumes give DMart superior bargaining power with FMCG companies.
- **Lean operations:** No-frills store format, minimal staff, basic store aesthetics.
- **EDLC-EDLP strategy:** Unlike competitors who offer periodic discounts (funded by vendor promotions), DMart maintains everyday low prices — this reduces marketing costs and simplifies operations.
- **Cluster-based expansion:** Opening stores in geographic clusters reduces supply chain costs and improves brand awareness.

**Secondary Moat: Behavioural Switching Costs (Moderate)**

Once a family makes DMart their go-to weekly shopping destination, the habit is sticky. The average DMart basket is large (₹1,500-2,000 per visit), reflecting a pattern of bulk, planned weekly shopping that is deeply embedded in household routines.

### Competitors & Threats

| Competitor | Threat Level | Nature of Competition |
|---|---|---|
| **Reliance Retail** (19,500+ stores) | High | Massive scale, deep pockets, multi-format strategy including JioMart. Has the financial muscle to sustain years of price wars. |
| **Blinkit / Zepto / Swiggy Instamart** (Quick Commerce) | **High and Rising** | 10-minute grocery delivery in metro cities. Directly attacks DMart's metro customer base. Blinkit alone targets 3,000 dark stores by FY27. |
| **Amazon India / Flipkart** | Moderate | General e-commerce; indirect threat through grocery delivery. |
| **Spencer's / More Retail / Star Bazaar** | Low | Smaller scale, weaker unit economics, not existential threats. |
| **Local Kirana stores** (13M+ stores) | Low | Unorganised retail losing share to organised retail over time. |

### Is the Moat Enduring or Eroding?

**The moat is under pressure for the first time in DMart's history.**

Quick commerce represents the first genuine structural threat to DMart's business model. In Tier 1 and Tier 2 cities (which represent a significant portion of DMart's revenue), Blinkit, Zepto, and Swiggy Instamart are capturing the "convenience-first" shopper who previously visited DMart for small-to-medium baskets. DMart's same-store sales growth of **5.6% in Q3 FY26** is among the weakest on record.

However, the threat has important limits:
1. Quick commerce operates at a structural loss — dark stores are expensive, and unit economics remain negative for most players. The model requires continuous capital infusion.
2. Quick commerce is concentrated in top 8-10 cities. DMart's expansion into Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities is relatively insulated.
3. The large, planned weekly basket (₹1,500-2,000) is different from the quick commerce use case (₹200-500 impulse/convenience purchases). DMart may lose the top-up trip but retain the big weekly trip.

### Susceptibility to Change

> *"We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Warren Buffett

This is where DMart gets a **yellow flag, not a red one.** The fundamental human need — buying groceries at the lowest price — is not changing. What IS changing is the delivery mechanism in metro cities. DMart's response (DMart Ready, its online arm) has been slow and underwhelming — the app experience is basic, delivery is slot-based rather than instant, and coverage is limited. However, the physical store model in non-metro India is unlikely to face disruption for at least a decade.

**Honest Moat Assessment: Moderate and partially eroding in metros; still strong in Tier 2/3 cities and for large-basket weekly shopping. The moat is 7/10 — strong but not impregnable.**

## 9. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

### Buffett's $1 Test

For every ₹1 of retained earnings, has the company created at least ₹1 of market value?

- **Total retained earnings from FY2017 to FY2025:** ₹320 + 479 + 806 + 902 + 1,301 + 1,099 + 1,492 + 2,378 + 2,536 + 2,707 = ~₹14,020 Cr (cumulative since FY2016)
- **Change in market capitalisation (IPO price ₹299 in Mar 2017 → ₹3,844 in Mar 2026):** Market cap went from ~₹18,700 Cr (IPO) to ~₹2,50,000 Cr = **₹2,31,300 Cr increase**
- **$1 Test:** ₹2,31,300 Cr / ₹14,020 Cr = **₹16.50 of market value created per ₹1 of retained earnings**

This is an **extraordinary result**. However, much of this reflects the re-rating from IPO pricing to the subsequent market premium rather than pure fundamental value creation. Using book value instead: BV went from ₹3,842 Cr (FY2017) to ₹21,428 Cr (FY2025), an increase of ₹17,586 Cr on ₹14,020 Cr of retained earnings — a more modest but still excellent **₹1.25 per ₹1 retained.**

### Capital Allocation

- **No dividends:** All earnings reinvested. Appropriate for a company with strong reinvestment opportunities.
- **No buybacks:** The stock has always traded at a premium, so buybacks would be value-destructive. Management is correct to avoid them.
- **No acquisitions:** DMart has grown entirely organically. No M&A risk, no goodwill impairment risk. This is the purest organic growth story in Indian retail.
- **ESOPs/Dilution:** Minimal. Shares went from 62.4 Cr (FY2018) to 65.1 Cr (FY2025) — only 4.3% dilution over 7 years. De minimis.

### Management Quality

**Founder: Radhakishan Damani** — Promoter holding of **74.65%** (one of the highest promoter holdings among large-cap Indian companies). This is extraordinary skin in the game. Damani's personal wealth is overwhelmingly tied to DMart's success.

**Key transition:** Anshul Asawa has been appointed as CEO/MD effective February 2026, with Kalpana Unadkat as new Chairperson from April 2026. C. Bhave is stepping down. This represents the first major management transition since founding — a potential risk factor, though the Damani family retains controlling ownership.

> *"When a management team with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — Warren Buffett

The inverse is also true: DMart's economics are so strong that even imperfect management would struggle to ruin them. The Damani family's continued 74.65% ownership ensures alignment with minority shareholders.

**Management candour:** DMart management has acknowledged the quick commerce threat in recent analyst calls — this is a positive sign of honesty rather than denial. However, the response (DMart Ready) has been widely viewed as slow and underwhelming.

### Buffett Verdict

DMart is a **textbook "wonderful business at a crazy price."** The business economics are superb — a low-cost retailer with owned stores, a debt-free balance sheet, a legendary founder-promoter with 74.65% skin in the game, and an enormous growth runway as India's organised retail penetration expands from 12-15% toward 25-30% over the next decade.
