## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Description

Swaraj Engines Ltd was incorporated in 1985 and began operations in 1989. It was jointly promoted by Punjab Tractors Ltd and Kirloskar Oil Engines Ltd. The company has a singular, laser-focused business: **manufacturing diesel engines specifically for Swaraj tractors** in the range of 22 HP to above 65 HP, along with hi-tech engine components.

In 2007, Mahindra & Mahindra (M&M) acquired a majority stake in Punjab Tractors Ltd, bringing Swaraj Engines into the Mahindra ecosystem. Today, M&M (through its Farm Division) is effectively the sole customer — Swaraj Engines is a **captive engine supplier** to the Swaraj tractor brand, which is one of India's top tractor brands.

### Buffett Classification: Toll Bridge / Captive Supplier

This business is best understood as a **toll bridge within the Mahindra ecosystem**. Every Swaraj tractor that rolls off the assembly line needs a Swaraj engine. The company doesn't need to find customers, build brands, or compete for market share — it simply manufactures engines to order for its parent group's tractor division. It is the equivalent of a captive toll collector on a single, well-trafficked bridge.

> **Buffett: "I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."**

Swaraj Engines fits this description almost perfectly. The business is so simple that it barely needs explaining: make engines, ship them to the parent company, collect payment. There is no complex go-to-market strategy, no customer acquisition cost, no marketing budget to speak of, no channel conflicts. The demand is driven entirely by Swaraj tractor sales, which in turn are driven by the Indian agricultural cycle.

> **Buffett: "Never invest in a business you cannot understand."**

A 10-year-old could understand this business: a factory makes tractor engines and sells them to one customer. That's it. This is firmly within any investor's circle of competence.

### Key Business Characteristics
- **Single customer dependency** — Mahindra's Swaraj Division (both a strength and a risk)
- **Zero debt** — The company has essentially no borrowings
- **Asset-light** — Minimal fixed assets relative to revenue
- **High return on capital** — ROCE generally above 35% (range: 32–56%; only dipped to 32% in the FY2016 trough)
- **High dividend payout** — Historically 65–91% payout ratio
- **Negative working capital** — Gets paid faster than it pays suppliers (in most years)
- **No pricing power concerns** — Transfer pricing within the Mahindra group, but the relationship has consistently delivered healthy margins

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: Captive Supplier / Switching Cost Moat (Within Mahindra Group)

Swaraj Engines' moat is unusual — it is a **structural, contractual moat** rather than a traditional competitive moat. The company's position as the sole engine supplier for Swaraj tractors creates an effective monopoly within its niche. The switching costs for Mahindra are enormous: changing the engine supplier would require redesigning the entire tractor platform, re-certifying emissions, retraining service networks, and disrupting an integrated supply chain that has worked seamlessly for 35+ years.

### Moat Assessment

**Is the moat enduring or eroding?**

The moat is **enduring but inherently limited**. As long as Swaraj tractors are sold, Swaraj Engines will supply the engines. The moat doesn't expand — it simply persists. The risk is not that someone steals the business; it's that the parent (Mahindra) could theoretically bring engine manufacturing in-house or merge Swaraj Engines entirely. However, given that this arrangement has persisted since 1989 with no sign of change, and that Swaraj Engines has a separate listed entity with minority shareholders, this risk appears low.

**Market Share Dynamics:**

Swaraj tractors hold approximately 10-12% of the Indian tractor market, making it the #3 or #4 brand. The tractor market is dominated by Mahindra (40%+), TAFE/Massey Ferguson (~15%), and Escorts/Kubota. Swaraj's market share has been broadly stable. Since Swaraj Engines' revenue is entirely tied to Swaraj tractor volumes, the relevant question is: Can the Swaraj brand grow its market share?

The answer appears to be: **modestly, yes**. Revenue has grown at a 10-12% CAGR (10-year basis), outpacing the broader tractor industry growth of ~7-8% CAGR, suggesting some market share gains or better product mix (higher HP engines).

**Key Competitors (to the Swaraj tractor brand, not directly to Swaraj Engines):**

- **Mahindra Tractors** — Interestingly, the biggest competitor is within the same group. But M&M has maintained the Swaraj brand as a separate, value-for-money offering vs. Mahindra's premium positioning.
- **TAFE (Massey Ferguson)** — Strong in South India, ~15% share.
- **Escorts Kubota** — Growing brand, strong in North India.
- **Sonalika (International Tractors)** — Aggressive growth, competitive pricing.

None of these pose an existential threat to Swaraj. The tractor market is oligopolistic with established brand loyalties among farmers.

**Technological Disruption Risk:**

**LOW.** Tractors are mechanical workhorses. Electric tractors are decades away from being practical for Indian agriculture (where tractors operate in remote areas for hours). The diesel engine will remain the dominant tractor powertrain for at least 15-20 years. There is no EV disruption imminent in this space.

> **Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money and even more in the future."**

This business epitomises Buffett's preference for the **absence of change**. A diesel tractor engine in 2026 functions fundamentally the same as one in 2006 or 1996. The manufacturing process is well-understood. The customer relationship is 35+ years old. The product demand is driven by one of the most enduring economic activities on earth — agriculture. There is no app-driven disruption coming. No cloud migration. No AI pivot needed.

### Moat Verdict: MODERATE — Narrow but Durable

The moat is REAL but NARROW. It protects a specific, profitable niche but doesn't have the expansive, self-reinforcing characteristics of a Coca-Cola or Visa moat. It is a captive-supplier moat, which is highly durable but size-limited. The business will persist as long as Swaraj tractors are manufactured — and given Mahindra's commitment to the Swaraj brand, this appears likely for the foreseeable future.

### Part B: Scaling Runway — TAM & Penetration

**Total Addressable Market (TAM):**

The Indian tractor market is approximately 9-10 lakh units per year (FY2025), valued at approximately ₹55,000-60,000 Cr. Swaraj holds ~10-12% market share.
