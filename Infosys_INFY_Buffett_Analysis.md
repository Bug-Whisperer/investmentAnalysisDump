## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Infosys is India's second-largest IT services company (behind TCS), providing consulting, technology, outsourcing, and digital transformation services to global enterprises. Founded in 1981, it earns revenue primarily by deploying skilled technology professionals to solve business problems for Fortune 500 clients — a classic **labour-arbitrage-plus-intellectual-property model**.

**What kind of business is this in Buffett's taxonomy?**

This is a **toll-bridge business with switching costs**, but the toll is NOT permanent — it depends on the continued inability of clients to replicate the services cheaply in-house. Infosys embeds itself deeply into client operations (ERP systems, data infrastructure, cloud migrations, cybersecurity). Once embedded, switching costs are high — replacing an IT services partner mid-project is expensive, risky, and disruptive. The average client relationship spans 5-10+ years.

However, unlike a true Buffett toll bridge (like Coca-Cola or See's Candies), the "toll" here is not protected by a consumer brand or a physical bottleneck. It is protected by **complexity** — the sheer difficulty of enterprise technology — and by **cost arbitrage** — Indian engineers cost a fraction of American or European ones. Both of these moats are under pressure from AI, which is the central question of this analysis.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Yes. The business model is simple: smart people solve technology problems for large companies. The economics are straightforward — revenue per employee minus cost per employee equals profit. The question isn't understanding; it's **durability**.

> *"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — Buffett

Infosys doesn't quite meet this test. It requires competent management to navigate technology transitions — and the current AI transition is the most significant in its history.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Moat Type:** Switching Costs + Scale Advantages + (Eroding) Cost Advantage

**Moat Assessment:**

1. **Switching Costs (STRONG but narrowing):** Enterprise clients deeply integrate IT vendors into mission-critical systems. Replacing Infosys on a multi-year ERP implementation or cloud migration is costly and risky. Client relationships span 5-15+ years — the top 10 clients contribute ~25% of revenue, and $100M+ clients have grown from ~20 to ~40+ over the last decade. These relationships are sticky.

2. **Scale Advantage (MODERATE):** With 300,000+ employees and decades of process optimisation, Infosys can deliver at scale and cost levels that smaller competitors cannot match. The training infrastructure, delivery frameworks (like the Global Delivery Model), and bench management systems took decades to build.

3. **Cost Advantage (ERODING — the critical risk):** The traditional moat for Indian IT was simple: Indian engineers at $20-30/hour vs. American engineers at $100-150/hour. AI coding tools (like Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Devin, Cursor) are beginning to close this gap — not by raising Indian costs, but by dramatically lowering the cost of AI-generated code to near-zero marginal cost. This is the existential question.

4. **Brand & Trust (MODERATE):** Infosys is a trusted brand among CIOs globally. Ethical reputation (named among World's Most Ethical Companies for 6 consecutive years). However, brand in IT services is a hygiene factor — it gets you to the shortlist, not to the contract.

**Key Competitors:**

| Competitor | Revenue | Threat Level | Nature of Threat |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TCS** | ~$29B | Manageable | Peer — similar business model, similar AI risks |
| **Accenture** | ~$65B | Significant | Higher consulting mix, stronger AI pivot, premium positioning |
| **Cognizant** | ~$19B | Manageable | Peer competitor, similar challenges |
| **Wipro / HCLTech** | $10-13B | Manageable | Weaker peers |
| **AI Tools (Claude, Copilot, Devin)** | N/A | **EXISTENTIAL if unchecked** | Replaces human coding hours with near-zero marginal cost AI |
| **Hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP)** | N/A | Significant | Moving up the stack from infrastructure to services |

**The AI Disruption Question — Is This an Existential Threat?**

This is the central question and deserves a brutally honest assessment.

**The Bear Case (Vinod Khosla / Citrini Research view):**
- AI coding agents can perform similar work to entry/mid-level Indian engineers at near-zero marginal cost
- The entire Indian IT model was built on labour arbitrage — AI destroys the arbitrage
- Contract cancellations will accelerate as companies build software internally using AI tools
- Motilal Oswal estimates 9-12% revenue displacement over 3-4 years
- Vinod Khosla predicts India's IT/BPO sectors could "almost completely disappear" by 2030

**The Bull Case (JP Morgan / Infosys management view):**
- AI is another tool that EXPANDS enterprise output rather than replacing vendors
- IT services firms are the "plumbers of the tech world" — essential for integrating complex AI solutions
- Legacy systems dominate enterprises; migration requires human expertise that AI struggles with TODAY
- Infosys has 4,600+ AI projects underway, AI services at 5.5% of revenue and growing
- The $300-400 billion AI services opportunity could MORE than offset displaced revenues
- Regulatory compliance, data privacy, and bespoke client needs slow AI displacement

**The Buffett Assessment — Honest Middle Ground:**

AI is NOT an existential threat to Infosys in the next 3-5 years, but it IS a structural threat to the traditional business model over 5-10 years. Here's why:

The bear case assumes AI replaces ALL human involvement in enterprise IT. This ignores the reality that enterprise technology is messy, complex, and deeply integrated with business processes. The 60% of enterprise revenue that comes from application maintenance, testing, and basic development IS vulnerable to AI automation over time. But the 40% that comes from consulting, complex system integration, AI implementation, and change management is likely to GROW as enterprises adopt AI.

The outcome depends entirely on whether Infosys can transition from selling "human hours" to selling "outcomes and AI-enabled solutions." The early signs are encouraging (5.5% of revenue from AI, 4,600 projects, $4.8B in large deal wins in Q3 FY26 alone), but the transition is far from complete.

> *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Buffett

This quote is the critical Buffett lens. Infosys faces SIGNIFICANT change in how it makes money. Buffett would be cautious here. He would NOT call it a pass — but he would demand a very attractive price to compensate for the uncertainty.

**Is the moat enduring or eroding?** The traditional moat (labour cost arbitrage) is ERODING. The potential new moat (AI implementation expertise at scale) is FORMING but unproven. This is a **moat-in-transition** — the most dangerous phase for any business, and the phase where Buffett is typically most cautious.

## 9. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

**Buffett's $1 Test:**

Over FY2018-FY2025 (7 years):
- Total Retained Earnings (cumulative): ~₹42,000 crore (Net Income ~₹1,46,000 crore minus Dividends+Buybacks ~₹1,04,000 crore)
- Change in Market Cap: From ~₹5.6L crore (at ₹1,300 in FY2018) to ~₹5.1L crore today = **NEGATIVE ₹50,000 crore**

**RESULT: FAILS the $1 test.** The market has destroyed value on retained earnings over this period. However, this is primarily a function of PE multiple compression (from ~22-25x to ~18x) driven by AI fear, not operational deterioration. If we judge by book value growth (₹149 to ₹232 = 55% increase), the retained capital has been deployed well.

**Capital Allocation Track Record:**
- Five buybacks since 2017, most executed at reasonable prices. The 2025 buyback at ₹1,800/share looks prescient given the subsequent decline to ₹1,250.
- 85% of FCF returned to shareholders — among the most shareholder-friendly policies in Indian markets.
- Conservative M&A — no value-destructive large acquisitions. Bolt-on deals like Versent add capability without overpaying.
- SBC at ~0.7% of revenue is modest and not a concern.

**Management Candor:**
CEO Salil Parekh has been reasonably transparent about AI risks and opportunities. The company's Investor AI Day (Feb 2026) directly addressed the existential threat narrative, presenting concrete data (5.5% of revenue from AI, 4,600 projects, 30+ new AI service offerings). Management is not hiding from the challenge.

**Insider Ownership:** Promoter holding is low at 14.5% (Nandan Nilekani and co-founders). This is below ideal for Buffett's "skin in the game" test, but is typical for mature, widely-held IT companies. FII holding at 30% and DII at 41% indicates strong institutional confidence.

> *"In evaluating people, you look for three qualities: integrity, intelligence, and energy. And if they don't have the first, the other two will kill you."* — Buffett

Infosys management scores well on integrity (no governance scandals, 6x Ethical Companies award) and intelligence (navigating multiple technology transitions). Energy is being directed toward AI transformation — the right focus.
