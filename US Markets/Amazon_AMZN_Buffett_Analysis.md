## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Amazon is the world's largest e-commerce marketplace, the dominant cloud computing platform (AWS), a major digital advertising player, and a logistics behemoth. The business model is deceptively simple on the surface — sell everything to everyone, cheaper and faster than anyone else — but beneath it lies one of the most complex operational machines ever built.

**Buffett Classification:** Amazon is a **toll bridge + consumer franchise + switching-cost business**, all rolled into one:

- **AWS is a toll bridge:** Every company running on AWS pays Amazon a recurring fee for compute, storage, and AI services. Switching costs are enormous — migrating workloads is expensive, risky, and time-consuming. AWS has ~31% global cloud market share.
- **The Marketplace is a toll bridge:** ~60% of Amazon's retail units are sold by third-party sellers who pay Amazon referral fees, FBA fees, and advertising fees. Amazon takes a cut of every transaction without holding inventory risk.
- **Prime is a consumer franchise:** 200M+ global members paying annual subscriptions create a self-reinforcing flywheel — more Prime members → more demand → more sellers → more selection → more Prime members.
- **Advertising is a toll bridge on top of a toll bridge:** Sellers and brands pay Amazon to be visible on the platform that Amazon already controls.

Buffett: *"The key to investing is determining the competitive advantage of any given company and, above all, the durability of that advantage."* Amazon's advantage is its flywheel — low prices attract customers, which attract sellers, which increase selection, which further lowers prices. This flywheel has been spinning for 30 years and is now virtually impossible to replicate.

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Moat Type: Multi-layered — Network Effects + Switching Costs + Cost Advantage + Brand**

**1. Network Effects (Marketplace):** More buyers attract more sellers, which increases selection and competition among sellers, which lowers prices, which attracts more buyers. This two-sided marketplace flywheel has been compounding for 25+ years and is virtually impossible to replicate from scratch. ~60% of units sold are from third-party sellers who depend on Amazon's traffic and fulfilment infrastructure.

**2. Switching Costs (AWS):** Enterprises build their entire technology stack on AWS — databases, compute, machine learning models, security configurations. Migrating to Azure or GCP is a multi-year, multi-million dollar project with significant execution risk. AWS customer retention rates are estimated at 95%+. The more services a customer uses, the stickier they become.

**3. Cost Advantage (Logistics & Scale):** Amazon has built the largest logistics network in the US, surpassing UPS and FedEx in package volume. This scale allows same-day and next-day delivery at costs competitors cannot match. Fulfilment cost per unit declines with volume — a textbook scale advantage.

**4. Brand (Prime):** Amazon is the default starting point for product searches in the US (over 60% of product searches start on Amazon, not Google). Prime membership creates habitual purchasing behaviour that is extremely difficult to break.

**Key Competitors:**
- **E-Commerce:** Walmart (the only credible US competitor, gaining share but still far behind online), Shopify (enables DTC but doesn't directly compete), Temu/Shein (price-focused, low-quality threat)
- **Cloud:** Microsoft Azure (~24% share, closing the gap in enterprise), Google Cloud (~11% share, strong in AI/ML). AWS maintains ~31% market share.
- **Advertising:** Google and Meta dominate digital advertising, but Amazon's ad business (~$57B in 2025) is the fastest-growing major ad platform, with the unique advantage of purchase-intent data.

**Buffett's Disruption Test:** *"We're looking for the absence of change."* Amazon is somewhat paradoxical here — it operates in rapidly changing technology sectors BUT it is typically the DISRUPTOR, not the disrupted. AWS created cloud computing; Amazon redefined retail logistics; Alexa pioneered voice computing. The risk is not that Amazon gets disrupted — it's that its massive capital investments (especially in AI) may not generate the expected returns. However, with 30 years of capital allocation excellence, management has earned the benefit of the doubt.

**Moat Verdict: WIDE and WIDENING.** The moat has actually gotten stronger over the last 5 years as Amazon's logistics and AWS infrastructure advantages have compounded. The advertising business is a new moat layer that didn't exist 5 years ago.

## 12. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

**Buffett's $1 Test (2016-2025):**
- Total retained earnings over 10 years: $246.4B
- Change in market capitalisation: ~$2344B
- **For every $1 retained, $9.51 of market value was created. ✅ PASS (emphatically)**

**Management Assessment:**

- **Andy Jassy (CEO since 2021):** Former head of AWS, deeply technical, operationally focused. Has delivered a dramatic cost efficiency programme since 2022 — headcount reduction, warehouse optimisation, regional fulfilment model — that drove the margin expansion. His track record at AWS (from $0 to $129B revenue) speaks for itself.

- **Jeff Bezos (Executive Chairman):** Still owns ~9% ($240B+), maintaining significant skin in the game. His long-term vision and willingness to sacrifice short-term earnings for long-term dominance has been the defining strategic advantage.

Every major capital allocation decision over the last 20 years — AWS, Prime, FBA, logistics, Alexa (arguable), advertising — has created enormous shareholder value. The one notable mistake was the Rivian investment ($12.7B write-down in 2022), but this is minor in context.

- **SBC:** Declining as a percentage of revenue (from 4.2% in 2023 to 2.7% in 2025), though still meaningful in absolute terms.

Buffett: *"You're looking for three things: intelligence, energy, and integrity. And if they don't have the last one, don't even bother with the first two."* Amazon's management scores high on all three.

## 21. REGULATORY RISKS & POLICY HEADWINDS

**Primary Regulatory Risks:**

1. **Antitrust (FTC):** The FTC lawsuit alleging monopolistic practices in the marketplace (favouring own products, penalising sellers who sell for less elsewhere) is ongoing. The $2.5B settlement in Q3 2025 resolved one part but broader litigation continues. However, Amazon has survived 25 years of antitrust scrutiny — consumers love the service, which makes a heavy-handed breakup politically difficult.

2. **EU Digital Markets Act (DMA):** Amazon is designated as a "gatekeeper" under DMA, requiring it to allow third-party app stores, share data with sellers, and ensure interoperability. Compliance costs are manageable but could modestly reduce marketplace take rates in Europe.

3. **AI Regulation:** Evolving regulatory frameworks around AI could affect AWS's AI services (Bedrock, SageMaker). However, Amazon is likely to benefit from regulation that raises compliance costs — incumbents with resources to comply gain advantage over smaller competitors.

4. **Labour Regulation:** Unionisation efforts at Amazon warehouses could increase labour costs. Amazon's warehouse workforce is ~1.5M people — a 10% increase in labour costs would reduce operating income by ~$8-10B (roughly 10% of OI).

5. **Tariffs:** The Trump administration's tariff policies could increase costs for goods sourced from China. Amazon's 3P marketplace model partially insulates it (sellers bear the cost), but 1P retail margins could be pressured.

**Buffett's Verdict on Regulatory Risk: (a) Manageable and potentially overpriced by the market.** Amazon has navigated regulatory headwinds for its entire existence. The regulatory framework is unlikely to destroy the business franchise, and may actually strengthen the moat by raising barriers to entry for smaller competitors.
