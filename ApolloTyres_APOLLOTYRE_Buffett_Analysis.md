## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Apollo Tyres Ltd, established in 1972, is one of India's largest tyre manufacturers. The business is straightforward: it buys natural rubber, synthetic rubber, carbon black, and chemicals, manufactures tyres in seven factories (five in India, one in the Netherlands, one in Hungary), and sells them under the **Apollo** and **Vredestein** brands to two customer channels — Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) and the replacement market — across 100+ countries.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. You buy rubber, you make tyres, you sell them. This is a classic **commodity-with-brand-overlay** business. The raw material (rubber) is a commodity, the end product (tyres) is quasi-commodity, but branding, distribution, and OEM relationships provide partial differentiation.

**Business Model Type:** This is **NOT a toll bridge** (no recurring fees on transactions). It is **NOT a consumer franchise** (consumers don't crave Apollo tyres the way they crave Coca-Cola). It has **moderate switching costs** (OEM approvals take years, and fleet operators develop supplier relationships), but fundamentally it sits closer to the **commodity** end of the Buffett spectrum.

> *Buffett: "The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business."*

Apollo has **limited pricing power**. When rubber prices spike, tyre companies raise prices — but competition constrains how much and how fast. When rubber prices drop, the benefit accrues to margins temporarily before competition forces prices down. This is the hallmark of a commodity-adjacent business.

**Revenue Split (FY25):** ~87% India, ~11% Europe, ~2% Others.
**Segment Split:** ~28-29% market share in Truck & Bus (TBR), ~20% in Passenger Car Radial (PCR) in India. Strong #2 position behind MRF domestically.

**10-Year Average ROE: ~9%**
**5-Year Average ROE: ~7%**

This is the single most damning metric for a Buffett analysis. **A 9-10% ROE is mediocre.** It barely covers the cost of equity (which is ~11-12% for an Indian mid-cap). The business is, on average, **not earning above its cost of capital.**

FY24's 12.4% ROE was the cyclical peak — rubber was cheap, prices were sticky, debt was falling. FY25 reverted to 7.6% as rubber costs rose and pricing power proved limited.

> *Buffett: "The best business to own is one that over an extended period can employ large amounts of incremental capital at very high rates of return."*

Apollo Tyres has employed large amounts of incremental capital (₹15,000+ Cr in capex over the last decade) at **mediocre rates of return**. This is the opposite of a Buffett business.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Moat Assessment: NARROW MOAT — and potentially narrowing.**

**Moat Sources (What exists):**

1. **Brand Recognition:** Apollo is a well-known brand in Indian T&B and PCR segments. Vredestein is a respected European premium brand. But tyre brands don't command the loyalty of consumer brands — price is a major factor.

2. **OEM Relationships / Switching Costs:** Getting approved as an OEM supplier takes 2-3 years of testing. Once approved, there's inertia. Apollo supplies to Tata, Maruti, Hyundai, etc. This provides moderate recurring revenue.

3. **Distribution Network:** 6,200+ dealer outlets in India, 5,400+ in Europe. This is hard to replicate quickly, but MRF has an even larger network.

4. **Scale Advantage:** As India's #2 tyre maker, Apollo has manufacturing scale. But tyre manufacturing is not winner-take-all — MRF, CEAT, JK Tyre, and Bridgestone all operate profitably.

**Moat Weaknesses (What's eroding):**

1. **No Pricing Power:** Tyre pricing is largely dictated by rubber costs + competition. Apollo cannot sustainably charge a premium over MRF or CEAT — consumers will switch.

2. **Raw Material Vulnerability:** Rubber (~60-65% of costs) is volatile and uncontrollable. Margins are at the mercy of commodity cycles.

3. **European Operations Underperform:** The European business has consistently been a drag on consolidated margins and returns. It faces intense competition from Continental, Michelin, and Bridgestone on premium, and Chinese imports on value.

4. **Competition Intensifying:** Chinese tyre imports into India are rising. Fintech-enabled distribution is reducing dealer loyalty. EV tyre requirements may shift competitive dynamics.

**Key Competitors:**
- **MRF:** India's #1, stronger brand, higher margins, zero debt. THE competitive threat.
- **CEAT (RPG Group):** Aggressive in PCR, growing fast.
- **JK Tyre:** Strong in TBR, but more leveraged.
- **Balkrishna Industries (BKT):** Niche OHT player with 15%+ net margins — shows what specialisation can do.
- **Bridgestone, Michelin, Continental:** Global giants present in India.

**Susceptibility to Change:**

This is where the Buffett lens gets interesting. Tyres as a product category are remarkably **resistant to change** — vehicles will need rubber tyres for decades. The basic product hasn't fundamentally changed in 50 years. BUT:
- EVs require different tyre characteristics (heavier vehicles, instant torque, noise reduction)
- Connected/smart tyres could shift value to technology players
- Sustainability pressures may change raw material sourcing

> *Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."*

The tyre industry's **product** is stable, but the **competitive dynamics** are not — Chinese imports, EV disruption, and digital distribution are all change vectors. The moat exists but is narrow and not widening.

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK — Can This Business Scale and Is It Scaling?

### Part A: The Unit Economics of Scaling

**Revenue Scalability:** MODERATE. Each new tyre requires rubber, energy, and manufacturing — this is a physical goods business with meaningful marginal costs. It is NOT a zero-marginal-cost business like software. However, there is operating leverage from fixed manufacturing overheads.

**Profit Scalability — Incremental Margin Analysis:**

| Period | Revenue Change (₹ Cr) | Op. Profit Change (₹ Cr) | Incremental Op. Margin | Current OPM |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY21→FY22 | +3,551 | -223 | **-6.3%** | 12% |
| FY22→FY23 | +3,620 | +738 | **20.4%** | 14% |
| FY23→FY24 | +810 | +1,135 | **140.1%** | 18% |
| FY24→FY25 | +745 | -873 | **-117.2%** | 14% |
| FY25→TTM | +1,436 | +317 | **22.1%** | 14% |

The incremental margins are wildly volatile — swinging from +140% to -117%. This is NOT the signature of a business with genuine operating leverage. This is the signature of a **commodity business** where margins are driven by raw material costs, not by scaling efficiencies.

**Capital Scalability:**

| Metric | FY2020 | FY2021 | FY2022 | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| CapEx as % of Revenue | 17.2% | 13.5% | 5.6% | 1.9% | 2.8% | 0.8% |
| Revenue / CapEx | 5.8x | 7.4x | 17.9x | 51.4x | 36.0x | 129.3x |
| Working Capital Days | -22 | -27 | -23 | -6 | 8 | 22 |

CapEx intensity has dropped dramatically as the company harvests prior investments, but working capital days are expanding worryingly (from -27 to +22). The next capex cycle (₹5,810 Cr) will reset CapEx/Revenue to higher levels.

### Part B: Scaling Runway

**TAM:** India tyre market ~₹1,00,000 Cr, growing at ~7-8% CAGR. Apollo's current revenue ~₹26,000 Cr → ~26% domestic revenue share. India tyre market is expected to reach USD 27.67 billion (~₹2.3 lakh Cr) by 2034.

**Runway:** Reasonable — the Indian market is growing with vehicle penetration, but Apollo's growth has barely matched the market. Market share has been roughly stable.

**S-Curve Position:** **Growth Phase (but decelerating).** Apollo is a mature player in a growing market. Revenue growth has decelerated from 18% (FY19) to 3% (FY25). The business is past the inflection point and approaching the linear-growth-with-market phase.

### Part C: Scaling Verdict

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue scalability | 4 | Physical goods, meaningful marginal cost |
| Profit scalability | 3 | Margins driven by rubber, not scale |
| Capital scalability | 5 | Post-capex harvest phase, but new cycle starting |

**Classification:** Moderate Scaler (Kraft Heinz analogy) — can grow but requires proportional capital; limited operating leverage. The rubber cost dependency ensures that scaling doesn't meaningfully improve unit economics.

## 9. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

**Promoter Family:** Onkar S. Kanwar (Chairman) and Neeraj Kanwar (Vice Chairman & MD). The Kanwar family has led Apollo since its founding. Promoter holding: **36.93%** (Dec 2025) — meaningful but declining from 44% in FY17.

**Buffett's $1 Test:**

Over FY16-FY25 (10 years):
- Total Retained Earnings: Revenue retained after dividends ≈ ₹6,000+ Cr
- Market Cap change: From ~₹14,000 Cr (FY16 at ~₹275/share) to ~₹26,700 Cr now (₹420/share)
- Market Cap gain: ~₹12,700 Cr
- Per ₹1 retained: ~₹2.1 of market value created

This passes the $1 test at first glance, but much of the market cap gain reflects broader market re-rating (Sensex doubled in this period). Adjusting for that, the value creation from retained earnings is marginal.

**Capital Allocation Track Record:**

- **Hungary plant investment:** Questionable. European margins have been consistently below India. The ~₹3,000 Cr invested in Hungary has generated mediocre returns.
- **Debt management:** Improved significantly in FY22-25. The deleveraging has been disciplined.
- **Dividends:** Reasonable payout of 22-28% in recent years.
- **Share buybacks:** None. This is a missed opportunity given the stock trades below intrinsic value.
- **Equity dilution:** 25% dilution over the decade through issuances — value-destructive.

**Management Candour:** Reasonable. Concall transcripts show honest discussion of challenges, though there's a tendency to emphasise positives.

**Insider Ownership Trend:** Promoter holding declining from 44% to 37% is a **yellow flag**. Management is not buying more shares despite the stock being at relatively low valuations.

> *Buffett: "I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."*

Apollo Tyres' management is competent but not exceptional. The business is not so wonderful that management quality is irrelevant — in a cyclical, capital-intensive, commodity-adjacent business, capital allocation skill matters enormously, and the track record is mixed.
