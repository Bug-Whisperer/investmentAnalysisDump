## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Shalimar Paints Ltd, incorporated in 1902, is one of India's oldest paint companies. It manufactures, sells, and distributes decorative paints, industrial coatings, and marine coatings through three manufacturing facilities (Chennai, Nashik, and Sikandrabad in Uttar Pradesh), 48+ sales depots, 6,670+ dealers, and 7,300+ retail touch points. The company employs approximately 633 people.

**Business Model:** Shalimar operates as a branded paint manufacturer selling through a dealer-distribution network. It is a **commodity-adjacent branded business** — paints themselves are differentiated primarily by brand perception, dealer relationships, and colour/finish quality, but raw material costs (titanium dioxide, solvents, resins) are commodity-driven. The business is capital-intensive (manufacturing plants) with moderate working capital requirements.

**Products:** Decorative paints (interior/exterior emulsions, enamels, distemper, primers, putty, waterproofing) and industrial coatings (marine, floor, epoxy). Recently launched HERO 5-in-1 Super Premium line.

**Ownership:** Part of the Ratan Jindal (O.P. Jindal group) and S.S. Jhunjhunwala group. As of March 2026, IPO-bound Infra.Market holds ~52.85% shareholding (via Hella Infra Market Pvt Ltd). Total promoter holding: ~74.96%. Promoters have pledged 70.5% of their holding — a significant red flag.

**Would Buffett Understand It?** Yes — paint manufacturing is simple to understand. Buy raw materials, manufacture paint, sell through dealers. However, Buffett would immediately note that Shalimar is a **tiny player in a market dominated by an oligopoly** (Asian Paints ~55%, Berger ~20%, Kansai Nerolac ~15%). With less than 1% market share, Shalimar has virtually no pricing power and no durable competitive advantage.

> *"The most important thing in evaluating a business is figuring out how big the moat is around the business."* — Warren Buffett

Shalimar's moat is essentially **non-existent**. It has brand heritage (120+ years) but negligible brand power relative to Asian Paints, Berger, or Nerolac. It has no cost advantage, no network effects, and trivial switching costs. This is a commodity business masquerading as a branded business.

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Moat Type: NONE.** Shalimar Paints has no identifiable economic moat.

**Market Position:** The Indian paint market is ~₹96,000 Cr (USD 11.45 billion, total paints and coatings including decorative, industrial, automotive, and unorganized segments) and growing at ~9% CAGR. Shalimar's revenue of ₹599 Cr represents approximately **0.6% of the total market** — a rounding error in an industry dominated by:
- Asian Paints: ~55% share of the organized decorative segment (~₹34,000 Cr consolidated revenue), 26% ROCE
- Berger Paints: ~20% organized decorative share (~₹11,500 Cr revenue), 25% ROCE
- Kansai Nerolac: ~15% organized decorative share (~₹7,800 Cr revenue), 13% ROCE
- AkzoNobel India: ~7% organized decorative share

> *Note: Market share percentages cited above refer to the organized decorative paints segment, which is a subset of the ₹96,000 Cr total market (decorative + industrial + automotive + unorganized). The organized decorative segment accounts for roughly 50-55% of the total market.*

Additionally, new well-capitalised entrants (Birla Opus, JSW Paints, Grasim/UltraTech) are investing thousands of crores to enter this market, intensifying competition at every price point. The competitive environment is **the worst it has been in three decades** for small players.

**Key Competitors & Threat Assessment:**
- **Asian Paints:** Existential threat. Dominant in distribution, brand, technology, cost efficiency. Spending ₹8,750 Cr on capacity expansion alone.
- **Berger Paints:** Existential threat. Rapidly gaining share with aggressive dealer expansion.
- **Birla Opus / Grasim:** Existential threat for small players. Entering with massive capital and the UltraTech cement distribution network.
- **JSW Paints:** Significant threat. Acquired AkzoNobel India; well-capitalised.
