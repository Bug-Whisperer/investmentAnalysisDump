## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Indigo Paints Ltd, incorporated in 2000 and headquartered in Pune, manufactures and sells **decorative paints** across India. The product range spans interior and exterior emulsions, enamels, putties, primers, wood coatings, floor coats, and roof coats. The company operates through three manufacturing plants (Rajasthan, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu) and recently commenced production at a new solvent-based plant (12,000 KLPA capacity) in Jodhpur, with a water-based plant (90,000 KLPA) expected by June 2026.

**Business Model — The Category-Creator Strategy:**

Indigo Paints is best understood as a **niche differentiator in a commodity-adjacent industry**. While decorative paints are broadly fungible (wall paint is wall paint), Indigo has carved out a defensible niche by introducing **category-creating products** — Metallic Emulsions, Tile Coat Emulsions, Bright Ceiling Coat, Floor Coat, and others — products that did not previously exist in the Indian decorative paint market. The company claims **~80-90% market share** in several of these differentiated categories.

This is a classic **consumer franchise model** with elements of a **switching-cost business**: once a contractor or homeowner discovers a product category (e.g., metallic wall finishes) that only Indigo offers, the brand becomes synonymous with the category. The distribution network (now spanning 27 states and 7 union territories) and dealer loyalty add friction to switching.

Would Buffett understand it? Absolutely. Paint is simple, essential, and consumed repeatedly. Houses need repainting every 5-7 years. Urbanisation and rising incomes expand the addressable market. The product is relatively low-cost relative to total construction spend (paint is <5% of project cost), so consumers prioritise quality and brand over price — the hallmark of a Buffett-friendly business.

> *"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — Buffett

Indigo's business is simple to understand: buy raw materials (titanium dioxide, pigments, resins, solvents), manufacture paint, distribute through dealers, and earn a margin. The moat question — whether Indigo's niche differentiation and distribution reach can withstand the onslaught from Asian Paints (₹35,000 Cr revenue), Berger Paints (₹11,000 Cr), and new entrants like Birla Opus and JSW Paints — is the central issue this analysis must address.

**Subsidiary: Apple Chemie India Ltd** — a fast-growing subsidiary (revenue up 31.5% YoY in Q3 FY26 consolidated), which adds to the consolidated picture. However, standalone Indigo Paints is the primary economic engine.

> *"Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre."* — Buffett

The business earns comfortably above its cost of capital (ROCE of 20% vs. ~10-11% cost of capital). The key question is whether ROE can recover from the current 14.7% as the new capacity ramps up and asset turnover improves. If ROE can sustain at 15-17%, this is a good (not great) compounder. If ROE continues declining, the investment thesis weakens.

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**Industry Structure:**

The Indian decorative paints industry (~₹80,000+ Cr market) is an oligopoly dominated by Asian Paints (~50% market share), Berger Paints (~18%), and Kansai Nerolac (~10%). Indigo Paints is among the smaller but fastest-growing players, ranked approximately 5th-6th by revenue.

**Moat Assessment:**

| Moat Type | Strength | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand** | Moderate | Recognised in niche categories but not in the same league as Asian Paints' brand equity. Aggressive advertising (Dhoni endorsement, IPL) has built awareness. |
| **Category Creation** | Strong (but narrow) | 80-90% market share in differentiated products (Metallic Emulsions, Tile Coat). This is a genuine innovation moat — but it can be replicated by larger players over time. |
| **Distribution** | Growing but still weak vs. leaders | Dealer network across 27 states. Asian Paints has 70,000+ dealers vs. Indigo's smaller network. |
| **Switching Costs** | Low-Moderate | Paint is relatively low-switching-cost. Dealer/contractor relationships provide some stickiness. |
| **Cost Advantage** | None | Indigo is a sub-scale producer vs. Asian Paints. No cost moat. |
| **Network Effects** | None | Not applicable to paints. |

**Key Competitors and Threats:**

1. **Asian Paints** (₹35,000 Cr revenue): The 800-pound gorilla. Has the distribution, brand, technology, and capital to replicate any product Indigo creates. The existential question for Indigo is whether Asian Paints decides to aggressively compete in Indigo's niche categories. **Threat level: Existential.**

2. **Berger Paints** (₹11,000 Cr revenue): Second largest, steadily gaining share. Strong in East India. **Threat: Manageable.**

3. **Birla Opus** (Grasim Industries' paint venture): The most significant new threat. Birla is investing ₹10,000 Cr to build paint capacity and aggressively discounting to gain market share. This has pressured the entire industry's pricing. **Threat: High — the primary reason for Indigo's growth deceleration.**

4. **JSW Paints**: Another new entrant, but smaller scale. **Threat: Moderate.**

**Is the moat enduring or eroding?**

The moat is **narrow and potentially eroding**. Indigo's category-creation advantage was strongest when it was the only player in niche segments. As larger competitors (Asian Paints, Birla) expand their product ranges and match Indigo's innovations, the niche advantage narrows. The growth deceleration to 1.8% in FY25 may be an early sign of this dynamic.

**Susceptibility to Change:**

Decorative paints is a remarkably stable business. The basic product hasn't changed fundamentally in decades. Houses will need painting for the foreseeable future. There is no existential technology disruption risk. Buffett would appreciate this absence of change. The risk is competitive, not technological — and competitive risk in a growing market is more manageable than disruption risk.

> *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Buffett

The paint industry fits this criterion perfectly. The question is whether Indigo specifically has a durable enough moat, or whether it will be ground down by the larger players' distribution and scale advantages.
