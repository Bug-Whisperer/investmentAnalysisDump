## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Description

JK Paper Ltd, established in 1962, is India's leading manufacturer of office papers, coated papers, and packaging boards. The company operates large-scale paper mills (primarily at Rayagada in Odisha and Fort Songadh in Gujarat) with integrated pulp manufacturing facilities. It also has plantation-based raw material sourcing through social forestry programmes.

The business model is straightforward: procure wood pulp and waste paper, process them through capital-intensive mills, and sell paper and packaging board products. Revenue comes from two broad segments — **Paper & Board** (writing/printing paper, copier paper, coated paper) and **Packaging Board** (virgin fibre-based packaging). The company has also acquired Sirpur Paper Mills and holds a stake in BPPL (packaging).

### Buffett Business Classification

**This is a commodity business with partial franchise characteristics.** JK Paper is not a toll bridge (no recurring, non-discretionary revenue stream), not a consumer franchise (paper is not branded in the consumer's mind the way Coca-Cola is), and not a high switching-cost business (customers can switch paper suppliers relatively easily). It is primarily a **commodity manufacturer** with some differentiation through quality, brand recognition in the B2B copier paper segment, and integrated operations.

> *Buffett: "The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by 10%, then you've got a terrible business."*

JK Paper has **limited pricing power**. Paper prices are cyclical and driven by global supply-demand dynamics, raw material costs, and import competition. The company's margins have swung wildly — from 8% OPM in FY14 to 31% in FY23 and back down to 14% in FY25 — a hallmark of a commodity business. Buffett would understand this business easily (it's simple), but he would **not** be enthused by its commodity economics.

> *Buffett: "We like to buy businesses with some untapped pricing power."* JK Paper does NOT have untapped pricing power — it is a price-taker in global paper markets.

### Trend Narrative

The P&L tells a classic **boom-bust commodity cycle story**:

- **FY2019-FY2023 was the golden era**: Paper prices surged globally post-COVID due to supply constraints, Chinese capacity shutdowns, and packaging demand. Revenue nearly doubled from ₹3,257 Cr (FY19) to ₹6,437 Cr (FY23). OPM expanded from 27% to a peak of **31%** in FY23. Net profit hit a staggering ₹1,208 Cr — a level likely never to be repeated in the near term.

- **FY2024-FY2025 is the reversion**: Revenue plateaued (~₹6,700 Cr) but margins collapsed. OPM fell from 31% → 24% → 14%. Net profit crashed **66%** from ₹1,208 Cr to ₹412 Cr. EPS fell from ₹70.59 to ₹24.19. The TTM EPS of ₹15.33 suggests further deterioration.

- **Earnings growth is NOT outpacing revenue growth** — it is vastly underperforming. Revenue grew ~1% in FY25 while net profit fell 63%. This is operating DELEVERAGE — high fixed costs (depreciation up from ₹128 Cr in FY19 to ₹332 Cr in FY25 due to capacity expansion) amplify margin compression.

- The **share count stabilised** at ~16.9 Cr after buybacks in 2020 (previously diluted through FCCB conversions in 2016-2018 from ~14.9 Cr to ~17.8 Cr).

> *Buffett: "Only when the tide goes out do you discover who has been swimming naked."* The tide went out on paper prices in FY24-25, and JK Paper's commodity nature was fully exposed — OPM halved, net profit cratered. This is NOT a business with durable earnings power.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: Narrow — Cost Advantage (Partial) + Scale**

JK Paper has some competitive advantages, but they are not durable enough to constitute a wide moat:

1. **Scale advantage:** JK Paper is India's largest paper manufacturer with integrated pulp mills. Scale provides some cost advantage in procurement, production efficiency, and distribution. However, scale alone is not a moat — commodity businesses with scale advantages can still earn poor returns when supply exceeds demand.

2. **Integrated operations:** Captive pulp manufacturing and plantation-based raw material sourcing provide partial insulation from raw material cost volatility. The company's social forestry programme covering ~150,000+ acres ensures some supply security.

3. **Brand recognition in B2B:** JK Copier is a recognised brand in the Indian office paper segment, providing some pricing support. However, this is B2B brand recognition, not consumer brand loyalty — it's easier to displace.

4. **Import substitution position:** India imports significant paper/board quantities. Domestic manufacturers benefit from logistics advantages and occasional government support (anti-dumping duties).

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| West Coast Paper | Manageable | Smaller scale, similar integrated model |
| ITC (Paperboards) | Significant | Large conglomerate with deeper pockets, integrated |
| International paper imports (ASEAN, China) | Significant | Low-cost imports can flood market during overcapacity |
| Seshasayee Paper, Andhra Paper | Manageable | Smaller players |
| Digital disruption (paperless offices) | Structural (Long-term) | Writing/printing paper demand structurally declining |
| Packaging players (non-paper alternatives) | Moderate | Plastics, flexible packaging compete with paperboard |

### Is the Moat Enduring or Eroding?

**The moat is NARROW and under structural pressure:**

- **Writing/printing paper demand is structurally declining** globally as digitisation reduces office paper consumption. JK Paper has pivoted towards packaging board, but this segment is more competitive.

- **The business IS susceptible to change.** The paper industry is undergoing a fundamental shift from writing/printing paper to packaging board. This is a multi-decade transition. JK Paper is adapting, but adaptation requires capital and carries execution risk.

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money and even more in the future."*

JK Paper faces significant change on multiple fronts — demand mix shifting, digital disruption of core product, and cyclical pricing. **Buffett would likely pass on this business because the landscape is changing too much to predict 10-20 years forward with confidence.**

- **Market share** is relatively stable in India, but the Indian paper market itself is being reshaped by imports, digitisation, and the shift to packaging.
