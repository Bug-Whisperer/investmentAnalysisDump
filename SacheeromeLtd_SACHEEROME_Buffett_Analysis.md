## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Sacheerome Ltd is a **fragrance and flavour (F&F) manufacturer** — a B2B supplier of concentrated fragrances and flavours to FMCG companies across India and globally. Founded in 1992 by Mr. Manoj Arora (a third-generation entrepreneur in the F&F industry), the company creates custom formulations for personal care, home care, fabric care, fine fragrances, food & beverages, bakery, confectionery, oral care, and more. Clients include marquee FMCG names like ITC, Wipro, Raymond, Dabur, Emami, and Himalaya. The company exports to 30+ countries, with a growing presence in the Middle East and Africa.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Yes — this is essentially an **ingredient supplier with high switching costs**. Once a fragrance or flavour is formulated into a consumer product (say, a specific shampoo variant), the FMCG company rarely changes its F&F supplier for the lifecycle of that product. The formulation is custom, proprietary, and deeply embedded in the end-product's identity. This creates a **toll-bridge-like business** where Sacheerome earns a recurring stream on every unit of the FMCG product sold.

**Buffett Business Classification:** Switching-cost business with elements of a consumer franchise (brand-level stickiness through formulations). Sacheerome is NOT a commodity chemical maker — it sells custom, IP-intensive formulations, not bulk chemicals.

> *Buffett: "I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* Sacheerome's business is straightforward — create proprietary fragrance/flavour formulations that become embedded in clients' products, generating recurring revenue with high retention. The founder-promoter family has 40+ years in this niche, and the business is simple enough to understand in one sitting.

**Key Feature:** Sacheerome claims to have established India's first automated and robotic fragrance manufacturing plant (2015), with precision to 0.002 mg — a genuine quality and consistency moat in an industry where batch-to-batch consistency is critical.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Industry Structure

The Indian F&F industry is estimated at ₹4,500–5,000+ Cr (flavours alone) and growing at 6–7% CAGR. It is **oligopolistic at the top** — global giants Givaudan, Firmenich (now dsm-firmenich), IFF, and Symrise collectively hold ~60% of the Indian market. The largest domestic player is S.H. Kelkar (Keva) with ~10–12% market share domestically (~20% in fragrances). Sacheerome is a mid-tier domestic player with ~₹108 Cr revenue (FY25), growing rapidly but still small relative to the market.

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: Switching Costs + Niche Expertise (Moderate-to-Strong)**

1. **Switching Costs (Strong):** Once a fragrance or flavour is formulated into a consumer product, the FMCG client rarely switches suppliers during the product's lifecycle. The formulation is proprietary, approved through regulatory testing (IFRA, FSSAI, FDA), and the consumer's perception of the brand is tied to that specific scent/taste. Switching costs are real and meaningful.

2. **R&D & Formulation Library (Strong):** Sacheerome maintains a library of 10,000+ formulations — this is decades of accumulated IP. Each formulation represents a proprietary recipe that cannot be easily replicated. The company employs 24 perfumers/flavourists and 10 evaluators — highly specialised personnel.

3. **Manufacturing Precision (Moderate):** India's first robotic/automated fragrance plant with 0.002 mg precision — this consistency is critical for FMCG clients who demand batch-to-batch uniformity. Difficult for smaller competitors to replicate without significant capital investment.

4. **Client Relationships (Strong):** Serving ITC, Wipro, Raymond, Dabur, Emami, Himalaya — established relationships with blue-chip FMCG companies. Revenue concentration risk exists (top 5 clients contribute a significant share), but the stickiness is high.

5. **Brand/Reputation (Moderate):** Third-generation family business with 40+ years in the industry. Well-known within the B2B F&F community. Not a consumer brand, but a respected B2B supplier brand.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Revenue (Approx.) | Moat Strength | Threat Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| S.H. Kelkar (Keva) | ~₹2,000+ Cr | Strong (domestic leader, 20% share) | Manageable — different client segments |
| Givaudan (India) | Large (MNC subsidiary) | Very Strong (global R&D, scale) | Manageable — focuses on premium MNCs |

| Smaller domestic players | Various | Weak | Low — Sacheerome has scale and automation advantage |

### Susceptibility to Change

Buffett: *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change."*

The fragrance and flavour industry is remarkably **resistant to disruption**. The basic economics have not changed in decades — FMCG companies need custom formulations, they need consistency, they need regulatory compliance, and they need trusted suppliers. AI cannot replace a perfumer's nose. E-commerce doesn't disrupt B2B ingredient supply chains. Technology augments but doesn't replace human sensory expertise.

The industry has looked essentially the same for 30+ years: a few large global players, a handful of domestic champions, and a long tail of small formulators. There is no foreseeable technology or business model that threatens to fundamentally change how fragrances and flavours are created, manufactured, and supplied.

**Verdict:** The moat is **moderate-to-strong** and **stable-to-widening**. The switching costs are real, the R&D library is deepening, and the manufacturing capability (robotics, automation) creates a cost and quality advantage that smaller competitors cannot easily match. The main competitive risk is from MNC F&F giants moving downmarket — but the domestic FMCG segment is cost-sensitive and prefers domestic suppliers who understand Indian consumer preferences.

### Valuation Context

At ₹328 and a PE of 30.6x on TTM earnings, Sacheerome is **not cheap** on an absolute basis. However, for a company growing earnings at 50%+ CAGR with a clean balance sheet and 30% ROIC, a PE of 30x is not unreasonable. The PEG ratio is approximately 0.6x (30.6 / 52), which suggests the stock is **attractively priced relative to its growth rate**.

The P/B of 5.4x for a business earning 30% ROE is justified by the Gordon Growth Model:

**Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)**  
Where ROE = 27%, g = ROE × Retention = 27%, CoE = 12%  
Fair P/B = (0.27 - 0.27) / (0.12 - 0.27) — this results in a mathematical anomaly because g > CoE, which indicates the business is compounding faster than the discount rate. In such cases, the theoretical P/B is very high or undefined — essentially, the market is right to pay a significant premium for a business that compounds equity at 27% while investors require only 12%.

Compared to S.H. Kelkar (PE ~30–40x, lower growth), Sacheerome's valuation appears fair. Compared to global F&F players (Givaudan trades at 30–40x PE with single-digit growth), Sacheerome offers dramatically higher growth at a comparable multiple.
