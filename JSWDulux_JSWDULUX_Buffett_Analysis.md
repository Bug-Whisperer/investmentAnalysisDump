## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

JSW Dulux Ltd (formerly Akzo Nobel India Ltd) is India's 4th-largest paints and coatings company, manufacturing and selling decorative paints, industrial coatings, automotive coatings, marine and protective coatings, and powder coatings. Its marquee brand **Dulux** is among the most recognised paint brands globally. Other brands include **International, Sikkens, and Interpon.**

**The Business Model — Simply Put:**
The company buys chemical raw materials (titanium dioxide, resins, solvents, pigments), processes them into paints and coatings, and sells through a dealer/retailer network of ~22,000 outlets across 5,000+ towns in India. Revenue splits roughly 70% Decorative / 30% Industrial (post the Powder Coatings divestment in Sep 2025).

**Would Buffett Understand It?** Absolutely. This is a **consumer franchise business** — people buy paint because of the brand, the colour card, and the dealer recommendation, not because they compared chemical specifications. The product is consumed (repainted every 5–7 years), the brand drives pricing power, and the distribution network creates switching costs. Buffett: *"If you gave me $100 billion and said take away the soft drink leadership of Coca-Cola in the world, I'd give it back to you and say it can't be done."* The same logic applies to paint brands in India — the distribution moat is enormous.

**Critical Corporate Event — JSW Acquisition:**
In December 2025, **JSW Paints (part of the $23 billion JSW Group) acquired a 60.76% stake** in Akzo Nobel India for ₹12,915 Cr from the Dutch parent company (AkzoNobel NV). The company was renamed **JSW Dulux Ltd** effective March 11, 2026. Prior to the acquisition, the company divested its **Powder Coatings business** and **International Research Center (IRC)** back to AkzoNobel NV, generating an exceptional gain of ~₹1,874 Cr in Q2 FY26.

This is a **control transition** — new promoter, new chairman (Parth Jindal), new strategic direction. The analysis must distinguish between the historical track record (under AkzoNobel stewardship) and the forward outlook (under JSW ownership).

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

**The Indian Paints Industry Landscape (2026):**

The Indian paints market is worth ~₹96,000 Cr (USD 11.5 billion, 2025) and growing at ~9% CAGR. It is an **oligopoly** dominated by 4 players controlling ~90% of the organised market:

| Player | Market Share (Decorative) | Revenue (₹ Cr) | ROCE |
|---|---|---|---|
| Asian Paints | ~50% | ~33,900 | ~26% |
| Berger Paints | ~20% | ~11,500 | ~25% |
| Kansai Nerolac | ~15% | ~7,800 | ~13% |
| **JSW Dulux** | **~5% Deco, ~7% Industrial** | **~4,100** | **~42%** |
| Birla Opus (new) | ~6-7% (growing fast) | ~3,000 est. | Negative |
| JSW Paints (standalone) | ~2% | — | Negative |

**Moat Assessment:**

**1. Brand Moat (Strong):** Dulux is a globally recognised paint brand. In India, it commands premium positioning — consumers willingly pay a 10-15% premium for Dulux over local brands. Brand moats in paint are powerful because the purchase decision is high-involvement (your house), driven by dealer recommendation, and the price is a small fraction of the overall project cost.

**2. Distribution Moat (Moderate):** 22,000 retailers across 5,000+ towns is a significant network, but it is substantially smaller than Asian Paints (~1.5 lakh dealers) or Berger (~50,000). Distribution is THE critical moat in Indian paints — building it takes years and enormous investment. JSW Dulux is the smallest of the Big 4 by dealer count.

**3. Switching Costs (Moderate):** Dealers invest in tinting machines provided by paint companies, creating mild switching costs. Consumers have brand preferences but can switch easily.

## 21. REGULATORY RISKS & POLICY HEADWINDS

The paints industry faces **relatively benign regulatory risk.** There is no pricing control, no government ownership, no sin-tax risk. The primary regulatory considerations are:

**1. BIS/VOC Regulations:** Bureau of Indian Standards is tightening VOC (Volatile Organic Compound) limits and lead content norms. This actually STRENGTHENS the moat of organised players like JSW Dulux (who already comply) by raising costs for unorganised players who may need to reformulate.

**2. CCI Investigation Against Asian Paints:** The Competition Commission of India has ordered a probe into Asian Paints for allegedly abusing its dominant position by pressuring dealers into exclusivity. If Asian Paints is found guilty, this could BENEFIT JSW Dulux by opening up dealer access that was previously blocked.

**3. GST Structure:** Paints attract 18% GST (reduced from 28% in 2018). This reduction benefited the organised sector and is unlikely to reverse.

**Buffett's Verdict on Regulatory Risk: (a) Manageable and potentially BENEFICIAL.** Regulatory trends (VOC compliance, CCI action against Asian Paints) actually favour JSW Dulux. No structural or terminal regulatory risk exists.
