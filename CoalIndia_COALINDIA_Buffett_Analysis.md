## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Coal India Limited (CIL) is the **world's largest coal mining company** and a textbook **toll bridge + regulated monopoly** in Buffett's framework. Incorporated in 1975 after nationalisation, CIL is a 'Maharatna' PSU under the Ministry of Coal, Government of India, headquartered in Kolkata.

**The business model is stunningly simple:**
- CIL digs coal out of the ground (310 working mines across 85 mining areas in 8 states)
- It sells coal to power plants (which generate ~55% of India's electricity), steel mills, cement factories, and other industrial consumers
- It controls **>80% of India's domestic coal production** — an effective near-monopoly backed by government ownership (63.13% promoter holding)
- It operates through 10 subsidiaries and employs over 2.2 lakh (220,000+) people

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. This is as simple as it gets — a company that digs rocks out of the ground and sells them to power plants. A 10-year-old could understand it. There is no complex technology, no rapidly changing competitive landscape, no "disruption" narrative to parse.

**What type of business is it in Buffett's taxonomy?**

CIL is a hybrid of a **regulated toll bridge** and a **low-cost commodity producer with monopoly characteristics**:
- **Toll bridge:** Every power plant in India that burns domestic coal must essentially buy from CIL or pay significantly more for imports. Coal flows through CIL's gates.
- **Monopoly:** With 80%+ market share in domestic coal production and government backing, CIL has no meaningful domestic competitor of comparable scale. Private/captive mining is growing but remains a fraction of CIL's output.
- **Commodity:** Coal IS a commodity — but CIL's pricing is partially administered (not fully market-driven), which creates a quasi-regulated utility characteristic.

> *"The key to investing is not assessing how much an industry is going to affect society, or how much it will grow, but rather determining the competitive advantage of any given company and, above all, the durability of that advantage."* — Buffett

**The critical question: Does CIL have a circle of competence advantage?** Yes — it sits on India's coal reserves, has captive mining leases, established rail and road infrastructure for despatch, and a regulatory moat (government ownership + nationalised sector). No competitor can easily replicate 310 mines, 2.2 lakh employees, and decades of geological knowledge.

### Trend Narrative

The income statement tells a compelling story of **cyclical recovery and margin expansion**:

1. **FY2017-FY2018 was the trough** — Revenue stagnated, operating margins collapsed to 11-16% (wage hikes, overburden removal costs, tepid demand). EPS hit a low of ₹11.34.

2. **FY2019 marked the turnaround** — E-auction premiums surged, coal demand recovered, margins snapped back to 25%.

3. **FY2022-FY2024 was the golden period** — Post-COVID power demand boom + global energy crisis pushed e-auction realisations sharply higher. OPM expanded from 23% to 33%. 4. **FY2025 shows slight moderation** — Revenue dipped 1% YoY, net profit fell ~5.5%. This reflects normalisation of e-auction premiums and rising depreciation from heavy capex. **The TTM figures show further deceleration** (Net Profit at ₹29,755 Cr vs ₹35,302 Cr for FY2025).

> *"In the business world, the rearview mirror is always clearer than the windshield."* — Buffett

**The critical insight:** CIL's earnings are **cyclical** — they swing dramatically with coal demand, e-auction premiums, and wage revision cycles. The FY2023-FY2024 peak may NOT be the new normal. However, the structural floor has risen significantly (OPM of 30-33% appears sustainable vs. the old 20-25% range) due to operational improvements and mechanisation.

### Maintenance vs. Growth CapEx Decomposition

For a mining company, **maintenance capex** = the spend needed to maintain current production levels (mine maintenance, equipment replacement, overburden removal to keep existing mines productive). **Growth capex** = new mine openings, capacity expansion, first-mile connectivity, new railways/sidings, solar projects.

**Estimated Split:**
- Maintenance CapEx: ~₹8,000-10,000 Cr (roughly equal to D&A of ₹9,145 Cr in FY2025)
- Growth CapEx: ~₹8,000-10,000 Cr (new mine development, coal evacuation infrastructure, solar)

### Owner Earnings Calculation (Buffett's Preferred Metric)

| Metric | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 | 5Y Avg (FY21-25) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Income | 31,723 | 37,369 | 35,302 | 26,895 |
| Add: D&A | 6,833 | 6,735 | 9,145 | 6,170 |
| Less: Maintenance CapEx (≈D&A) | -6,833 | -6,735 | -9,145 | -6,170 |
| Less: SBC | Negligible | Negligible | Negligible | — |
| **= Owner Earnings** | **~31,723** | **~37,369** | **~35,302** | **~26,895** |

For CIL, Owner Earnings approximately equals Net Income because D&A ≈ Maintenance CapEx. This is a **high-quality earnings stream** — there is no hidden maintenance capex drag.

> *"Owner earnings represent (a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization... minus (c) the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment, etc. that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume."* — Buffett

**The key insight:** CIL's reported net income is a **reliable indicator** of true economic earnings. The company is NOT inflating profits by under-investing in maintenance. In fact, the rising capex (₹18,000 Cr in FY2025) suggests CIL is investing well ABOVE maintenance levels — the excess is genuine growth investment.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Classification: **Wide and Enduring — Government-Backed Monopoly + Cost Advantage**

**Moat Sources:**

| Moat Type | Strength | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Government monopoly / regulatory moat** | Very Strong | CIL controls >80% of domestic coal production. Coal mining was nationalised in 1973. Even with liberalisation of commercial mining (since 2020), CIL's dominance is entrenched by its massive scale, established leases, and government backing. |
| **Cost advantage** | Strong | CIL has the lowest cost of coal mining in the world (open-cast mining in India has structural cost advantages). Competitors (private mines, imports) cannot undercut CIL on a delivered-cost basis for most consumers. |
| **Scale advantage** | Very Strong | 310 mines, 781 MT production (FY2025), rail/road despatch infrastructure built over decades — impossible to replicate at any reasonable cost or timeframe. |
| **Switching costs** | Moderate | Power plants are designed for specific coal grades and are tied to CIL through Fuel Supply Agreements (FSAs). Switching to imports or private coal involves logistics, quality adjustments, and regulatory complexity. |
| **Network effects** | Weak | Not applicable to mining. |

### Competitive Landscape

**Key competitors and threat level:**

| Competitor | Threat Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Singareni Collieries (SCCL)** | Low | State-owned (Telangana), ~65 MT production. Regional player, not a national competitor. |
| **Private/Captive Mines** | Medium (rising) | Commercial mining opened in 2020. Adani, Vedanta, JSW, Tata Power have coal blocks. But total private production still <15% of CIL's output. Growing but slowly. |
| **Coal Imports** | Medium | Indonesian, Australian, South African coal competes at the margin, especially for coastal power plants. But imports are expensive (FX risk, logistics). India imported ~230 MT in FY2024 vs domestic production of ~1,000 MT. |
| **Renewable Energy** | **High (long-term)** | The existential threat. Solar/wind is now cheaper than new coal power on a levelised cost basis. India is adding 20-30 GW of renewable capacity annually. The coal-to-power demand chain will eventually peak and decline. |

### Is the Moat Eroding or Enduring?

**Short-to-medium term (5-10 years): ENDURING.** India's coal demand is projected to rise to ~1.5 billion tonnes by 2030. Coal still supplies ~55% of India's electricity. Renewable capacity additions are supplementing, not replacing, coal for now. CIL's FSA-based supply to power plants is contractually locked in.

**Long-term (15-30 years): SLOWLY ERODING.** The global energy transition is real. India has committed to net-zero by 2070. Coal power will eventually peak (likely 2035-2040) and then decline. CIL is diversifying into solar (197 MW installed, targeting 3 GW by FY2028 and 9.5 GW by FY2030), coal gasification, and critical minerals — but these are tiny relative to the core coal business.

> *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money and even more in the future."* — Buffett

**Buffett's Honest Assessment:** For the NEXT 10-15 years, CIL is in a strong position — India NEEDS coal and CIL is the monopoly supplier. Beyond 2040, the picture gets cloudy. Buffett would want a longer runway of predictability, but at the RIGHT PRICE, the next 10-15 years of cash flows are extremely valuable.

**The business is NOT highly susceptible to change in the near-term.** Coal demand in India is rising, not falling. The infrastructure to replace coal at scale does not yet exist. CIL will look substantially similar in 2035 as it does today. **This is the "absence of change" that Buffett looks for.**
