## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Model — Simple, Understandable, Repeatable

Gillette India Ltd is a subsidiary of Procter & Gamble (75% promoter holding) that manufactures and sells grooming products (razors, blades, shaving gels, creams, aftershave — ~76% of revenue) and oral care products (Oral-B toothbrushes — ~24% of revenue) in India and for export.

**Would Buffett understand this business?** Absolutely. This is a textbook **consumer franchise** — a business that sells low-cost, high-frequency, branded consumables where the consumer has strong brand preference and low price sensitivity. Buffett bought into the global Gillette in 1989 and held it through the P&G merger. He called it one of his favourite businesses.

> *"It's pleasant to go to bed every night knowing that 2.5 billion males in the world will have to shave in the morning."* — Warren Buffett on Gillette (1996 Annual Meeting)

**Business Model Classification:**

- **Razor-and-Blade Model (Toll Bridge):** Sell the razor handle cheaply → lock the customer into repeat purchases of proprietary blade cartridges at high margins. This is a recurring revenue model with immense switching costs built into the product design.
- **Consumer Franchise:** Strong brand recognition (Gillette, Oral-B, Venus, Braun) with decades of consumer trust.
- **Switching-Cost Business:** Once a consumer buys a Gillette razor handle, they are locked into Gillette-compatible cartridges. Switching means buying a new handle AND learning a new shaving system.

The grooming category (~₹3,300 Cr in India) is dominated by Gillette with an estimated ~47% market share in razors, and the company has recorded its **highest-ever market share in Blades & Razors** in FY2024. The oral care business (Oral-B) is a growing but smaller segment.

**Revenue Mix (Approximate):** Grooming ~76% | Oral Care ~24%

### Trend Narrative

**Margins are expanding meaningfully.** The operating margin has risen from 21% in FY2019–2022 to 27–28% in recent periods — a structural step-up. ## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Wide — Brand + Switching Costs + Scale + Distribution**

**1. Brand Moat (Strong)**
Gillette is the most recognised grooming brand globally and in India. The tagline "The Best A Man Can Get" has decades of marketing investment behind it. In India, Gillette has achieved its highest-ever market share in Blades & Razors. The Oral-B brand is similarly dominant in manual toothbrushes.

**2. Switching Costs (Strong)**
The razor-and-blade model creates mechanical switching costs — each razor handle only accepts its proprietary cartridges. Once you buy a Gillette MACH3, you can only use MACH3 cartridges. Switching means abandoning the handle investment and learning a new system.

**3. Cost Advantage / Scale (Moderate-Strong)**
As a P&G subsidiary, Gillette India benefits from global R&D (P&G spends ~$2B annually on R&D), global manufacturing know-how, and massive advertising budgets that no Indian competitor can match. P&G's Grooming division generates ~$6.5-7B globally; Gillette India rides on this platform.

**4. Distribution Network (Strong)**
Gillette reaches ~1.5 million retail outlets across India — from urban supermarkets to rural kirana stores. This distribution is expensive and time-consuming to replicate.

### Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Super-Max | Manageable | Low-cost double-edge blades; declining relevance as India premiumises |
| Bombay Shaving Company | Manageable | D2C; small scale; <5% market share |
| LetsShave (Dorco) | Manageable | Online-first; limited retail presence |
| Zlade/others | Low | Niche online players |
| Philips (Trimmers) | Moderate | Growing threat — electric trimmers could structurally disrupt razors |
| Local barbers | Structural | ~60% of Indian men still use barber services; this is both competition and opportunity |

### Is the Moat Enduring or Eroding?

**The moat is enduring in its current form but faces one long-term structural risk: electric trimmers.** Young urban Indian men are increasingly switching from razors to trimmers for stubble maintenance. Gillette India does sell Braun trimmers, but this is a much smaller and less recurring business than razor cartridges. If the trimmer trend accelerates, it could slowly erode the razor-and-blade model's recurring revenue advantage.

**However, for the next 10-20 years, the business looks remarkably stable:**
- India's male grooming market is under-penetrated
- Premiumisation trend (single-blade → multi-blade) drives revenue and margin mix improvement
- Rural penetration has room to grow
- Oral-B has significant growth runway in India's oral care market

> *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Warren Buffett

Gillette India scores well here — men will shave tomorrow just as they shaved yesterday. The fundamental act of shaving hasn't changed in decades, and Gillette's competitive position has only strengthened.

## 9. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

### Buffett's $1 Test

Over the period FY2019 to FY2024 (6 fiscal years, i.e. earnings generated from July 2018 to June 2024):

- **Total earnings retained:** Net Income over 6 years ≈ ₹1,850 Cr. Dividends paid ≈ ₹1,596 Cr. Retained ≈ ₹254 Cr.
- **Change in market cap (matched period):** From ~₹15,970 Cr (end-FY2018, June 2018, price ~₹4,900) to ~₹21,420 Cr (end-FY2024, June 2024, price ~₹6,570) = ~₹5,450 Cr increase.
- **Market value created per ₹1 retained:** ₹5,450 / ₹254 = **~₹21 per ₹1 retained.**

This is a strong result — well above Buffett's $1:$1 hurdle — but it is somewhat misleading because the company retains very little of its earnings, paying out nearly everything as dividends. The more relevant observation is that the stock appreciated ~34% (₹4,900 to ₹6,570) over this period while the company distributed most of its ₹1,850 Cr in earnings back to shareholders as dividends.

### Capital Allocation Assessment

- **No acquisitions** — the company has made zero acquisitions. P&G grows organically. This is textbook Buffett discipline.
- **No buybacks** — in India, P&G/Gillette has not done buybacks. Given the stock trades at 22x+ book value, buybacks would be capital-destructive anyway.
- **Generous dividends** — the right choice given the limited reinvestment runway within the business.
- **Zero equity dilution** — no share issuances of any kind.
- **Promoter stake stable at 75%** — the maximum permitted under Indian regulations. P&G has meaningful skin in the game.

### Management Candour

As a P&G subsidiary, management communication is professional and systematic but somewhat formulaic. There are no flashy promises or aggressive guidance — consistent with P&G's global culture of conservative, steady execution.

### SBC

Not material / not separately disclosed — this is not a concern for Gillette India.

> *"When management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — Warren Buffett

In Gillette's case, the business economics are so good that management quality is almost secondary. But P&G's professional management is a positive — they are competent, conservative, and aligned (through the 75% ownership) with minority shareholders.
