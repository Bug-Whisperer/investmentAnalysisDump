## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Bank of India (BoI) is a **government-owned universal bank**, nationalised in 1969, headquartered in Mumbai. It is the **sixth-largest nationalised bank** in India with total business (Advances + Deposits) exceeding ₹16 lakh crore. It operates ~5,083 domestic branches, ~5,690 ATMs, and 45+ international offices/branches across major financial centres (Tokyo, Singapore, Hong Kong, London, New York, Paris, DIFC Dubai, GIFT City).

**Business Model:** BoI is a classic **spread-income (NIM-driven) business** supplemented by fee income and treasury gains. The raw material is deposits (especially low-cost CASA deposits), which the bank lends out at a higher rate, earning the spread. It also earns fee income from trade finance, forex operations, and bancassurance/cross-selling.

**Type:** Government-owned Public Sector Bank (PSB). The GoI holds 73.38% — down from 89.10% in FY2020, indicating some dilution over the years through equity raises.

**Would Buffett understand this?** Absolutely — banking is within his circle of competence. He bought Wells Fargo, Bank of America, and owned GEICO. However, Buffett has been wary of PSU banks in most countries because of political interference in lending decisions, directed lending (priority sector), and cyclical NPA blowups. BoI's history vindicates this wariness — it posted **five consecutive years of losses (FY2016-FY2020)** before its turnaround.

> *"When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — Buffett

For banks, bad economics = bad asset quality. BoI's NPA crisis (FY2016-FY2020) destroyed nearly a decade of accumulated profits. The question now is whether the turnaround is structural or merely cyclical.

**Primary Moat Sources:**
- Government ownership (implicit sovereign guarantee — depositor confidence)
- Extensive branch network (5,083+ branches — expensive to replicate)
- International presence (45+ offices — rare among PSBs)
- CASA franchise (though lower than top private banks)

**Weaknesses Buffett would note:**
- Government interference in lending decisions
- PSB culture — slower decision-making, less accountability
- NIM structurally lower than private bank peers (~2.5-2.8% vs 4%+ for HDFC/ICICI)
- History of catastrophic NPA cycles

### Trend Narrative

The turnaround story is undeniable. From five consecutive years of losses (FY2016-FY2020), BoI has staged a remarkable recovery:

1. **NIM is compressing, not expanding.** Global NIM has fallen from ~2.9% in FY2024 to ~2.57% in FY2025 and ~2.51% in 9M FY26. This is the single biggest concern. The compression is driven by rising cost of deposits as CASA share declines and competition for term deposits intensifies. Domestic NIM at 2.76% in 9M FY26 is well below the PSB average of ~3.0%.

2. **Cost-to-income ratio has improved** — from elevated levels during the NPA crisis years to approximately **55-60%** in recent periods (per investor presentations). This reflects genuine operating leverage from digital adoption, branch rationalisation, and stronger income growth outpacing cost growth.

3. **Provision intensity is declining** — the bank was provisioning more than its entire operating profit during the NPA crisis (FY2018-20). Now, with credit costs at just 0.47% (FY2025) vs. peak levels of 3%+, provisions consume a much smaller share of operating profit. This is the primary driver of the profit surge. However, this also means the "easy" earnings growth from lower provisions is largely behind us. Going forward, earnings growth must come from genuine NII growth — which is harder with compressing NIMs.

4. **Significant equity dilution occurred:** Shares outstanding went from 64.3 Cr in FY2014 to 455.3 Cr in FY2025 — a **7x dilution**. This is devastating for per-share compounding. EPS of ₹20.97 in FY2025 is still far below the ₹46.50 earned in FY2014 with a much smaller share base. The dilution funded recapitalisation during the NPA crisis — the government injected capital to keep the bank alive. This is precisely the pattern Buffett avoids.

> *"When you combine ignorance and leverage, you get some pretty interesting results."* — Buffett

### Buffett's Financial Fortress Test — Adapted for Banks

**1. Capital Adequacy: ✅ STRONG**
CRAR at ~17.1% (Sep 2025) is well above the regulatory minimum of 10.875% and above the 15% "strong" threshold. CET-1 at ~14.5% provides a comfortable buffer. The bank is not expected to need external capital in the near term.

**2. Asset Quality: ✅ IMPROVING RAPIDLY, BUT STILL ABOVE AVERAGE**
GNPA has fallen dramatically from 15.84% (FY2020) to 2.54% (Sep 2025) — a remarkable cleanup. NNPA at 0.65% is approaching the 1% comfort zone. PCR at ~94% is excellent. Slippage ratio at 0.64% for 9M FY26 is very encouraging. However, GNPA at 2.54% is still higher than top private banks (HDFC <1.5%, ICICI <2%) and even some PSB peers (Indian Bank ~2.2%).

**3. Funding Stability: ⚠️ AREA OF CONCERN**
CASA ratio has been declining — from ~44% in FY2021-22 to ~40% in H1 FY26. This is a systemic trend (depositors migrating to term deposits for higher rates), but BoI's CASA is also below PSB peer average. The rapid growth in borrowings (from ₹26,821 Cr in FY2022 to ₹123,869 Cr in FY2025) is concerning — it indicates deposits haven't kept pace with loan growth, forcing the bank to rely on costlier wholesale funding.

**4. Hidden Risks: ⚠️ ICRA flags the "vulnerable book" (SMA-1 + SMA-2 + restructured book) remains HIGH relative to core capital.** The headline NPA numbers look good, but potential stress lurking in the SMA accounts is a monitorable.

> *"You only find out who is swimming naked when the tide goes out."* — Buffett

The current credit cycle is benign. The true test of BoI's asset quality will come in the next downturn.

*\*Normalised Credit Cost = 5-year average credit cost (~2.0% of average advances) applied to current advances. This is conservative and reflects the through-cycle provisioning need.*

**Wait — this shows NEGATIVE normalised earnings?** Yes, and this is the critical insight. If you assume BoI needs to provision at its own 5-year average credit cost (which includes the tail end of the NPA crisis), the "true" earning power is still negative. This is because the 5-year average is heavily weighted by the crisis years.

**A more fair normalised credit cost using the last 3 years (FY2023-FY2025 average of ~0.82%)** yields:

| Metric | FY2025 |
|---|---|
| Net Income | 9,552 |
| Add: D&A | 604 |
| Less: Normalised Credit Cost (0.82% of ~₹6,18,500 avg advances) | ~5,072 |
| = **Normalised Earnings** (3-year norm) | **~5,084** |
| **Normalised EPS** | **~₹11.2** |

**Current credit cost (0.47% in FY2025) is WELL BELOW the 3-year average** — this means FY2025 reported earnings are **above normal**. The bank is benefiting from a cyclically benign credit environment. If credit costs revert to even the 3-year average, EPS would fall from ₹21 to ~₹11.

**PCR is rising** (from ~80% to ~94%) — this is conservative and positive. The bank is building reserves.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: NARROW — Government-granted franchise + branch network**

1. **CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits: ⚠️ BELOW AVERAGE**
   - CASA at ~40% is below SBI (~40%), well below HDFC Bank (~38% but with much higher overall franchise quality) and Indian Bank (~40%). BoI's CASA has been trending DOWN, which is the wrong direction.
   - Cost of deposits rising from 3.3% (FY2022) to ~4.9% (H1 FY26) — this directly compresses NIM.

2. **Distribution Network: ✅ ADEQUATE**
   - 5,083+ branches, 5,690+ ATMs, 45+ international offices. This is a genuine moat — building this network from scratch would cost thousands of crores. However, digital banking is gradually eroding the branch moat.

3. **Underwriting Skill: ❌ HISTORICALLY POOR**
   - BoI's GNPA peaked at 25%+ (standalone) during the crisis. This is among the worst in the PSB universe. While it has improved dramatically, the track record through the last cycle (IL&FS, infrastructure NPAs, COVID) was abysmal. One cycle of clean performance does not prove underwriting moat.

4. **Technology & Digital: ⚠️ LAGGING**
   - PSU banks generally lag private peers in technology. BoI has invested in digital, but it does not have a YONO-like platform (SBI) or the tech infrastructure of HDFC/ICICI.

5. **Regulatory Moat: ✅ EXISTS**
   - Banking licences are scarce. RBI has granted very few new universal bank licences. This protects incumbents.

6. **Fintech Disruption Risk: ⚠️ MODERATE**
   - UPI is eating into fee income. Digital lenders are competing for retail unsecured loans. However, the primary business (corporate and MSME lending funded by deposits) is relatively protected from fintech disruption in the near term.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| SBI | Manageable | 800-lb gorilla, but operates in a different league; BoI doesn't directly compete for the same mandates |
| Bank of Baroda | **Direct** | Closest PSB peer by size; better NIM, better ROA, better asset quality |
| Union Bank of India | **Direct** | Similar size post-merger; better cost ratios |
| Indian Bank | **Direct** | Smaller but better ROA (1.2%+), better asset quality |
| Canara Bank | **Direct** | Similar size; comparable metrics |
| HDFC Bank / ICICI Bank | Existential (long-term) | Far superior technology, brand, underwriting — gradually taking market share from all PSBs |

### Is the business susceptible to change?

Buffett likes businesses where the future looks like the past. Banking is structurally stable — people will always need deposits and loans. However, HOW banking is delivered is changing rapidly (digital, UPI, fintech). PSBs face a slow erosion of market share to private banks and technology-driven lenders. This is a multi-decade trend that is unlikely to reverse.

> *"We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Buffett

BoI is a business facing gradual change — not disruptive, but persistent. Private banks' deposit market share has risen from ~20% to ~35%+ over two decades. This trend continues.

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK

### Part A: Unit Economics of Scaling (Bank-Specific)

**1. Deposit Scalability: ⚠️ MIXED**
- Deposits are growing ~11-12% YoY, but CASA ratio is declining. The marginal deposit is increasingly a costlier term deposit.
- Borrowings have exploded from ₹26,821 Cr (FY2022) to ₹123,869 Cr (FY2025) — a 4.6x increase. This suggests deposits are NOT scaling fast enough to fund loan growth.

**2. Loan Book Scalability: ✅ DECENT**
- Advances growing 11-14% YoY with improving asset quality (GNPA declining). This is the gold standard for bank scaling.
- Credit cost declining while loan book grows — excellent signal.
- RAM (Retail, Agri, MSME) at ~59% of advances, up from lower levels — diversification improving.

**3. Operational Scalability: ✅ GOOD**
- Cost-to-income ratio improving steadily from elevated crisis-era levels (~65%+) toward ~55%. Genuine operating leverage visible.
- Digital transactions increasing. Business per employee and per branch likely improving.

**4. Capital Scalability: ⚠️ THE BOTTLENECK**
- Internal capital generation rate = ROE (12%) × Retention (81%) = ~9.7%. This supports ~10% advance growth while maintaining CRAR.
- For 14%+ advance growth (current pace), the bank may need to either slow growth or raise capital again in 3-4 years.
- The bank has NOT raised equity since FY2024 — positive. If ROE improves to 15%+ and sustains, capital scalability improves.

### Part B: S-Curve Position

BoI is in the **Growth/Recovery Phase** — post-turnaround, with accelerating profitability but not yet proven through a credit cycle. It is NOT at an inflection point (that was FY2022-23 when NPAs peaked and started declining). The most explosive re-rating has likely already happened (stock went from ₹25 in FY2020 to ₹170 now — a 7x move).

### Fair P/B Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Where:
  ROE = 12% (sustainable, conservative)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 12% × 80% = 9.6%
  CoE = 12% (India equity risk premium for PSB)

Fair P/B = (12% - 9.6%) / (12% - 9.6%) = 2.4% / 2.4% = 1.0x
```

At sustainable ROE of 12%, the fair P/B is approximately **1.0x book** — which is almost exactly where the stock trades today.

If ROE sustainably improves to 15%:
```
Fair P/B = (15% - 12%) / (12% - 12%) → Undefined (g approaches CoE)
```
Using a more conservative g of 10%: Fair P/B = (15% - 10%) / (12% - 10%) = **2.5x**

**The valuation is EXTREMELY SENSITIVE to sustainable ROE.** At 12% ROE, BoI is fairly valued at 1x book. At 15% ROE (which it is approaching), it deserves 2-2.5x book. The entire investment thesis hinges on whether 15% ROE is sustainable.
