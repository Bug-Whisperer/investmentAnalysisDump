## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Description

Indian Bank, established in 1907, is a **large public sector universal bank** — the seventh-largest PSB in India by deposits and advances. Post the 2020 amalgamation with Allahabad Bank, it became a significantly larger entity. The bank operates through four segments: Retail Banking (~41%), Corporate/Wholesale Banking (~32%), Treasury (~24%), and Others (~2%).

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. Banking is one of Buffett's favourite sectors. He has owned Wells Fargo, Bank of America, US Bancorp, and multiple other banks over decades. The business model is as old as capitalism: take deposits (cheap raw material), lend them out at higher rates (the product), earn the spread (the profit). The economic engine is simple.

> *Buffett: "Banking is a very good business if you don't do anything dumb."*

### Type of Financial Institution

**Universal Bank (Public Sector)** — offers the full spectrum of banking services: savings/current accounts, term deposits, retail loans (home, vehicle, personal, education), MSME lending, agricultural credit, corporate loans, trade finance, treasury operations, and third-party distribution (insurance, mutual funds).

### Core Economic Engine

The engine is **primarily spread income (NIM-driven)**. Indian Bank's domestic NIM stands at **3.4%** as of Q3 FY26 — among the better NIMs in the PSU banking space. The moat sources, in order of importance:

1. **Low-cost deposit franchise (CASA ~39%)** — though this has declined from ~42% in FY22. The bank has a strong South Indian deposit base (Tamil Nadu is its home market) with sticky retail depositors. Cost of deposits, while rising, remains competitive.

2. **Distribution reach** — ~5,965 branches (34% rural, 26% semi-urban, 20% urban, 20% metro), ~5,624 ATMs, and ~16,247 business correspondents. This physical network, especially the rural/semi-urban penetration, creates a funding moat that is expensive and time-consuming to replicate.

3. **Government backing** — GoI ownership at 73.84% provides implicit sovereign guarantee on deposits, making it a trusted "safe" bank for retail depositors. This is a structural moat for deposit-gathering.

4. **Improving underwriting discipline** — GNPA has declined from 8.47% (FY22) to 2.23% (Q3 FY26). This is a dramatic cleanup, though the starting point was terrible.

> *Buffett on banks: "The banking business is no favorite of ours. When assets are twenty times equity — a common ratio in this industry — mistakes that involve only a small portion of assets can destroy a major portion of equity."*

For Indian Bank, assets are ~12x equity (improving from higher leverage). This is moderate leverage for a bank, and the key question is whether asset quality is truly clean or hiding skeletons.

### Trend Narrative

**This is a classic PSU bank turnaround story — and a powerful one.**

1. **NIM has expanded steadily** from ~2.9% to ~3.4%. This is excellent for a PSU bank and comparable to some private banks. The improvement reflects better loan mix (more retail/MSME, less stressed corporate) and repricing of the book.

2. **Cost-to-Income ratio has collapsed** from ~54% (FY18-20) to ~33% (FY25). This is among the best in the Indian banking industry (private bank median is ~40-45%). The bank has achieved massive operating leverage — revenue has grown while operating expenses have actually DECLINED from ₹21,577 Cr (FY23) to ₹19,818 Cr (FY25). This is rare and remarkable.

3. **Provision intensity has fallen dramatically** — from 87% of PPOP (FY19, the NPA crisis peak) to ~52% (FY25). This means more of operating profit is flowing to the bottom line. The question is: is this sustainable, or is the bank in a cyclically benign credit environment?

4. **Earnings growth is GENUINE** — driven by NIM expansion + operating leverage + lower provisions. This is the trifecta. But history warns that PSU banks have shown this pattern before (2003-2007), only to see NPAs explode later.

5. **Dilution has been significant.** Shares outstanding went from 48 Cr (FY18) to 134.7 Cr (FY25) — nearly 3x dilution, largely from the Allahabad Bank merger (FY21) and QIPs. Despite this, EPS has grown from ₹27.29 to ₹83.61 — overwhelming the dilution.

> *Buffett: "In the banking industry, the weights swing with exceptional force. When trouble comes, it's not sticks and stones that are thrown but cannonballs."* Indian Bank took those cannonballs during FY18-20 (NPA crisis). The question is whether the arsenal is now empty or reloading.

### Susceptibility to Change

Banking in India is NOT going to look fundamentally different in 10-20 years. People will still need savings accounts, home loans, and working capital credit. The delivery mechanism may shift (more digital, fewer branches), but the core business of deposit-taking and lending endures.

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* Banking passes this test. The product itself hasn't changed in centuries. The risk for Indian Bank is not obsolescence — it's competitive erosion as private banks and fintechs capture a growing share of the profitable segments (affluent retail, fee income), leaving PSBs with the less profitable (rural, agriculture, government scheme lending).

**Honest Moat Verdict:** The moat is **moderate and conditionally improving.** If Indian Bank can sustain its NIM, hold credit quality, and improve digital capabilities, it has a viable franchise. The moat is wide enough to make money — but not wide enough to prevent gradual share loss in high-value segments.

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK [BANK/NBFC MODE]

### Part A: Bank-Specific Scalability

- Deposit growth is broadly keeping pace with advance growth — no dangerous mismatch.
- **Verdict: Moderate.** The bank can grow deposits but the mix is deteriorating.

**2. Loan Book Scalability**
- Advances growing at 11-13% CAGR. Loan mix shifting towards retail (23%) and MSME (18%).
- Credit cost has DECLINED while the loan book grows — the gold standard. GNPA falling from 8.47% to 2.23% while book grew ~50%.
- Diversification: Agriculture 25%, Retail 23%, MSME 18%, Corporate 34% — reasonably diversified.
- **Verdict: Good.** The bank is scaling the book while improving quality.

**3. Operational Scalability**
- Cost-to-Income ratio collapsed from 54% to 33% — extraordinary operating leverage.
- Branch count stable (~5,965). The bank is extracting more revenue per branch without adding new ones.
- Employee count at 40,515 — business per employee is rising significantly.
- **Verdict: Excellent.** Best-in-class operating leverage.

**4. Capital Scalability**
- Internal capital generation rate: ROE 17% × Retention 81% = **~14%**
- Asset growth target: 10-12% → internal capital generation is SUFFICIENT
- CRAR at 16.6% provides buffer for higher growth
- No equity dilution needed for the next 3-5 years at current growth rates
- **Verdict: Good.** Self-funding growth without dilution.

### Fair P/B Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)
Where:
  ROE = 16% (sustainable, slightly below current 17% for conservatism)
  g = ROE × Retention = 16% × 81% = ~13%
  CoE = 12% (PSU bank risk premium above risk-free)

Fair P/B = (0.16 - 0.13) / (0.12 - 0.13) = 0.03 / (-0.01) → undefined (g > CoE!)
```

**When growth exceeds the cost of equity, the fair P/B is technically infinite** — the model breaks down because the bank is creating value faster than the discount rate. This happens when a bank compounds BV at a rate above the investor's required return.

Let's use a more practical approach with a **capped growth rate** (say g = 10%, accounting for eventual mean reversion of ROE):

```
Fair P/B = (0.16 - 0.10) / (0.12 - 0.10) = 0.06 / 0.02 = 3.0x
```

At a sustainable ROE of 14% (even more conservative):
```
Fair P/B = (0.14 - 0.10) / (0.12 - 0.10) = 0.04 / 0.02 = 2.0x
```

### Fair P/B Range: 2.0x - 3.0x (assuming sustainable ROE of 14-16%)

**Current P/B of 1.47x is BELOW the fair value range.** The market is pricing Indian Bank as a "Good" bank (1.5-2.5x P/B) when the financial trajectory suggests it is approaching "Excellent" status.
