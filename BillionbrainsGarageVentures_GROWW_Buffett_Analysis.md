## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Groww (operated by Billionbrains Garage Ventures Ltd) is India's largest digital investment platform by active users on the NSE. Founded in 2017 and launched with mutual fund distribution, it expanded into stock broking, derivatives, bonds, ETFs, IPOs, commodities, margin trading, and personal loans. It serves customers across 98% of Indian pin codes through a mobile-first, direct-to-consumer model.

**Would Buffett understand this business?** Yes — but with caveats. At its core, Groww is a **toll bridge on India's financialisation wave**. Every time a retail investor buys a stock, mutual fund, or derivative on Groww, the company earns a fee. The economics are similar to a payments network (Visa/Mastercard) layered on top of India's capital markets plumbing. The business is simple: attract users → make investing easy → clip a toll on every transaction → cross-sell more products to the same customer base.

**Buffett:** *"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* Groww's business model has the virtue of simplicity — it intermediates between retail investors and financial markets through technology. However, it operates in a **highly regulated and competitive** industry where SEBI rules can change the economics overnight (as they did with F&O lot sizes and STT hikes). This is NOT a consumer franchise like Coca-Cola where the product is immune to regulatory disruption.

**The business model is best described as a switching-cost + network-effect platform.** Once users have their demat account, trading history, and portfolio on Groww, switching is friction-laden. As more users join, the platform's data advantage and product economics improve. Revenue streams include brokerage fees (53% from equity derivatives), float income on client balances, margin trading facility (MTF) interest, mutual fund distribution commissions, and personal loan origination fees.

**Maintenance CapEx is negligible.** The platform's "maintenance cost" is engineering salaries, cloud hosting, and regulatory compliance — all of which flow through operating expenses, not CapEx. ## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: Switching Costs + Network Effects + Scale Advantage

Groww has built a meaningful moat through multiple reinforcing layers:

**1. Switching Costs (Moderate-to-Strong):** Once a user has a demat account, trade history, and portfolio on Groww, switching involves transferring the demat account (cumbersome process), losing portfolio tracking continuity, and re-learning a new interface. For mutual fund SIP investors (Groww's largest user segment by count), inertia is extremely powerful.

**2. Scale Advantage (Strong):** With 37 million demat accounts, 20.4 million transacting users, and #1 market share on NSE (26.8% of active clients), Groww has the lowest customer acquisition cost among digital brokers. Marketing spend per acquired user declines as brand recognition grows — CAC fell 33% QoQ to ~₹900 in Q3 FY26.

**3. Data & Cross-Sell Network (Growing):** Every user interaction generates data that improves product recommendations, risk assessment (for lending), and platform experience. The platform can sell stocks, mutual funds, derivatives, bonds, MTF, personal loans, and AMC products to the same user at near-zero incremental distribution cost.

### Is the Moat Enduring or Eroding?

The moat is **genuine but faces real threats:**

**Key Competitors:**
- **Zerodha** — The original disruptor and #2 by active users. Privately held, profitable, conservative growth approach. Manageable threat — losing market share to Groww.
- **Angel One** — Public, aggressive marketing, expanding product suite. Competent competitor.
- **ICICI Direct / HDFC Securities / Kotak Securities** — Bank-backed brokers with captive customer bases. Slow to innovate but have deep pockets and trust.
- **Dhan, Fyers, 5paisa** — Smaller digital platforms nibbling at the edges.
- **UPI/Fintech Giants (PhonePe, Paytm, Google Pay)** — Have massive user bases and could enter broking/investing more aggressively. This is the **existential-level threat**.

**Is the business susceptible to change?** This is the critical question. **Buffett explicitly avoids businesses facing rapid change.** The broking industry IS subject to change — SEBI regulations (STT hikes, F&O lot size changes in late 2024, potential new rules in 2026), technology disruption (AI-driven investing), and competitive dynamics shift frequently.

**Buffett:** *"We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* Groww fails this specific test. The regulatory environment for Indian brokers changes materially every 1-2 years, and 53% of Groww's revenue comes from equity derivatives — a segment that SEBI has explicitly targeted for retail protection measures. This is a yellow flag for a Buffett-style investor.

**Founder-Led Management:** Groww is led by co-founders Lalit Keshre (CEO), Harsh Jain, Neeraj Singh, and Ishan Bansal — all former Flipkart employees. The tech-first DNA is evident in the product's simplicity and rapid feature rollout.

**Buffett's $1 Test:**
- Retained Earnings (FY23-FY25): ₹458 + (−₹805) + ₹1,824 = **₹1,477 Cr**
- Book Value Increase (FY23 → FY25): ₹4,812 − ₹3,273 = **₹1,539 Cr**
- **₹1.04 of book value created per ₹1 retained** — Pass (marginally above $1)

Note: The traditional $1 test using market cap change is not applicable because the company was unlisted for most of this period. The IPO in November 2025 precludes a meaningful market-cap-based comparison.

**Capital Allocation:**
- No acquisitions to evaluate (organic growth only, which is positive).
- IPO was primarily an OFS (₹5,572 Cr) — existing investors liquidating, not the company raising growth capital. Only ₹1,060 Cr was fresh issue.
- No dividends or buybacks (appropriate for a high-growth company).
- NBFC subsidiary lending book of ~₹1,390 Cr — measured approach to lending; not reckless.

**Insider Ownership:** Promoter holding at 27.81% as of Dec 2025. The four co-founders collectively hold a meaningful stake. However, the large "Public" holding at 64.13% includes pre-IPO investors (Tiger Global, Sequoia, Ribbit Capital, etc.) who may sell as lock-ins expire.

**Buffett:** *"In looking for people to hire, look for three qualities: integrity, intelligence, and energy. And if they don't have the first, the other two will kill you."* Management has demonstrated intelligence and energy (building India's #1 broking platform from zero in 6 years). Integrity appears sound — the company turned profitable on a GAAP basis before IPO (unlike many startup IPOs), CARE assigned AA; Stable rating, and there are no red flags in regulatory dealings.
