## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Bank of Baroda is India's **second-largest public sector bank** and **fourth-largest bank overall** by assets, with consolidated assets of ~₹18.6 lakh crore (FY2025). It was founded in 1908 by Maharaja Sayajirao Gaekwad III and nationalised in 1969. In April 2019, Dena Bank and Vijaya Bank were merged into BoB — making it a substantially larger entity.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. Banking is one of the simplest business models: borrow money cheaply (deposits), lend it at higher rates (advances), and keep the spread (NIM). The trick is not losing money on bad loans.

> *Buffett: "Banking is a very good business if you don't do anything dumb."*

**Type of Financial Institution:** Universal Public Sector Bank — full-service bank with retail, corporate, SME, agriculture, international, and treasury operations. The Government of India holds 63.97%.

**The Economic Engine:** Primarily NIM-driven (spread income), supplemented by fee income (commissions, cross-sell of insurance/mutual funds) and treasury gains. The core moat rests on:

1. **Cheap funding (deposits/CASA)** — CASA ratio ~38% (domestic ~39.3%), competitive cost of deposits at ~4.75% (Q3 FY26)
2. **Distribution reach** — 8,400+ domestic branches, 84 overseas offices in 17 countries
3. **Sovereign backing** — GoI ownership provides implicit guarantee, ensuring deposit stability
4. **Scale** — 6% market share in Indian banking by assets

**Critical context:** For banks, deposits (debt) are the **raw material** of the business, not a financing choice. This fundamentally changes how every financial metric must be interpreted.

> *Buffett: "The most important thing in evaluating a bank is the quality of its loans. The second most important thing is the cost of its deposits."*

### Trend Narrative

The transformation is dramatic. Bank of Baroda went from near-zero profitability in FY2020-21 (post-merger integration + COVID + massive NPA provisioning) to ₹20,865 Cr net profit in FY2025. This is a textbook PSU bank turnaround.

**Key observations:**

- **NIM is compressing:** From a peak of 3.31% in FY23 to 3.02% in FY25 and ~2.79% in Q3 FY26. This is driven by rising deposit costs (time deposits repricing upward) while lending yields face competitive pressure. NIM compression is the biggest near-term risk.
- **Cost-to-Income ratio is stable** at ~48%, which is decent for a large PSU bank. Not best-in-class (SBI ~52%, HDFC Bank ~40%) but respectable.
- **Provision intensity collapsed** from 109% of PPOP in FY2020 to ~15% in FY2025. This is the primary driver of the profit explosion. The question is whether this is a permanent improvement or a cyclical tailwind.
- **Earnings growth is decelerating:** From 390% in FY22 to 10.6% in FY25 and likely single-digits in FY26. The "easy" earnings gains from provision normalisation are behind us. Future growth must come from operating improvement.

> *Buffett: "In evaluating banks, you must judge management by what they've done with the money they've retained. The real test is long-term ROE."*

### Buffett's Financial Fortress — Bank Adaptation

**1. Capital Adequacy — PASS ✅**
CRAR of 17.19% (FY25) is well above the regulatory minimum of 11.5%. CET-1 at 13.78% provides substantial buffer. Even after declining to 15.29% in Q3 FY26 (due to growth), this remains comfortable. The bank has board approval for a ₹8,500 Cr QIP if needed.

**2. Asset Quality — Dramatic Improvement ✅ (with caution)**
GNPA has collapsed from **9.40% in FY20 to 2.04% in Q3 FY26** — a stunning transformation. NNPA at 0.57% is near-pristine. Slippage ratio at 0.78% (FY25) is below 1% — indicating tight underwriting. This is the best asset quality in BoB's post-merger history.

However, GNPA >2% still isn't best-in-class (HDFC Bank ~1.2%, ICICI ~2.0%). The improvement came partly from massive write-offs (₹8,989 Cr in FY25 alone) — these mask the absolute quantum of bad loans.

**3. Funding Stability — ADEQUATE ⚠️**
CASA ratio has **declined** from ~44% (FY21-22) to ~38.5% (Q3 FY26). This is a concern. The bank is increasingly relying on costlier time deposits and bulk deposits to fund growth. Cost of deposits rose from 3.5% to 4.85% over three years. Deposit growth (10%) trails advance growth (13-15%), creating a credit-deposit ratio of ~85%, which is elevated.

**4. Hidden Risks — MONITORING ⚠️**
Restructured book has shrunk significantly from ~2.5% of advances (FY22-23) to under 1%. SMA-1 and SMA-2 accounts need monitoring. The Q1 FY26 slippage ratio spiked temporarily due to one international account — management expects resolution. Overall, the vulnerable book is shrinking.

> *Buffett: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact." For PSU banks, bad economics = bad asset quality. BoB has demonstrably improved, but one credit cycle can destroy a decade of profits.*

> *Buffett: "A great bank earns high ROE primarily through high ROA, not excessive leverage." BoB earns its ROE the right way — through decent spreads and low credit costs — but it's not "great." It's "good." And "good" at a bank means 1.0-1.5x book, not 3x book.*

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits — ADEQUATE ⚠️**
CASA ratio at ~38.5% (domestic ~39.3%) is **below the PSU bank average** and well below private bank leaders (HDFC Bank ~39%, Kotak ~52%). CASA ratio has been **declining** — this is a structural concern. The cost advantage exists vs. wholesale-funded NBFCs but is narrowing vs. private banks.

**2. Distribution & Branch Network — STRONG ✅**
8,400+ domestic branches and 84 overseas offices. This is expensive to replicate. Rural and semi-urban presence gives access to low-cost deposits and priority sector lending.

**3. Underwriting Skill — IMPROVING ✅**
Through-cycle performance was TERRIBLE (GNPA peaked at 9.4% in FY20 post-merger). But post-cleanup, the bank has demonstrated much better discipline. Slippage ratio of 0.78% (FY25) is excellent. The question is whether this holds through the next downturn.

**4. Sovereign Backing — DURABLE MOAT ✅**
GoI ownership at 63.97% provides implicit deposit guarantee. This will NEVER go away (unless privatised — which is politically unlikely in the medium term). This is the #1 moat for PSU banks.

**5. Technology & Digital — IMPROVING ⚠️**
The BOB World app and digital initiatives are progressing, but BoB lags significantly behind private banks (HDFC, ICICI, Kotak) in digital experience and fintech integration.

**6. Fintech Disruption Risk — MODERATE ⚠️**
UPI has commoditised payments (fee erosion). Digital lenders (Bajaj Finance, fintechs) are eroding unsecured lending margins. However, BoB's secured lending focus (home loans, auto, gold) provides some insulation.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| SBI | Manageable | Fellow PSU; largest bank; competes on scale. BoB is the #2 PSU. |
| HDFC Bank | Significant | Best-in-class private bank; superior technology, CASA, ROA |
| ICICI Bank | Significant | Aggressive retail push; strong digital platform |
| PNB | Manageable | Fellow PSU; weaker asset quality |
| Kotak Mahindra | Moderate | Premium positioning; high CASA; smaller scale |

### Is the Business Susceptible to Change?

Banking in India faces significant technological disruption (UPI, digital lending, account aggregators, CBDC experiments). However, the fundamental model — gather deposits, lend them out — has persisted for centuries.

> *Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."*

Banking IS relatively resistant to change in its core function. The channels change (branch → mobile), but the spread business endures. BoB's sovereign backing and massive branch network provide **structural permanence** even as the competitive landscape shifts. This is not a business that will be disrupted out of existence in 10-20 years.

**Moat Verdict: MODERATE — Narrow but durable.** The moat exists (sovereign backing, distribution, scale) but is not wide (CASA declining, technology lagging, competitive pressure on NIMs). This is a "good" bank, not a "great" one.

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK — Can This Business Scale?

### Part A: The Unit Economics of Scaling

**Deposit Scalability:** CASA growth (~8% YoY) is lagging overall deposit growth (~10%), meaning the bank is scaling its deposit base but the QUALITY of deposits is deteriorating (more expensive time deposits). This is anti-scaling on the cost front.

**Loan Book Scalability:** Advances growing 13-15% with GNPA declining — this is the gold standard. The bank IS scaling its loan book while improving asset quality. Credit cost remains stable at ~0.47%.

**Operational Scalability:** Cost-to-income ratio has been stubbornly flat at ~48% — limited operating leverage. Digital adoption should help, but BoB hasn't demonstrated meaningful cost improvement from technology investments yet.

**Capital Scalability:** Internal capital generation of ~12.6% supports 11-13% growth. For growth above 15%, the bank would need the approved QIP (dilution). This is a constraint.

### Part B: Scaling Runway

**TAM:** Indian banking credit is ~₹170 lakh crore and growing at 12-14% annually. India's credit-to-GDP ratio (~55%) is well below global averages (~130-150%). The runway is enormous — easily 3-4x from here over 15-20 years.

**BoB's position:** ~6% market share. With Indian banking growing at 12%+ and BoB growing in line, there is massive runway.

**S-Curve Position:** BoB is in the **Growth Phase** — large and growing, model proven, but decelerating. It's not at an inflection point (that was FY22-23 when the NPA cleanup was completed). Returns from here depend on execution and reinvestment quality.

### Part C: Scaling Verdict

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue scalability | 6 | NIM compression offsets volume growth |
| Profit scalability | 5 | Incremental margins flat; provision tailwind fading |
| Capital scalability | 7 | Self-funding at current growth rates; no dilution since FY21 |
| Scaling runway (TAM) | 8 | India's credit-to-GDP ratio has vast room |
| S-Curve position | 6 | Growth phase but decelerating; not at inflection |
| **Overall Scalability Score** | **6/10** | **Moderate Scaler** |

**Classification: Moderate Scaler** — Kraft Heinz analogy. Can grow but requires proportional capital; limited operating leverage. The bank will grow with Indian GDP + credit growth, but won't dramatically improve its unit economics. This is NOT a Coca-Cola (1988) or Apple (2016) situation.

## 9. MANAGEMENT QUALITY & BUFFETT'S $1 TEST

### Buffett's $1 Test

From FY2020 to FY2025:
- Retained earnings: ~₹37,690 Cr cumulative
- Market cap change: From ~₹22,000 Cr (FY20 price ~₹48) to ~₹1,45,000 Cr = +₹1,23,000 Cr
- **For every ₹1 retained, ~₹3.26 of market value was created.** This is an excellent result — but it includes the massive re-rating from distressed (0.3x book) to normalised (0.9x book) valuations. The re-rating won't repeat.

### Capital Allocation

- **No equity dilution since FY21** — Excellent discipline
- **Dividend payout ~21%** — Reasonable; retaining ~79% to compound book value
- **Green Infrastructure Bonds** (₹10,000 Cr at 7.10%) — Innovative funding
- **No major acquisitions** — Sensible after the complex 3-way merger of 2019

### Management Assessment

MD & CEO Debadatta Chand has guided the bank through continued improvement. NPA management has been excellent. However, the NIM compression from 3.31% to 2.79% highlights vulnerability to rate cycles that management cannot fully control.

**PSU management caveat:** BoB's management is subject to government appointment cycles. CEOs rotate every 2-3 years. This structural issue limits long-term strategic consistency. Buffett would find this deeply uncomfortable.

> *Buffett: "We look for three things: intelligence, energy, and integrity. If they don't have the last one, the first two will kill you."*

PSU banks generally pass the integrity test (government oversight) but fail the "permanent, incentivised management" test that Buffett prefers.

### Fair P/B Multiple Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Where:
  ROE = 14% (sustainable, conservative estimate)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 14% × 79% ≈ 11%
  CoE = 12% (appropriate for a PSU bank)

Fair P/B = (14% - 11%) / (12% - 11%) = 3.0% / 1.0% = 3.0x
```

**Wait — this seems too high.** The Gordon Growth Model assumes perpetual growth at 11%, which is unrealistic long-term. Let's use a more conservative long-term growth rate:

```
Adjusted: g = 8% (long-term nominal GDP growth proxy)
Fair P/B = (14% - 8%) / (12% - 8%) = 6% / 4% = 1.5x
```

**Fair P/B: 1.2x - 1.5x** (range based on assumed sustainable ROE of 12-15%)

At the current P/B of **0.91x**, the stock is trading at a **30-40% discount** to its fair P/B multiple.
