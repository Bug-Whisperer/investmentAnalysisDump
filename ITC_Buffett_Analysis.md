## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Circle of Competence

> *"Never invest in a business you cannot understand."* — Warren Buffett

ITC Ltd, established in 1910, is India's largest cigarette manufacturer with an **80% market share** in the organized domestic cigarette market. But ITC is far more than a cigarette company — it is a diversified conglomerate operating across four major segments:

1. **FMCG — Cigarettes (42% of revenue, ~78% of PBIT):** Brands include Insignia, India Kings, Classic, Gold Flake, Navy Cut, Players, and Scissors. This is the profit engine — a near-monopoly with extraordinary pricing power.
2. **FMCG — Others (~27% of revenue):** Aashirvaad, Sunfeast, Bingo!, Yippee!, Classmate, Savlon, Fiama — a growing portfolio of brands built over 20+ years, now approaching profitability inflection.
3. **Paperboards, Paper & Packaging (~14% of revenue):** India's largest manufacturer, integrated from forestry to finished boards.
4. **Agri Business (~17% of revenue):** One of India's largest exporters of agricultural commodities.

**Additionally:** ITC recently demerged its **Hotels business** (ITC Hotels Ltd) — an event reflected in the FY25 financials as a massive one-time other income of ~₹15,391 Cr in Q4 FY25.

**Buffett's Lens:**
This is a business Buffett would instantly recognize — a dominant consumer franchise with pricing power, massive distribution, and brand loyalty that transcends generations. The cigarette business, in particular, resembles the Coca-Cola and See's Candies model: minimal capex, enormous cash generation, and an addictive product with few substitutes.

> *"The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. And if you have to have a prayer session before raising the price by a tenth of a cent, then you've got a terrible business."* — Warren Buffett

ITC's cigarette business has repeatedly demonstrated this: absorbing tax hikes through selective price increases while retaining customers, thanks to brand loyalty and an 80% market share. However — and this is critical — **the government's ability to impose punitive taxes is the single biggest risk to this pricing power**. More on this in our dedicated regulatory risk section.

## 8. THE REGULATORY RISK — Cigarette Taxes, Duties & Policy Headwinds

> *"The most important thing to do if you find yourself in a hole is to stop digging."* — Warren Buffett

### The Core Issue:

The Indian government has historically used **cigarette taxation as a revenue tool and a public health instrument**. The Union Budget for FY2026-27 (announced January 2026) raised the **National Calamity Contingent Duty (NCCD) on cigarettes**, effective 1 February 2026. This is in addition to the already punitive GST regime (28% GST + varying Compensation Cess of up to 290%).

**Cigarettes are the most heavily taxed consumer product in India.** Taxes constitute approximately **50-55% of the retail price** of a cigarette, compared to just 20-30% in most other countries.

### Historical Tax Impact on ITC:

| Event | Year | Impact on ITC |
|---|---|---|
| Steep excise duty hike | FY14-FY16 | Cigarette volumes declined ~10-12% cumulatively; revenue growth stalled |
| GST implementation (28% + cess) | FY18 | Mild disruption, volumes recovered within 2 quarters |
| NCCD hike (~13% weighted avg.) | FY21 (COVID year) | Volumes fell sharply, OPM held at 35% due to price hikes |
| Stable tax regime | FY22-FY24 | **Volume recovery + price hikes = golden period; operating profit surged to ₹25,000+ Cr** |
| NCCD hike (Feb 2026) | FY26/FY27 | **Current headwind — market fears volume decline of 3-7%** |

### Why This Matters — The Demand Curve:

| Tax Hike Magnitude | Likely Volume Impact | ITC's Response | Net Revenue Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mild (5-8%)** | -1% to -3% volume | Price hike to fully absorb | Neutral to mildly positive |
| **Moderate (10-15%)** | -3% to -5% volume | Partial price hike + mix improvement | Flat to mildly negative short-term |
| **Severe (>20%)** | -5% to -10% volume | Price hike + downtrading to lower-tier brands | **Negative for 1-2 years, then recovery** |

### The Feb 2026 Hike — Analysis:

The NCCD hike effective 1 February 2026 appears to be in the **moderate range**. Based on historical precedent:

1. **Volume Impact:** Industry volumes may decline 3-5% over the next 2-3 quarters as the price hike gets passed through.
2. **Revenue Impact:** ITC will raise prices selectively. Net revenue impact should be neutral-to-positive within 2-3 quarters as price increases offset volume declines.
3. **Margin Impact:** Short-term OPM compression of 100-200 bps is possible, followed by recovery as volumes normalize.
4. **Shift to Illicit Market:** The biggest long-term risk. Every tax hike pushes some smokers toward untaxed bidis, chewing tobacco, or illicit cigarettes, which already account for ~75% of India's tobacco consumption. ITC's legal market shrinks, but its share of the legal market actually increases.

> *"It's only when the tide goes out that you learn who's been swimming naked."* — Warren Buffett

Here's the paradox: **every tax hike on legal cigarettes actually strengthens ITC's competitive moat** because:
- No new player can enter (licensing freeze)
- Smaller players lack the scale to absorb tax increases
- ITC's pricing power ensures it can pass through costs
- The illicit market grows, but ITC retains dominance in the organized segment

### What Would Buffett Make of This?

Buffett would view the regulatory risk through three lenses:

**1. History as a Guide:** ITC has survived and thrived through dozens of tax hikes over 115 years. The cigarette business generated ₹25,000+ Cr EBIT in FY23-24 after absorbing every single tax hike thrown at it. The business has **proven its resilience repeatedly**.

**2. The Elasticity Argument:** Cigarettes have low price elasticity of demand. A 10% price hike typically results in only a 3-4% volume decline. The net revenue effect is positive. This is unlike most consumer goods where a 10% price hike could destroy demand.

> *"I'd rather own a company that has a product that people are going to buy regardless of whether the economy is doing well or not."* — Warren Buffett

Smokers are creatures of habit. They don't quit because of a ₹5 price increase — they might trade down from Gold Flake to Navy Cut, but they stay within ITC's portfolio.

**3. The Terminal Risk:** The only scenario that would terrify Buffett is a **total ban on cigarettes** — which is politically and economically infeasible. The Indian government collects ₹50,000-60,000 Cr annually from tobacco taxation. Banning cigarettes would create a massive revenue hole, spike illicit consumption, and eliminate millions of agriculture and retail jobs. The government needs ITC as much as ITC needs smokers.

### Risk Rating:

| Risk Factor | Probability | Impact | Net Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Moderate tax hike (current scenario) | **High** | **Low-Medium** | **Manageable** |
| Severe, punitive tax hike (>25%) | Medium | Medium-High | Concerning but temporary |
| Advertising/packaging restrictions | **High** | **Low** | Negligible (already severe) |
| Total cigarette ban | **Very Low** | **Catastrophic** | Negligible probability |
| Shift to ESG-only investing | Medium | Medium | **Structural FII outflow** (already happening) |
| Volume decline due to health awareness | **Medium-High** | **Low-Medium** | **Gradual, offset by pricing power** |

**Buffett's Verdict on Regulatory Risk:** Manageable. The business has survived 115 years of hostile regulation and emerged stronger each time. The moat actually widens with every tax hike. The stock price dip creates an opportunity, not a permanent impairment.

**The pattern is crystal clear:**
1. Government hikes taxes → volumes decline 5-15% over 1-2 years
2. ITC raises prices → revenue stabilizes within 1-2 years
3. Volumes gradually recover as consumers adjust to new price points
4. ITC's earnings power is higher than before the tax hike

**Every single tax hike cycle has ended with ITC earning more than before.** This is the power of pricing in an oligopoly with inelastic demand.

### Why the Market Overreacts Every Time:

> *"Opportunities come infrequently. When it rains gold, put out the bucket, not the thimble."* — Warren Buffett

The market prices in the **worst-case volume scenario** during tax hike announcements but ignores:
- ITC's ability to raise prices (which more than offsets volume loss)
- The recovery of volumes within 1-2 years
- The elimination of marginal competition (smaller players exit)
- The strengthening of ITC's monopoly position

The Feb 2026 hike has pushed the stock from ₹440 to ₹318 — a **28% decline**. Based on historical precedent, **the actual earnings impact will be less than 5%**, while the stock has already priced in a **28% permanent impairment**. This asymmetry is where Buffett makes money.
