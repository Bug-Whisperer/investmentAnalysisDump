## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

S H Kelkar & Company (marketed under the brand **KEVA**) is India's largest domestic manufacturer of fragrances and flavours. Founded in 1922, the company creates fragrance compounds used in soaps, detergents, personal care, fine fragrances, and home care products, as well as flavour compounds for food, dairy, beverages, and pharmaceuticals. It also manufactures aroma ingredients and novel patented molecules.

**Buffett Classification: Niche Specialty Ingredients Supplier — A "Toll Bridge" on FMCG**

The business sits at a critical point in the FMCG value chain. Every bar of soap, every bottle of shampoo, every packet of detergent needs a fragrance. The fragrance cost is typically 2-5% of the end product's cost, but it is 80-90% of the consumer's purchasing decision for many personal care products — you buy soap because of how it smells. This creates a "low cost, high criticality" dynamic that Buffett loves.

> *Buffett: "Our approach is very much profiting from lack of change rather than from change. With Wrigley chewing gum, it's the lack of change that appeals to me."*

Fragrances and flavours are a stable, non-cyclical business tied to daily consumption of personal care and household products. People don't stop using soap in a recession. The Indian F&F market grows at roughly 8-10% annually, driven by rising per capita FMCG consumption, premiumisation, and growing hygiene awareness.

**Key Business Characteristics:**
- **~20% domestic fragrance market share** — the only listed Indian F&F player of scale
- **8,000+ fragrance formulations** — deep product library built over 100 years
- Supplies to both Indian FMCG companies (regional players, mid-tier brands) and increasingly MNCs
- International presence via PFW Aroma (Netherlands, acquired 2011), Keva Europe, and new US market entry
- **Flavours segment** growing rapidly (37.9% YoY in 9M FY26) from a smaller base
- **Aroma ingredients** — vertically integrated into upstream molecules; some patented
- Recently entered the US market — a potentially significant expansion opportunity

### Trend Narrative

Revenue growth has been consistent at 10%+ on a trailing basis — not explosive but respectable for a specialty chemicals business. The company successfully doubled revenue from ~₹1,000 Cr (FY18) to ~₹2,100 Cr (FY25) in seven years.

However, the critical problem is visible: **earnings have NOT kept pace with revenue growth.** FY25 EPS of ₹5.29 is barely higher than FY16's ₹5.05 — a decade of almost zero EPS growth despite revenue more than doubling. EBITDA margins have swung violently between 13-18%, and net margins are highly erratic (3-11%).

The culprits are clear:
1. **Rising interest costs** — from ₹5-6 Cr (FY17-18) to ₹49 Cr (FY25), a 10x increase reflecting ballooning debt
2. **Rising depreciation** — from ₹30 Cr to ₹95 Cr, reflecting heavy capex
3. **FY25 specifically impacted by the Vashivali factory fire** (April 2024) — destroying inventory, disrupting operations, and causing one-time charges. Insurance claims of ~₹150 Cr are pending/partially received.
4. **Volatile "Other Income"** — ranging from +₹36 Cr to -₹38 Cr, distorting reported profits

> *Buffett: "Earnings can be as pliable as putty when a charlatan heads the company. Eventually, however, economics will trump accounting."*

The economics here show a business that has been investing heavily (capex, acquisitions, international expansion) but has not yet translated that investment into commensurate bottom-line growth. This is the single biggest question mark.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Narrow Moat — Switching Costs + Niche Specialisation**

**Moat Sources:**

1. **Formulation Expertise & Product Library (8,000+ fragrances):** Creating a fragrance is part science, part art. It takes years to build a library of formulations, and customers become embedded because reformulating existing products with a new supplier risks changing the scent that consumers already prefer. This creates genuine switching costs.

2. **Customer Relationships & Application Knowledge:** F&F companies work closely with FMCG clients during product development. Once a fragrance is designed into a product, switching is costly and risky.

3. **Indian Market Knowledge:** SHK understands Indian consumer preferences (attar-based, regional scent profiles) better than MNC competitors. This is a meaningful advantage in the mass/mid-market segment.

4. **Vertical Integration into Aroma Ingredients:** Owning upstream molecules provides cost advantages and supply security.

5. **Only Listed Indian F&F Company of Scale:** No direct listed domestic peer — provides a uniqueness premium for investors seeking F&F exposure.

### Moat Weaknesses:

1. **Global MNCs are formidable competitors:** Givaudan, IFF-Firmenich, and Symrise collectively control ~60% of the global F&F market. They have vastly larger R&D budgets, broader product portfolios, and deeper relationships with global FMCG companies.

2. **Limited pricing power:** Fragrance is a cost input for FMCG companies. When raw material costs rise, SHK cannot always pass them through immediately, leading to margin compression (as seen in FY22-23 and FY25).

3. **Scale disadvantage vs. MNCs:** SHK's revenue (~₹2,100 Cr) is a fraction of Givaudan (~$7B) or IFF (~$11B). R&D spending is consequently much lower.

4. **Technology disruption risk is LOW:** Fragrance creation is unlikely to be disrupted by technology — it requires chemistry expertise, olfactory talent, and physical manufacturing. AI may enhance molecule discovery but won't replace the core competence.

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."*

The fragrance business itself is wonderfully resistant to change — people will always need their products to smell good. But SHK's competitive position WITHIN this industry is only moderate, not dominant. The moat exists but is narrow, and the company is fighting well-capitalised global competitors for share in the more profitable MNC customer segment.

**Honest Verdict:** The moat is real but narrow. It protects the base business (Indian mass market) but may not be sufficient to sustainably earn above-average returns as the company moves into more competitive segments (international, MNC customers).

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK — Can This Business Scale and Is It Scaling?

### Part A: Unit Economics of Scaling

**Revenue Scalability:** Moderate. Fragrances are formulated products — each new customer/product requires custom development. This is not a zero-marginal-cost business. However, once a fragrance is designed into a client's product, it generates recurring revenue with minimal additional cost.

**Assessment: ERRATIC — No consistent operating leverage.**

In some years (FY21, FY24), incremental margins are excellent (38-56%), suggesting the business CAN scale profitably. But in other years (FY18, FY19, FY22, FY25), incremental margins are NEGATIVE — the company is spending more than the incremental revenue generates. This inconsistency is a serious concern.

**Capital Scalability:**

The business requires meaningful capital to scale. Revenue has doubled but so has the asset base — this is NOT capital-light scaling.

### Part B: Scaling Runway

**TAM:** The Indian F&F market is estimated at ~₹8,000-10,000 Cr and growing at 8-10% annually. SHK has ~14-20% domestic market share. The global F&F market is ~$30 billion. SHK's current revenue of ~₹2,100 Cr represents a tiny fraction of the global opportunity.

**Runway:** Substantial. At ₹2,100 Cr revenue vs. a domestic TAM of ~₹10,000 Cr, the domestic runway is ~5x. Including global opportunity (US market entry, European operations), the runway expands significantly.

**S-Curve Position:** The company is in the **Growth Phase** — model proven domestically, now expanding internationally and into flavours. Not yet at the inflection point where margins expand sustainably.

> *Buffett: "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the enemy of the mediocre."* SHK is currently in the "mediocre" zone — time has not been kind over the past decade (stock price is below IPO price). The business needs to demonstrate that its heavy investment phase translates into sustainably higher margins and returns.

> *Buffett: "When a management team with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."*

To be fair, the stock was richly valued at IPO (P/E ~35x) during the 2015 market euphoria, and the current price at ₹121 is depressed by the Vashivali fire impact and broader small/mid-cap selloff. But the $1 test is objective, and it fails.

### Capital Allocation Track Record: Mixed

**Positives:**
- Share buybacks reduced shares from 14.5 Cr to 13.8 Cr — good discipline
- Consistent dividend payments (though erratic payout ratios)
- International expansion via acquisitions (PFW Netherlands) has added capabilities
- US market entry is strategically sound

**Negatives:**
- Debt has risen 10x in a decade — aggressive use of leverage for expansion
- Returns on invested capital have declined (ROCE from 19% to 10%)
- The Vashivali fire exposed concentration risk — heavy dependence on a single facility
- Promoter pledge of shares (Keva Constructions pledged 10 lakh shares in Jan 2026) is a yellow flag

### Promoter Ownership: 54.87% (Dec 2025) — declining from 58.95% (FY24)

The ~4% decline in promoter holding over 2 years is concerning. Promoters are selling or diluting — not the signal of confidence Buffett looks for.

### Management Candour: Adequate

Management provides regular investor presentations, concall transcripts, and discusses challenges openly (fire impact, margin pressure). The new Group CFO (Jagdish Agarwal) has committed to financial discipline and working capital efficiency.
