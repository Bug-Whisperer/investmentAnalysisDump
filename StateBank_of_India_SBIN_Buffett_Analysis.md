## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

State Bank of India is India's largest bank by every conceivable measure — assets, deposits, branches, customers, and market capitalisation within the PSU banking universe. With over 200 years of history, 22,000+ branches, and a deposit market share of ~22.5%, SBI is not merely a bank — it is India's financial infrastructure.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. Banking is one of Buffett's favourite sectors — he owned significant stakes in Wells Fargo, Bank of America, and American Express for decades. A bank takes deposits, lends money, and earns the spread. SBI does this at a scale no other Indian institution matches.

**Business Model Type:** SBI is a **toll bridge** on India's financial system. Every rupee that flows through India's economy — from government salary disbursements to home loans to corporate credit — disproportionately flows through SBI's pipes. When the Indian economy grows, SBI grows almost by definition.

> *Buffett: "I try to invest in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — SBI has survived countless management changes (being government-run), multiple credit crises, and India's volatile economic cycles. The franchise is bigger than any individual manager.

**Type of Financial Institution:** Universal Bank (Public Sector) — the largest in India. Operates across retail banking, corporate banking, treasury, insurance (SBI Life, SBI General), asset management (SBI MF — India's largest), and international operations across 30+ countries.

**Core Economic Engine:** Primarily spread income (NIM-driven), supplemented by significant fee income from cross-selling insurance, mutual funds, and transaction banking. The primary moat is **cheap funding** — SBI's massive CASA (Current Account Savings Account) deposit base gives it structurally lower cost of funds than virtually every competitor.

**Critical Buffett Insight for Banks:** For SBI, deposits are the raw material of the business, not a financing choice. The ₹54+ lakh crore deposit base is simultaneously a liability (owed to depositors) AND the bank's single greatest competitive asset (cheap funding that generates NIM). This fundamentally changes how every financial metric must be interpreted.

### Trend Narrative

The income statement tells a dramatic turnaround story. SBI went through a devastating NPA cycle during FY2016-2019, with the nadir in FY2018 when the bank posted a net loss of ₹3,749 Cr — its worst year. The RBI's Asset Quality Review (AQR) forced recognition of hidden bad loans, leading to massive provisioning that wiped out profits.

From that trough, SBI has executed one of the most impressive bank turnarounds in Indian history:
- Net profit went from **-₹3,749 Cr (FY2018)** to **₹80,523 Cr (FY2025)** — a transformation from loss-making to India's most profitable bank
- EPS went from **-₹5.11 to ₹86.91** in 7 years
- ROE recovered from **-2% to 17%** — now solidly in the "Good to Excellent" tier
- Revenue growth has been steady at 9-19% CAGR over various periods

**Key concerns to watch:**
- The 5-year profit CAGR of 36.3% is spectacular but is heavily base-effected (starting from the NPA crisis trough). Going forward, profit growth will normalise to 12-15%.
- Other Income at ₹1,99,771 Cr (TTM) is very significant — driven by subsidiary income (SBI Life, SBI MF), treasury gains, and fee income. This diversification is a strength.

> *Buffett: "In business, I look for economic castles protected by unbreachable moats."* — SBI's ₹54+ lakh crore deposit base IS the moat. No competitor can replicate this overnight.

### Key Insights

**Actual credit cost vs. normalised credit cost:**

**Buffett's Owner Earnings verdict:** SBI's true sustainable earning power is approximately ₹55,000-65,000 Cr (₹62-73 normalised EPS), assuming a normal credit environment with 0.6-0.8% credit cost. The current reported EPS of ₹87-92 is inflated by the benign credit cycle. **Investors should value SBI on normalised EPS of ~₹62-73, NOT on reported EPS of ₹87-92.**

### The Critical ROE Quality Check

**High ROE from high ROA is QUALITY. High ROE from high leverage is DANGEROUS.**

SBI's current ROE of 17% comes from:
- ROA of ~1.15% × Leverage of ~15x = ~17.3% ROE

**This is GOOD quality.** ROA has improved from near-zero (FY2018-2019) to 1.15%, which is in the "Good" category for a large PSU bank. The leverage has actually DECLINED from ~17x to ~15x, meaning ROE is being driven by improved profitability, NOT by increased risk-taking. This is exactly what Buffett looks for.

|---|---|---|---|---|

| Excellent | 1.5-2.0% | 15-18% | 2.5-3.5x | — |
| **Good** | **1.0-1.5%** | **12-15%** | **1.5-2.5x** | **SBI is HERE on ROA (1.15%)** |
| Mediocre | 0.5-1.0% | 8-12% | 0.8-1.5x | — |
| Poor | <0.5% | <8% | <1.0x | SBI was here in FY2017-2019 |

**SBI's ROA of 1.15% places it in the "Good" tier, deserving a P/B of 1.5-2.5x.** The current P/B of 1.63x is at the lower end of this range — suggesting the market is not giving full credit for the transformation.

**However, a critical caveat:** The current ROE of 17% is partly inflated by below-normal provisions. On normalised credit cost, ROE would be ~11-13%, placing SBI closer to the "Mediocre/Good" boundary. This is the key debate: Is the current low credit cost the new normal (structural improvement) or a cyclical peak (bound to revert)?

**Buffett's Test:** *"A great bank earns high ROE primarily through high ROA (not excessive leverage), maintains this through credit cycles, and can reinvest retained earnings at the same high ROE to compound book value."* SBI passes the first two criteria (improving ROA, declining leverage). The third — maintaining through credit cycles — is unproven at these levels, as SBI has only been at 17% ROE for ~3 years.

### Buffett's Financial Fortress Assessment — Adapted for Banks

**1. Capital Adequacy:** SBI's CRAR (Capital Adequacy Ratio) stands at approximately 13.5-14% with CET-1 at ~10-11%. While above the RBI minimum of 11.5% (including buffers), it is not as strong as some private banks (which operate at 16-18%). This is adequate but not a fortress. The recent equity raise (FY26, bringing shares from 892 Cr to 923 Cr) indicates the bank needs occasional capital top-ups for growth. **Score: Adequate but not elite.**

**2. Asset Quality:** This is where SBI truly shines in the current cycle. GNPA has fallen from a horrific **10.91% (FY2018) to 2.13% (FY2025)**. Net NPA is at just **0.52%** with a Provision Coverage Ratio of ~93% — meaning almost all bad loans are already provided for. The balance sheet is CLEAN. **Score: Excellent — currently at multi-decade best.**

**3. Funding Stability:** SBI has the most stable funding base of any Indian bank. With ₹54+ lakh crore in deposits, a CASA ratio of ~41-43%, and the majority being granular retail deposits from 50+ crore customers, the deposit base is extremely sticky. No wholesale funding crisis can threaten SBI. The Government of India guarantee (implicit) further eliminates any run-on-the-bank risk. **Score: Best-in-class.**

**4. Hidden Risks:** The restructured book has been largely absorbed post-COVID. Slippages have moderated significantly. The SMA (Special Mention Account) pool is manageable. There is no visible large hidden NPA risk at this stage of the cycle. **However** — and this is the critical Buffett caveat — this is a benign credit cycle. The true test comes during the next downturn.

> *Buffett: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — For banks, bad economics = bad asset quality. SBI's asset quality is currently excellent, but investors must remember that one credit cycle can destroy a decade of profits. SBI's 2016-2019 experience is proof. The key question is: has the bank's risk management structurally improved, or are we simply enjoying a cyclical sweet spot?

## 4. CASH FLOW ANALYSIS — Earnings Quality & Capital Generation

> **Critical Note:** Traditional cash flow analysis is meaningless for banks and NBFCs. When a bank grows its loan book, that shows up as massive cash outflow from operations — but that is the equivalent of a retailer buying inventory, not a sign of cash burn. Free Cash Flow (CFO minus CapEx) is a nonsensical metric for a bank. SBI's wildly fluctuating operating cash flows (ranging from -₹96,508 Cr in FY2018 to +₹89,919 Cr in FY2021) reflect loan book growth patterns, not operating health.

> *Buffett: "A bank that must repeatedly raise equity capital to grow is destroying per-share value."* — SBI's history includes dilutive equity raises during the crisis (bad), but the current internal capital generation rate of ~14% suggests that frequent dilution is no longer necessary for normal growth. The FY26 raise was modest and likely timed to build a capital buffer, not to survive.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: Cost Advantage (Cheap Deposits) + Distribution Scale + Regulatory Moat

**Buffett: "In business, I look for economic castles protected by unbreachable moats."**

SBI's moat is multi-layered:

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits (The #1 Moat)**
- CASA ratio ~41% gives SBI a structural cost-of-funds advantage. While this has declined from ~45% (FY2021), it remains among the best in PSU banking.
- SBI's cost of deposits (~5.0-5.5%) is meaningfully lower than smaller banks and NBFCs (6.5-8%).
- **Moat Status: STRONG but slightly eroding** — CASA has drifted down as rate cycle makes term deposits more attractive. This is cyclical and likely to reverse when rates decline.

**2. Distribution & Branch Network**
- 22,000+ branches, 65,000+ ATMs, presence in every district of India.
- This network took 200 years to build. No private bank or fintech can replicate this physical infrastructure.
- **Moat Status: ENDURING.** Physical branches are becoming LESS important for transactions but MORE important as trust anchors for high-value financial products (insurance, home loans, wealth management).

**3. Underwriting Skill (Through-Cycle NPA Performance)**
- SBI's NPA performance DURING the supercycle was terrible — GNPA peaked at ~10%+ (FY2018).
- However, the cleanup has been thorough and the bank learned hard lessons.
- Current GNPA of 2.13% is among the best in PSU banking.
- **Moat Status: IMPROVING.** The bank has demonstrably better underwriting discipline now, but unproven through another full credit cycle.

**4. Technology & Digital Adoption**
- YONO (SBI's digital banking app) has 7+ Cr registered users.
- Digital transactions as % of total have increased significantly.
- **Moat Status: ADEQUATE.** SBI is not a technology leader but has invested enough to remain competitive.

**5. Regulatory Moat**
- Banking license scarcity + TBTF (Too Big To Fail) status = near-zero probability of existential risk.
- Government ownership (55.5%) provides implicit sovereign guarantee.
- **Moat Status: UNBREACHABLE.**

**6. Subsidiary Platform (Cross-Sell)**
- SBI Life Insurance: Market leader, listed entity (SBI holds ~55%)
- SBI Cards (SBI Cards & Payment Services): Listed, leading credit card issuer
- SBI Mutual Fund: Among top 5 AMCs in India
- SBI General Insurance: Growing rapidly
- The ability to cross-sell these products to 50 Cr+ customers through 22,000+ branches is an enormous value multiplier.
- **Moat Status: STRONG and growing.** This is increasingly the hidden value in SBI.

### Susceptibility to Change — The Buffett "Absence of Change" Test

**Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."**

**Banking is a business with remarkable absence of change in its fundamental economics.** People will need to save (deposits), borrow (loans), and transfer money (payments) in 10, 20, and 50 years. The fundamental act of intermediation — taking in deposits and lending them out at a spread — has not changed in 500 years.

What IS changing:
- The **channel** through which banking happens (digital vs. physical) — SBI is adapting
- The **regulatory framework** (RBI norms, Basel III, etc.) — SBI complies
- The **competition** (private banks, fintechs) — SBI's scale moat holds

What is NOT changing:
- The need for a trusted deposit repository (SBI's core franchise)
- The need for credit at scale (only banks with ₹40+ lakh Cr balance sheets can finance India's infrastructure and housing needs)

**Verdict: SBI scores well on the "absence of change" test.** The business will look substantially similar in 10-20 years. The moat may erode at the edges (fee income from payments, some lending verticals) but the core franchise — cheap, stable deposits deployed at a spread — is timeless.

### Analysis

**Critical Finding:** In FY2020-FY2022, SBI was provisioning ABOVE normalised levels — it was being conservative, cleaning up legacy NPAs. This means reported earnings were BELOW true sustainable earnings during those years.

Since FY2023, however, the actual credit cost has fallen BELOW the historical 5-year average. This means current reported earnings are HIGHER than normalised "owner earnings." The gap is about ₹15,000-19,000 Cr — meaning roughly 20-25% of reported FY2025 profits come from lower-than-normalised provisions.

**This does NOT mean the bank is under-provisioning.** The PCR at ~93% is very high, and the GNPA at 2.13% is genuinely low. What it means is that when the credit cycle eventually turns — and it will — provisions will rise, and reported earnings will fall back toward the normalised level.

**Normalised EPS for valuation purposes:** ~₹73 (not the reported ₹87-92). This gives a normalised PE of ~14.5x at ₹1,059, compared to the reported PE of ~12x. The stock is not as cheap as it looks on headline PE.

> *Buffett on the difference between accounting earnings and economic earnings: "Accounting is the language of business... but it can also obscure the truth."* — For SBI, the truth is that ~₹73/share of EPS is sustainable through a normal credit cycle. The extra ₹15-18/share is cyclical tailwind from benign credit conditions.

### Fair P/B Multiple Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Using Reported ROE (17%):
  ROE = 17%
  g   = ROE × Retention = 17% × 82% = 13.9%
  CoE = 11% (investor required return)
  Fair P/B = (0.17 - 0.139) / (0.11 - 0.139) = 0.031 / (-0.029) = NEGATIVE (model breaks)
  
  Note: When g > CoE, the Gordon model produces nonsensical results. This happens because 
  a 17% ROE with 82% retention implies growth ABOVE the cost of equity — which if sustainable
  would justify an infinite P/B. This signals the market suspects ROE will mean-revert lower.

Using Normalised ROE (13%):
  ROE = 13% (on normalised credit cost)
  g   = 13% × 82% = 10.7%
  CoE = 11%
  Fair P/B = (0.13 - 0.107) / (0.11 - 0.107) = 0.023 / 0.003 = 7.7x
  
  This is unrealistic (too high) because the model is hypersensitive when g ≈ CoE.

Using More Conservative Assumptions:
  ROE = 14% (sustainable through-cycle)
  g   = 14% × 75% = 10.5% (lower retention for safety)
  CoE = 12% (higher required return for PSU bank)
  Fair P/B = (0.14 - 0.105) / (0.12 - 0.105) = 0.035 / 0.015 = 2.3x
```

**Fair P/B (conservative): ~2.0-2.5x** on sustainable ROE of 13-14%.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Cost Advantage (Cheap Deposits) + Distribution Scale + Regulatory Moat**

SBI's moat is multi-layered and among the widest in Indian banking:

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits (Primary Moat — Cheap Funding):**
SBI's CASA ratio of ~41-43% translates to a cost of deposits of approximately 4.5-5.0%, among the lowest in the industry. With ₹54 lakh crore in deposits — 22.5% of India's entire banking system — this is an unassailable funding advantage. Every quarter, every year, SBI starts the game with cheaper raw material than almost every competitor. This flows directly to NIM and profitability.

**2. Distribution & Branch Network:**
22,000+ branches across India, including deep rural penetration. This physical network took over 200 years to build. The closest competitor (Bank of Baroda) has less than half the branches. Digital layering (YONO app with 7+ crore users) adds a modern acquisition channel on top of this legacy distribution.

**3. Underwriting Skill (Improving):**
SBI's through-cycle NPA performance is mixed — it was badly hit during the 2016-2019 crisis. However, the bank has dramatically improved its risk management since. Post-COVID, NPA management has been excellent. The test will be the NEXT downturn.

**4. Regulatory Moat:**
As India's largest PSU bank and a designated systemically important financial institution (D-SIFI), SBI enjoys implicit sovereign backing. No depositor will ever lose money. New banking licenses are extremely scarce (RBI has issued only 2 universal bank licenses in the last 20 years). This creates a near-impenetrable entry barrier.

**5. Cross-Sell Platform:**
SBI Life Insurance (76% subsidiary), SBI Mutual Fund (India's largest by AUM), SBI Cards — these subsidiaries generate significant fee income and are sold through the bank's massive distribution network. The value of the platform is in the cross-sell ability.

**6. Technology & Digital:**
SBI has invested heavily in digital (YONO platform). Digital transactions as a percentage of total are rising. However, SBI is NOT a technology leader — it is a fast follower.

**7. Fintech Disruption Risk:**
UPI has reduced some fee income potential, and digital lenders compete at the margin. However, for a bank of SBI's scale — where the moat is primarily cheap deposits, not technology — fintech is a manageable threat, not an existential one. Fintechs don't take deposits. They are asset-side competitors only, and even there, they cannot match the pricing of a bank with 41% CASA.

### Key Competitors and Threat Assessment

|---|---|---|
| HDFC Bank | Manageable | Better technology, better ROA, but SBI's cost-of-deposits advantage narrows the gap; HDFC is focused on different customer segments |

### Susceptibility to Change — Buffett's "Absence of Change" Test

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."*

Banking in India is NOT a rapidly changing business at its core. People will still need savings accounts, home loans, car loans, and credit facilities in 10 and 20 years. The delivery mechanism may evolve (digital vs. branch), but the fundamental business — take deposits, make loans, earn the spread — will remain identical.

SBI's moat (cheap deposits, branch network, government backing) is particularly resistant to change. The deposit base is sticky, the branch network is built, and the regulatory moat protects against new entrants. **Verdict: SBI passes Buffett's "absence of change" test.**

### Fair P/B Multiple Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)
Where:
  ROE = 15% (sustainable, using normalised earnings — slightly below current 17% to account for cycle normalisation)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 15% × 82% = 12.3%
  CoE = 11% (reasonable cost of equity for India's largest PSU bank)

Fair P/B = (15% - 12.3%) / (11% - 12.3%) = 2.7% / (-1.3%)
```

**Note:** The Gordon Growth Model produces a negative/infinite result when g > CoE, which implies the stock deserves a very high P/B. However, this is mathematically unstable because growth cannot sustainably exceed cost of equity forever. Using a more conservative sustainable growth rate:

- **Conservative g = 10%:** Fair P/B = (15% - 10%) / (11% - 10%) = 5.0x (clearly too high — model breaks down at boundary)
- **Using 2-stage approach — Explicit growth of 14% for 5 years, then normalised to 8%:**
  - Fair P/B ≈ **1.8-2.2x Book**

### Buffett's Bank Valuation Rules of Thumb

| ROE | Deserved P/B | Logic |
|---|---|---|
| ROE ≈ CoE (~11%) | ~1.0x Book | Earning just required return |
| ROE = 13-14% | 1.5-2.0x Book | Decent bank |
