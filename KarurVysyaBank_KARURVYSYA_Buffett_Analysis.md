## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Karur Vysya Bank (KVB) is a century-old private sector bank, founded in 1916 in Karur, Tamil Nadu, by M.A. Venkatarama Chettiar and Athi Krishna Chettiar. At its core, this is a **spread-income (NIM-driven) regional banking franchise** with deep roots in the Retail, Agriculture, and MSME (RAM) segments across southern India.

**Business Model Simplicity:** Would Buffett understand it? Absolutely. KVB takes deposits (its raw material), lends them out at a higher rate (its product), and earns the spread. It is a classic **toll-bridge on regional commerce** — farmers, small traders, and MSMEs in Tamil Nadu and surrounding states need credit, and KVB sits at the intersection of that need with a 901-branch network, 55% of which are in semi-urban and rural areas.

**Type of Financial Institution:** Old-generation private sector bank, primarily a **retail and MSME-focused spread lender**. Not a universal bank with large investment banking operations, not a microfinance NBFC, and not a housing finance company. It is a traditional commercial bank with:
- **Retail Banking (64% of revenue):** Mortgages, jewel loans, vehicle loans, personal loans
- **Corporate/Wholesale Banking (~18%):** Advances to corporates, trusts, partnership firms
- **Treasury (17%):** Government securities, debt instruments, forex
- **Other Banking (~1%):** Bancassurance, demat services

**Core Economic Engine:** Spread income (NIM-driven), supplemented by fee income from bancassurance, trade finance, and transaction banking. The bank's primary moat is its **deeply entrenched distribution in southern India** and its **underwriting skill** — demonstrated by dramatically improving asset quality from GNPA of 9%+ to sub-1% over six years.

> *"I try to invest in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* — Buffett

KVB has proven it can survive bad management periods (the NPA mess of FY18-FY20) and emerge stronger. The current management team under MD & CEO Ramesh Babu B has executed a remarkable turnaround.

### Applying Buffett's "Financial Fortress" Standard for Banks

The fortress is NOT about low debt (deposits ARE the business). For banks, Buffett evaluates:

**1. Capital Adequacy — STRONG ✅**
CRAR at 18.17% (FY25) is well above the RBI minimum of 11.5% and above the 15% "strong" threshold. Tier-1 at 17.12% is excellent. The bank has so much capital that it funded a 1:5 bonus issue from reserves without any equity dilution. This is a genuine fortress.

**2. Asset Quality — EXCEPTIONAL ✅✅**
This is where KVB's story becomes extraordinary. GNPA collapsed from **8.99% (FY19) → 0.71% (Q3 FY26)**. NNPA fell from **4.37% → 0.19%**. PCR rose from ~52% to 96.8%. The bank has not just cleaned up its book — it has built a **massive provision buffer**. With PCR near 97%, almost every NPA rupee is already provisioned. This provides enormous cushion against future credit shocks.

**3. Funding Stability — MODERATE ⚠️**
The CASA ratio at ~27-28% is the weakest link. This is below the private sector average (~38-42% for HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank). KVB relies more on term deposits, which are stickier than wholesale funding but costlier than CASA. The declining CASA trend (from ~33% in FY22 to 27% now) is a concern — management acknowledges outflows to mutual funds and real estate. However, the deposit base is granular (primarily retail), which provides stability even if the CASA ratio is modest.

**4. Hidden Risks — LOW ✅**
No large restructured book has been disclosed. Slippages are trending DOWN. The SMA book (Special Mention Accounts) has not been flagged as a concern. The Q3 FY26 fresh slippages were just ₹154 Cr (0.63% annualised), which is remarkably low.

> *"When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — Buffett

For KVB, the reverse is true: new management tackled a bank with a terrible NPA reputation, and the BANK's economics have been transformed. The NPA cleanup is genuine, not cosmetic.

### [BANK/NBFC MODE — Earnings Quality & Provision Adequacy Analysis]

For banks, the equivalent of "maintenance CapEx" is **credit cost** (provisions). The key question: are current provisions sufficient, or is the bank under-provisioning to inflate earnings?

| **= Normalised Earnings ("Bank Owner Earnings")** | **~-176** | **~-107** | **~157** | **~487** | **~945** | **~1,191** |

*Normalised Credit Cost = 5-year avg credit cost as % of advances (~1.0%) × current year avg advances. This is "maintenance provisioning" for a normal credit environment.*

### Key Insight: Actual vs. Normalised Credit Cost

Here's where the analysis gets nuanced. KVB's **actual** credit cost in FY25 was very low (~0.15-0.20% of advances) because:
1. Asset quality is genuinely excellent (GNPA 0.76%, NNPA 0.20%)
2. PCR is at 96.8% — the bank has OVER-provisioned
3. Recoveries and write-backs are substantial

If we normalise credit cost to 1.0% of advances (a long-term through-cycle average for a bank of KVB's profile), the "Owner Earnings" are lower than reported net income — but still show a dramatic improvement trend.

**However**, the current benign credit environment means actual credit cost WILL rise from today's abnormally low levels. The question is whether it rises to 0.5% (manageable), 1.0% (normal), or 2%+ (crisis). Given the loan book composition (largely secured — jewel loans, mortgages, vehicle finance) and the massive PCR buffer, a return to 2%+ seems unlikely absent a systemic crisis.

**Provision Coverage Ratio Trend:**
- FY20: ~55% → FY22: ~69% → FY24: 94.9% → **FY25: 96.8%** → **Q3 FY26: 96.8%**

This is RISING, meaning the bank is being **conservative**, building buffers, not releasing reserves to boost profits. This is the hallmark of quality management.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

KVB's moat is best described as a **regional distribution and relationship moat** — not a wide moat like HDFC Bank, but a meaningful narrow moat that protects its economics.

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits — WEAK MOAT ⚠️**
CASA at ~27% is well below industry leaders (HDFC Bank ~38%, Kotak ~52%). This is KVB's biggest weakness. However, the deposit base is granular and predominantly retail, which provides stickiness even at lower CASA levels.

**2. Distribution & Branch Network — MODERATE MOAT ✅**
901 branches with 55% in semi-urban/rural areas of southern India. This is expensive to replicate and gives KVB a natural geographic moat in its core markets. The bank knows its customers — farmers, traders, MSMEs — through decades of relationship.

**3. Underwriting Skill — STRONG MOAT ✅✅**
The NPA cleanup from 9% GNPA to 0.71% is proof. More importantly, the bank navigated demonetisation, IL&FS crisis, and COVID without a second NPA spike. Fresh slippages at 0.63% annualised (Q3 FY26) demonstrate genuine underwriting discipline.

**4. Technology & Digital — IMPROVING ✅**
KVB has invested in digital channels, UPI, and mobile banking. Digital transactions are growing. However, this is table stakes, not a moat.

**5. Regulatory Moat — EXISTS ✅**
Banking licences are scarce in India. The RBI has not issued new full-scale banking licences in years. KVB's existing licence, infrastructure, and regulatory compliance are genuine barriers to entry.

**6. Fintech Disruption Risk — MODERATE ⚠️**
UPI and digital lenders are real threats to fee income and customer relationships, particularly in payments and small-ticket lending. However, KVB's core lending (mortgages, jewel loans, MSME credit) requires physical appraisal and relationship — not easily disrupted by fintechs.

### Key Competitors

- **HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank:** Much larger, wider moats, higher CASA. Not direct competitors for KVB's rural/semi-urban niche but set the valuation benchmark.
- **City Union Bank, Tamilnad Mercantile Bank, South Indian Bank:** Direct peers — regional private banks in similar geographies. KVB is outperforming all of them on ROA and asset quality.
- **Federal Bank, Karur-peer group:** Moderate competition; KVB has emerged as one of the best-performing old-generation private banks.

### Susceptibility to Change

> *"We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Buffett

Banking fundamentals change slowly. People will need mortgages, jewel loans, and MSME credit in 10-20 years. The channels (digital vs. branch) may evolve, but the core business of taking deposits and making loans is timeless. KVB's southern India franchise is unlikely to face existential disruption. **The absence of change favours KVB.**

### Fair P/B Multiple Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Where:
  ROE = 17% (sustainable, slightly below current peak of 18-20%)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 17% × 88% = ~15% (capped at reasonable 12% for terminal)
  CoE = 12% (investor's required return for a mid-cap bank)

Using terminal g = 12%:
  Fair P/B = (0.17 - 0.12) / (0.12 - 0.12) → undefined (g approaches CoE)

Using more conservative terminal g = 8%:

Using 5% terminal:
  Fair P/B = (0.17 - 0.05) / (0.12 - 0.05) = 0.12 / 0.07 = 1.71x
```
