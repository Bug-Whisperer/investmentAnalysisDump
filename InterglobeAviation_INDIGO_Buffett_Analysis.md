## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Model

Interglobe Aviation, operating as **IndiGo**, is India's largest airline by market share (~64% domestic, ~21% international). The business model is deceptively simple: move people from Point A to Point B at the lowest possible cost, on time, without frills. IndiGo operates as a **low-cost carrier (LCC)** — it sells seats, not experiences. Passengers pay low base fares and optionally add ancillary services (meals, baggage, seats, insurance).

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely — it is a simple transportation business. However, Buffett has historically **disliked airlines intensely.** His famous quote:

> *"If a farsighted capitalist had been present at Kitty Hawk, he would have done his friend a huge favor by shooting Orville down."*

He later softened, buying a basket of airline stocks in 2016-2017 (Delta, Southwest, United, American) — only to sell them all at a loss in 2020 during COVID. His reasoning: *"The world has changed for airlines. I don't know how it's changed. I hope it corrects itself in a reasonably prompt way."*

**Business Classification:**
IndiGo is a **capacity-constrained commodity business with some cost advantages.** It is NOT a toll bridge (passengers have alternatives), NOT a consumer franchise (no brand loyalty at the margin — passengers buy on price and schedule), and NOT a switching-cost business (zero switching costs). However, IndiGo has built **structural cost advantages** through fleet commonality (single-type Airbus fleet reducing maintenance complexity), sale-and-leaseback financial engineering, and operational scale that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**The Economic Engine:**
- Revenue = Passengers × Fare per Passenger + Ancillary Revenue
- Costs are dominated by: **Aviation Turbine Fuel (~35-40% of costs)**, aircraft lease rentals, airport charges, employee costs, and maintenance
- The business is a **high-fixed-cost, low-margin, high-volume machine** — tiny changes in load factor or fuel prices create massive profit swings
- IndiGo's edge: lowest Cost Per Available Seat Kilometer (CASK) in India, driven by fleet efficiency, high aircraft utilisation, and scale economies

**Key Statistics (as of FY26):**
- Fleet: 440+ aircraft (7th largest airline globally by daily departures)
- Daily flights: ~2,200+
- Destinations: 95+ domestic, 40+ international
- Passengers carried CY25: 124 million
- Market share: ~64% domestic

### Trend Narrative

IndiGo's revenue trajectory tells two stories: a powerful underlying growth engine (Sales CAGR of ~19% over FY15-FY25 per Screener) disrupted by catastrophic external shocks (COVID in FY21). Revenue collapsed 59% in FY21, then recovered with explosive 110% growth in FY23 as pent-up travel demand collided with reduced industry capacity (Jet Airways and GoFirst bankruptcies).

**Margin story is the critical concern for a Buffett investor.** Operating margins have swung from +20% (FY16) to -1% (FY19) to +24% (FY24) — this is extreme cyclicality. Whether these margins are sustainable or peak-cycle is THE key investment question.

Buffett: *"When a management team with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* Airlines have notoriously bad economics. IndiGo's management is brilliant — but is the business economics genuinely different this time?

### Critical IndAS 116 Context

**The balance sheet looks terrifying — but it is an accounting illusion.** Pre-FY20, IndiGo's borrowings were ₹2,429 Cr with D/E of 0.35. Post-FY20, borrowings exploded to ₹22,719 Cr — NOT because the company went on a borrowing spree, but because **IndAS 116 required capitalisation of all operating leases on the balance sheet.** The ~₹67,000 Cr "borrowings" in FY25 are overwhelmingly **aircraft lease liabilities** — the exact same leases that existed before, just now visible.

**True financial position assessment:**
- **Cash & Investments:** IndiGo held ~₹26,400 Cr in investments (as of March 2025 balance sheet) plus cash equivalents within other assets. For reference, per the Q1 FY25 earnings call (June 2024), total cash was ₹36,100 Cr (₹22,088 Cr free cash + ₹14,013 Cr restricted); the March 2025 figure would be higher given strong cash generation in FY25. This is a **massive cash pile** for an airline.
- **True "Net Debt" excluding leases:** Minimal. IndiGo's traditional debt is negligible — the company has historically operated a sale-and-leaseback model with Airbus, meaning it doesn't own most of its aircraft.
- **Negative Equity Period (FY22-FY23):** Accumulated losses during COVID wiped out equity entirely. This was NOT a solvency crisis — the company never had any difficulty meeting its obligations because of strong cash generation from advance ticket sales and the sale-and-leaseback model.

**Buffett's Financial Fortress Assessment:**
The balance sheet is paradoxically both weaker and stronger than it appears. Weaker because the massive lease obligations are real commitments. Stronger because the actual cash position is robust, and IndiGo's working capital is structurally negative (customers pay BEFORE flying — working capital days of -123), meaning growth GENERATES cash rather than consuming it. This is one of IndiGo's most important economic characteristics.

### Cash Flow Quality Assessment

IndiGo's cash flow profile is remarkable for an airline. The CFO/Operating Profit ratio (per Screener) generally exceeds 100%, suggesting the company converts operating profit to cash at favourable rates. This is driven by the **negative working capital cycle** — IndiGo collects cash from passengers weeks before they fly, creating a float that grows with revenue.

FY24-FY25 FCF of ₹20,780 and ₹22,846 Cr represent FCF margins of 30% and 28% — extraordinary for any business, let alone an airline. At ₹592/share FCF (FY25), the stock trades at ~7.7x FCF on FY25 earnings — far cheaper than it appears on a P/E basis.

**However, a critical caveat:** Under IndAS 116, lease payments that were previously operating expenses (in the P&L) are now classified as financing cash outflows (repayment of lease liabilities). This artificially INFLATES CFO and FCF. The true "cash available to the owner" after all lease obligations is lower than reported FCF. For a more honest comparison, EBITDAR minus cash lease rentals is a better proxy — which is essentially what the company reports as operating cash flow before IndAS 116 adjustments.

Buffett: *"Accounting is the language of business. If you don't understand it, you'll be relying on people who may or may not be telling you the truth."* IndAS 116 doesn't change IndiGo's economic reality — but it makes the numbers harder to interpret.

**4. Red Flags:** The Sep25 loss quarter is a stark reminder that airline profitability is binary — one bad quarter can erase an entire year's profits. The Middle East conflict, oil price volatility, and CEO transition (Pieter Elbers departed Mar 2026, Willie Walsh incoming Aug 2026) create significant near-term uncertainty.

**Verdict: DECELERATING.** Revenue growth is slowing, margins are under pressure from oil prices and geopolitical disruptions, and the TTM earnings power (₹83.67 EPS) is well below normalised levels (₹187.70 FY25 EPS). Buffett values predictability above all — and IndiGo's quarterly profile is anything but predictable.

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: Cost Advantage + Scale Economies + Emerging Brand**

IndiGo's competitive position in Indian aviation is **dominant but not impregnable.** The moat is built on:

**1. Cost Leadership — The Primary Moat (Strong)**
IndiGo has the lowest CASK (Cost per Available Seat Kilometer) among Indian carriers. This is achieved through: single aircraft type (Airbus A320 family — reduces training, maintenance, and spare parts complexity); young fleet (fuel-efficient NEO engines); high aircraft utilisation (~12 hours/day); lean operations with low overheads. This cost advantage has been consistent for over a decade.

**2. Scale Economies (Strong)**
With 64% domestic market share and 440+ aircraft, IndiGo benefits from bulk purchasing (fuel, spare parts, airport services), negotiating power with airports and OEMs, brand recognition driving direct bookings (lower distribution costs), and network density enabling connections without traditional hub-and-spoke.

**3. Brand Recognition (Moderate)**
IndiGo = reliable, on-time, affordable. Among Indian consumers, it is the default choice. However, brand loyalty in airlines is thin — a ₹500 fare difference will shift passengers.

**4. Regulatory Moat (Emerging)**
Indian aviation has brutally high barriers to entry: airport slot scarcity, capital requirements, licensing complexity, and an operating environment that has bankrupted Kingfisher, Jet Airways, GoFirst, and multiple others. IndiGo is the last profitable large-scale survivor. Its ability to access scarce airport slots (especially at congested metros) is a growing structural advantage.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Nature |
|---|---|---|

## 21. REGULATORY RISKS & POLICY HEADWINDS

### Key Regulatory Framework

IndiGo operates under the regulatory oversight of the **DGCA (Directorate General of Civil Aviation)**, Ministry of Civil Aviation (MoCA), and the Airports Authority of India (AAI). The regulatory stance is **broadly supportive** — the Indian government has identified aviation as a growth priority (UDAAN scheme, new airport construction, increased capacity).

### Current Headwinds

1. **FDTL (Flight Duty Time Limitation) Norms:** New DGCA norms implemented in 2025 limit pilot duty hours, constraining capacity growth. IndiGo experienced significant flight cancellations in Dec 2025 due to FDTL implementation. This is a structural constraint that limits near-term fleet utilisation.

2. **ATF Taxation:** Aviation Turbine Fuel is currently **excluded from GST** and is instead taxed via central excise duty plus state-level VAT (which varies from 1-30% across states), making Indian fuel costs among the highest globally. The industry has long lobbied for ATF's inclusion under GST, which would standardise and likely reduce the tax burden. This remains an ongoing structural headwind.

3. **Route Dispersal Guidelines:** Airlines are required to fly to unprofitable Tier 2/3 cities as a proportion of total routes. This constrains route optimisation.

### Buffett's Verdict on Regulatory Risk

The regulatory risk is **(a) Manageable and Fairly Priced.** India's aviation regulations are burdensome but not hostile — the government benefits from a healthy aviation sector. IndiGo has demonstrated the ability to operate profitably within these constraints. The FDTL norms are a one-time adjustment, not a permanent impairment. ATF taxation is a known cost baked into the business model.
