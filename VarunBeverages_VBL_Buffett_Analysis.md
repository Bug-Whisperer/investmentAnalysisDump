## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

Varun Beverages is deceptively simple to understand — and that is precisely what makes it Buffett-territory.

**Business Model:** VBL holds exclusive franchise rights from PepsiCo to manufacture, distribute, and sell PepsiCo-branded beverages across India and several international territories (Morocco, Zambia, Zimbabwe, South Africa, Nepal, Sri Lanka, and others). The brands include Pepsi, 7UP, Mountain Dew, Mirinda, Slice, Tropicana, Sting, Aquafina, and Gatorade. The company also sells snack products (Kurkure, Lay's, Doritos) in some markets and owns its own brands (Creambell ice cream, Jive).

**Economic Engine Type:** This is a **consumer franchise + toll bridge hybrid**:

- **Consumer Franchise:** VBL rides on PepsiCo's globally recognised brands. Consumers walk into shops and ask for "Pepsi" or "Sting" — they don't ask for "Varun Beverages." The brand pull comes from PepsiCo's decades of advertising investment. VBL benefits from this brand equity without bearing the full cost of brand building.

- **Toll Bridge:** As an exclusive franchisee, VBL operates a toll bridge between PepsiCo's brands and the Indian consumer. Every bottle of Pepsi sold in VBL's territory must pass through VBL's manufacturing and distribution infrastructure. PepsiCo cannot bypass VBL — the franchise agreement creates a contractual moat.

- **Switching Costs:** The switching costs are enormous — for PepsiCo, not for consumers. Replacing VBL as the bottler across India and multiple countries would require billions in capex for new plants, cold chain, distribution networks, and years of operational build-out. This makes VBL's position highly defensible.

> **Buffett:** *"I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* VBL is not quite idiot-proof (execution in manufacturing and distribution matters), but the business model is simple: make drinks, put them in bottles, distribute them everywhere. No PhD in AI required.

Would Buffett understand this business? **Absolutely.** He owns Coca-Cola — VBL is the Pepsi equivalent on the bottling side, and Buffett has been intimately familiar with the economics of bottling since his 1988 Coca-Cola investment. This is squarely within the circle of competence.

**The story is one of relentless expansion with improving margins — the hallmark of a scaling franchise:**

Exactly what Buffett looks for.

### Maintenance vs. Growth CapEx Estimation

- **Maintenance CapEx** (to keep existing plants running) ≈ Depreciation ≈ ₹1,216 Cr (Dec 2025). This is the minimum spend needed to prevent the existing asset base from deteriorating.

- **Growth CapEx** (new plants, territory acquisitions, capacity expansion) ≈ Total CapEx - Maintenance CapEx ≈ ₹4,311 - ₹1,216 = **₹3,095 Cr** (~72% of total capex is growth).

- **CapEx/Depreciation of 3.5x** confirms VBL is investing massively above replacement levels. For context, a mature, steady-state business would have CapEx/Dep of ~1.0-1.2x. VBL is investing at 3.5x — classic hyper-growth phase.

### Owner Earnings Calculation (Buffett's Preferred Metric)

| Metric | Dec 2022 | Dec 2023 | Dec 2024 | Dec 2025 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Income | 1,550 | 2,102 | 2,634 | 3,062 |
| + Depreciation & Amortisation | 617 | 681 | 947 | 1,216 |
| - Estimated Maintenance CapEx | -617 | -681 | -947 | -1,216 |

> Since maintenance CapEx ≈ Depreciation for a manufacturing business, Owner Earnings ≈ Net Income. This means VBL's reported earnings are a fair representation of true economic earnings. There is no significant gap between accounting earnings and owner earnings. This is a positive sign — the business is not inflating profits by under-maintaining its assets.

**P/Owner Earnings (current):** ₹1,39,069 Cr / ₹3,062 Cr = **~45.4x** (essentially equal to the PE ratio, confirming earnings quality).

> **Buffett:** *"Owner Earnings represent the relevant item for valuation purposes... reported earnings per share... plus depreciation, depletion, amortisation... less the average annual amount of capitalised expenditures for plant and equipment that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position and its unit volume."* For VBL, the accounting earnings ARE approximately the owner earnings — the business does not deceive.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Consumer Franchise + Contractual Exclusivity (Moderate-to-Strong)**

**Moat Sources:**

1. **Contractual Exclusivity with PepsiCo (Strong):** VBL is the sole PepsiCo bottler for ~90% of India and multiple international territories. This franchise agreement is renewed periodically, and the relationship dates back to the 1990s. PepsiCo has no incentive to disrupt a well-functioning bottling partner — Coca-Cola's experience of bringing bottling in-house (and the capital burden that entailed) serves as a cautionary tale.

2. **Distribution Network (Strong):** VBL operates 37+ manufacturing facilities across India and 10+ internationally, with over 3 million visi-coolers installed at retail points. This cold-chain and distribution network took decades to build and would cost billions to replicate.

3. **Scale Advantage (Moderate-to-Strong):** As the largest PepsiCo franchisee outside the US, VBL enjoys procurement economies, manufacturing scale, and distribution density that no competitor can match in its territories.

4. **Brand Power (Indirect, Strong):** The brands (Pepsi, 7UP, Sting, Mountain Dew) are owned by PepsiCo, not VBL. VBL benefits from the brand without controlling it. This is both a strength (PepsiCo invests in brand building) and a risk (VBL's fate is tied to PepsiCo's brand relevance).

### Moat Assessment — Is It Enduring or Eroding?

**The moat is enduring but not widening in the traditional sense:**

- The franchise agreement provides contractual protection, but it also means VBL's destiny is partly in PepsiCo's hands. If PepsiCo decided to restructure bottling (unlikely but possible), VBL's moat would evaporate.

- The energy drink category (Sting) is a **moat-widening catalyst** — Sting has become a runaway success in India, growing far faster than traditional colas. This shifts the product mix towards higher-margin, faster-growing categories.

- **Weakness:** VBL does not own the brands. This is the key difference between VBL and Coca-Cola (which Buffett owns). A bottler is inherently less moated than the brand owner.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Nature of Threat |
|---|---|---|
| **Coca-Cola / HCCB (Hindustan Coca-Cola Beverages)** | **High but Manageable** | The eternal Pepsi vs. Coke battle. HCCB is the direct competitor in every category. However, the Indian market is growing fast enough for both to thrive — this is not a zero-sum war. |
| **Reliance Consumer Products (Campa Cola)** | **Medium** | Reliance re-launched Campa Cola with aggressive pricing and distribution through Jio Mart / Reliance Retail. This is a new and well-funded competitor. However, brand loyalty in beverages is strong, and Campa's success so far has been limited. |
| **Local / Regional Players** | **Low** | Small regional brands lack the distribution, cold-chain, and brand power to compete at scale. |
| **Health & Wellness Beverages** | **Medium (Long-term)** | The trend towards healthier drinks (coconut water, kombucha, fresh juices) could erode the carbonated soft drink (CSD) market over 10-20 years. However, India's CSD per capita is still very low. |

### Susceptibility to Change

This is critical. **Buffett invests where change is ABSENT:**

- **The core business of bottling and distributing branded beverages has not changed meaningfully in 50+ years.** People buy cold drinks. They buy them from shops, restaurants, and vending machines. The product, the distribution, the consumption occasion — none of this has fundamentally changed.

- **Sting (energy drinks) is the major positive change.** The energy drink category barely existed in India 5 years ago. Now it's VBL's fastest-growing segment. This is additive change, not disruptive change — it adds a new revenue stream without cannibalising the old one.

- **Potential negative change:** Health-consciousness could reduce CSD consumption in the very long term (10-20 years). But India's per capita consumption is still a fraction of global levels, and the mass market in India is price-sensitive, not health-sensitive. This risk is real but distant.

> **Buffett:** *"We do not get enthused about change, with a few exceptions, as a way to make a lot of money. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money and even more in the future."* VBL scores well here — the fundamental business of making and distributing beverages is unlikely to change in any meaningful way over the next 10-20 years. People will still drink cold beverages. PepsiCo brands will still be relevant. VBL's plants and distribution will still be needed.

**Overall Moat Rating: 7/10 — Good, contractually protected, operationally reinforced, but ultimately dependent on PepsiCo's brand health and franchise renewal.**

## 14. ROE QUALITY CHECK & RETURN EXPECTATION FRAMEWORK

### Part A: DuPont Decomposition of ROE

```
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier
    = (Net Income / Revenue) × (Revenue / Total Assets) × (Total Assets / Shareholders' Equity)
```

| Year | Net Profit Margin | Asset Turnover | Equity Multiplier | ROE (Product) | Reported ROCE |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dec 2018 | 5.9% | 0.85 | 3.00 | 15.0% | 15% |
| Dec 2019 | 6.6% | 0.85 | 2.52 | 14.2% | 18% |
| Dec 2020 | 5.5% | 0.76 | 2.40 | 10.1% | 11% |
| Dec 2021 | 8.5% | 0.92 | 2.35 | 18.3% | 17% |
| Dec 2022 | **11.8%** | **1.13** | 2.28 | **30.4%** | **27%** |

| Dec 2025 | **14.1%** | **0.85** | **1.31** | 15.6% | 20% |

### Quality Assessment

**The DuPont decomposition reveals a critical transformation:**

1. **Net Profit Margin has nearly tripled** from 5.9% (2018) to 14.1% (2025). This is the primary driver of ROE improvement — genuine operating leverage and pricing power.

2. **Asset Turnover has declined** from 1.13x (peak in 2022) to 0.85x (2025). This reflects the massive asset base expansion (new plants that are not yet fully utilised). As capacity utilisation ramps up, asset turnover should recover.

3. **Equity Multiplier (Leverage) has collapsed** from 3.0x (2018) to 1.31x (2025). This is the reason ROE dropped from 30% to 16-17% — not because the business deteriorated, but because leverage was drastically reduced after the equity raise.

**The critical insight:** VBL's ROE is now driven almost entirely by **high net margins** and **decent asset turnover**, with **minimal leverage contribution**. This is the healthiest possible ROE composition — exactly what Buffett looks for.

**ROCE vs. ROE Relationship:**

- ROCE (20%) > ROE (~16-17%) — This confirms the business engine is powerful. The gap exists because ROCE is calculated on total capital employed (equity + debt), and VBL's debt is now so low that ROCE exceeds ROE. In a leveraged capital structure, ROE > ROCE (leverage amplifies returns to equity). VBL's current structure is the opposite — ultra-conservative.

> **Note:** The DuPont table above uses year-end equity, giving Dec 2025 ROE of 15.6%. Section 6 uses average equity for Dec 2025, giving 16.8%. Screener.in reports 16.2%. The directional conclusion — ROCE exceeding ROE on an ultra-low-leverage balance sheet — holds across all three methodologies.

- This means the "true" business quality is BETTER than the current ROE suggests. If VBL were to employ moderate leverage (D/E of 0.5x), ROE would jump to ~22-24% — closer to its 5-year average.

> **Buffett:** *"When you combine ignorance and leverage, you get some pretty interesting results."* VBL's management has chosen the opposite — knowledge without leverage. The business is generating 20% ROCE on an unleveraged balance sheet. This is genuine quality.
