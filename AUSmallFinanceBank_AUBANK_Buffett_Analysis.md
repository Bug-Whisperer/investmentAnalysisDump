## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

AU Small Finance Bank is India's **largest Small Finance Bank**, founded in 1996 by Sanjay Agarwal as a vehicle finance NBFC, converted into an SFB in April 2017, and now on the cusp of becoming a **Universal Bank** (RBI in-principle approval received August 7, 2025 — the first SFB ever to achieve this).

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. At its core, AU is a simple business: it collects deposits from individuals and lends those deposits out at higher rates to retail borrowers (vehicle loans, MSME loans, housing loans, gold loans). The spread between what it pays depositors and what it earns from borrowers is its profit engine. This is the oldest business model in banking.

**The Economic Engine:**
- **Spread Income (NIM-driven):** The primary engine. AU earns a NIM of 5.5-6.0%, significantly higher than universal banks (3.0-4.0%), because it lends to underserved retail segments at higher yields.
- **Fee Income:** Growing contributor (~15-16% of total income), driven by cross-selling insurance, mutual funds, credit cards, and transaction fees.
- **The Moat Sources:** (i) Deep understanding of retail/MSME underwriting in semi-urban/rural India built over 30 years; (ii) High-yield asset mix that most large banks cannot replicate; (iii) Growing deposit franchise; (iv) First-mover regulatory advantage in the SFB-to-Universal Bank transition.

**Business Segment Mix (9M FY25):** Retail Banking 76% | Treasury 13% | Wholesale Banking 9% | Others 2%

> *Buffett: "I try to invest in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."* AU's business is simple — take deposits, make retail loans, earn the spread. The question is whether the MOAT protecting that spread is durable. That's what the rest of this analysis determines.

## 5. CAPEX QUALITY ANALYSIS — Earnings Quality & Provision Adequacy

### [BANK/NBFC MODE]

For banks, the equivalent of "maintenance CapEx" is **credit cost (provisions)**. The key question: are current provisions sufficient to cover true expected losses?

### Normalised Earnings (Bank "Owner Earnings")

The correct formula for bank owner earnings adjusts reported net income for the difference between actual and normalised provisions, then subtracts SBC (a real cost often excluded) and adds back D&A:

`Normalised Earnings = Net Income + D&A + (Actual Provisions - Normalised Provisions) - SBC`

When actual provisions EXCEED normalised (conservative bank), this adds back the excess buffer, revealing higher sustainable earnings. When actual provisions are BELOW normalised (aggressive bank), this subtracts the shortfall, revealing lower sustainable earnings.

| Metric | FY20 | FY21 | FY22 | FY23 | FY24 | FY25 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Income (₹ Cr) | 675 | 1,171 | 1,130 | 1,428 | 1,535 | 2,106 |
| Add: D&A (₹ Cr) | 89 | 104 | 140 | 185 | 225 | 259 |
| Actual Provisions (₹ Cr) | ~256 | ~312 | ~606 | ~1,035 | ~1,341 | ~1,793 |
| Normalised Provisions (₹ Cr) | ~310 | ~400 | ~530 | ~690 | ~810 | ~1,190 |
| Provision Adjustment (Actual - Normalised) | **-54** | **-88** | **+76** | **+345** | **+531** | **+603** |
| Less: SBC (Est. ₹ Cr) | ~30 | ~40 | ~50 | ~60 | ~70 | ~90 |
| **= Normalised Earnings ("Owner Earnings")** | **~680** | **~1,147** | **~1,296** | **~1,898** | **~2,221** | **~2,878** |
| Normalised EPS (₹) | ~22.4 | ~36.8 | ~41.1 | ~28.5 | ~33.2 | **~38.6** |

*\*Normalised Provisions = 5-year average credit cost as % of advances (~1.0%) applied to current year's average advances. Actual Provisions = PPOP - PBT (derived from quarterly PPOP data and Screener PBT).*

**Interpretation:** In years where the bank OVER-provisions (FY22-FY25), normalised earnings are HIGHER than reported — the excess provisioning is depressing reported profits. In years where the bank UNDER-provisions (FY20-FY21), normalised earnings are LOWER than reported.

For FY25: Reported Net Income = ₹2,106 Cr. The bank over-provisioned by ~₹603 Cr (actual ₹1,793 vs normalised ₹1,190). After adding back D&A and the excess provision buffer, and subtracting SBC, normalised owner earnings = **~₹2,878 Cr** or **~₹38.6 per share**.

### Actual vs. Normalised Credit Cost

| Year | Actual Provisions (₹ Cr) | Normalised Provisions (₹ Cr) | Actual vs. Normal |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY20 | ~256 | ~310 | **Below Normal** — slightly flattering earnings |
| FY21 | ~312 | ~400 | **Below Normal** — earnings mildly inflated |
| FY22 | ~606 | ~530 | **Above Normal** — conservative provisioning |
| FY23 | ~1,035 | ~690 | **Well Above Normal** — building buffers |
| FY24 | **~1,341** | ~810 | **Well Above Normal** — heavy provisioning year |
| FY25 | **~1,793** | ~1,190 | **Well Above Normal** — bank is OVER-provisioning (conservative) |

**Critical Finding:** Contrary to initial assumptions, AU Bank has been provisioning ABOVE normalised levels in FY22-FY25. FY25's actual credit cost of ~₹1,793 Cr significantly exceeds the normalised ~₹1,190 Cr. This means reported earnings are CONSERVATIVELY stated — the true sustainable earnings power is HIGHER than reported.

**Implication:** The reported EPS of ~₹28 (FY25) is depressed by above-normal provisioning. Normalised owner earnings EPS is ~₹38.6. On normalised earnings, the PE drops from ~31x to ~25x — still not cheap, but meaningfully more reasonable. This confirms that AU's management is building credit buffers, not inflating earnings.

> *Buffett on owner earnings: "The figures in financial statements are the beginning, not the end, of business valuation."* For banks, normalising for credit costs is the most important adjustment — and in AU's case, the normalisation reveals a CONSERVATIVE management that is building buffers, not one inflating earnings.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: NARROW BUT WIDENING — Underwriting Skill + Regulatory Moat**

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits — 🟡 WEAK BUT IMPROVING**
CASA at 29% is LOW by banking standards (HDFC Bank: 38%, Kotak: 50%+). This means AU pays more for its deposits than large private banks. However, CASA + Retail TD at 65% provides reasonable funding stability. The universal bank transition will help — AU will be able to access cheaper wholesale deposits and corporate current accounts. This is the single biggest moat weakness.

**2. Distribution & Branch Network — ✅ STRONG**
2,726 touchpoints across 21 states with 59,872+ employees. Deep penetration in Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, and MP — semi-urban/rural areas that large banks find uneconomical to serve. This physical presence took 30 years to build and is very difficult to replicate.

**3. Underwriting Skill — ✅ STRONG**
30+ years of experience in vehicle finance and MSME lending. Track record of maintaining GNPA below 2% (pre-Fincare) through multiple cycles. The ability to lend to unbanked/underbanked segments at 12-18% yields while maintaining reasonable asset quality is a genuine skill moat.

**4. Technology & Digital — 🟡 MODERATE**
AU has invested in digital infrastructure (Video KYC, digital savings accounts, QR-based collections), but is not a technology leader. Digital is an enabler, not a differentiator.

**5. Regulatory Moat — ✅ STRONG AND WIDENING**
The RBI's in-principle approval for Universal Bank transition (first SFB ever) is a massive regulatory moat. This creates a multi-year first-mover advantage — competitors (Ujjivan, Jana) will need to wait years. As a Universal Bank, AU can: lend to larger corporates, offer full forex services, reduce priority sector lending from 60% to 40%, and access cheaper funds.

**6. Fintech Disruption Risk — 🟡 MODERATE**
UPI and digital lenders could erode transaction fee income. However, AU's core business (vehicle loans, MSME secured lending) requires physical presence and underwriting skill that fintechs cannot easily replicate. Gold loans and housing loans are inherently resistant to digital disruption.

### Susceptibility to Change

AU's core lending business (vehicles, MSME, housing) has NOT fundamentally changed in 30 years. People buy vehicles on credit, businesses need working capital, individuals need housing loans. The mechanics of underwriting, disbursement, and collection are incremental, not revolutionary. This is exactly the kind of "absence of change" Buffett looks for.

> *Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* AU's retail lending model has been stable for decades. The universal bank transition ADDS optionality without fundamentally changing what works.

### Key Competitors

| Competitor | Threat Level | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank** | Manageable | Focused on urban/metro; unlikely to aggressively enter AU's semi-urban turf |
| **Bajaj Finance** | Moderate | Strong in consumer finance; overlaps in some segments but different distribution |
| **Ujjivan, Jana SFBs** | Manageable | Smaller, less diversified; lack universal bank pathway |
| **Bandhan Bank** | Moderate | Similar SFB-turned-bank; but MFI-heavy and governance concerns |
| **Digital Lenders (Fintechs)** | Low-Moderate | Cannot replicate physical underwriting in AU's segments |

**Moat Verdict: NARROW but WIDENING.** The universal bank transition is the key catalyst that could transform AU from a niche SFB into a mainstream private bank. If it executes well, the moat widens significantly. If it stumbles (asset quality, integration), the narrow moat could erode.

### Fair P/B Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Where:
  ROE = 14% (sustainable, current level)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 14% × 96% = 13.4%
  CoE = 11% (investor's required return for a mid-cap bank)
```

**Problem:** When g > CoE, the Gordon Growth Model breaks down mathematically. This happens because AU's sustainable growth rate (13.4%) exceeds the assumed cost of equity (11%). In reality, this high growth rate cannot be sustained indefinitely.

**Using a long-term sustainable ROE of 15% and terminal growth of 8%:**
```
Fair P/B = (15% - 8%) / (11% - 8%) = 7% / 3% = 2.33x
```

**Using current ROE of 14% and terminal growth of 7%:**
```
Fair P/B = (14% - 7%) / (11% - 7%) = 7% / 4% = 1.75x
```

**Realistic Fair P/B Range: 2.0-2.5x Book**

**Current P/B: 3.9x — SIGNIFICANTLY ABOVE FAIR VALUE**
