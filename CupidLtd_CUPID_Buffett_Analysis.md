## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### What Does Cupid Do?

Cupid Limited, established in 1993 and headquartered in Nashik, Maharashtra, is India's premier manufacturer of male and female condoms, water-based lubricant jellies, and In Vitro Diagnostic (IVD) kits. The company exports to 110+ countries and generates approximately 90% of its revenue from international markets — predominantly through institutional B2B channels (government tenders, UNFPA, WHO procurement programmes, and global health agencies).

### Would Buffett Understand It?

**Absolutely.** This is a simple manufacturing business that makes an essential, recession-proof product. People need condoms regardless of the economic cycle. The business model is straightforward: buy raw materials (natural rubber latex), manufacture condoms/lubricants/IVD kits in a modern facility near Nashik, and sell them globally — primarily to institutional buyers (governments, UN agencies, NGOs) and increasingly through branded FMCG channels domestically.

### Business Model Type

Cupid is a **hybrid toll-bridge + switching-cost business**:

1. **Toll Bridge (Institutional/B2B — ~90% of revenue):** Cupid holds WHO/UNFPA pre-qualification for BOTH male and female condoms — making it the only company in the world with this dual pre-qualification. This is a regulatory moat. Governments and global health agencies procuring condoms at scale have a very limited pool of qualified suppliers. Cupid sits on the toll road between global health funding and actual product delivery.

2. **Emerging Consumer Franchise (India FMCG — growing):** The company is building a branded FMCG portfolio (deodorants, perfumes, hair oils, petroleum jelly, personal care) for the Indian consumer market. This is early-stage and not yet a franchise.

> *Buffett: "I try to buy stock in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."*

Cupid's B2B business has a structural advantage — WHO/UNFPA certification is difficult to obtain and takes years. But it's not fully idiot-proof: it depends on tender cycles, government procurement timelines, and geopolitical relationships. The consumer FMCG push is much earlier stage and unproven.

### Trend Narrative

The income statement reveals a **highly cyclical business masquerading as FMCG**. Revenue and profits swing dramatically year to year — this is NOT the steady, predictable earnings stream Buffett loves. Key observations:

- **Revenue has been lumpy:** The 5-year sales CAGR is a mere 2.7% (FY2020-FY2025). However, FY2020 was an outlier year (COVID-driven demand surge). The more recent TTM run-rate of ₹320 Cr is a dramatic acceleration.
- **Margins are volatile:** OPM has swung from 17% (FY2022 trough) to 41% (FY2016-17 peak). This is characteristic of a tender-driven B2B business where product mix and order timing drive margins.
- **The current acceleration is real but young:** 9M FY26 shows ₹259 Cr revenue (annualising to ₹345+ Cr) with 35% OPM. Q3 FY26 alone was ₹104 Cr revenue with 43% OPM — the best quarter ever.

- **Shares have increased:** Equity capital went from ₹13 Cr to ₹27 Cr (FY2025) due to the 1:1 bonus, and now to ₹134.5 Cr post the 4:1 bonus. While bonuses don't dilute, any future equity issuance at these elevated prices would be accretive — and management has already issued 4.65 lakh shares under ESOP in Jan 2026.

> *Buffett: "The key to investing is not assessing how much an industry is going to affect society, or how much it will grow, but rather determining the competitive advantage of any given company and, above all, the durability of that advantage."*

The profitability trend is encouraging in FY26 but the historical pattern warns against extrapolating one great year into perpetuity.

### Financial Fortress Assessment

**The Good:**
- **Virtually debt-free.** Debt/Equity has never exceeded 0.30x (FY2020 was an anomaly with lease/short-term borrowings). The company is essentially financed entirely by equity. This is a Buffett positive.
- **Historically strong cash position.** The company had ₹147 Cr in investments as of FY2024 — almost half its total assets.

- **"Other Assets" have ballooned alarmingly:** From ₹114 Cr (FY2024) to ₹196 Cr (FY2025) to ₹353 Cr (Sep 2025). This includes trade receivables and inventory. - **This is a RED FLAG.** A company reporting record profits but whose balance sheet is bloating with receivables and inventory either has (a) genuinely large orders in pipeline awaiting shipment/payment, or (b) is booking revenue aggressively or facing collection issues. Given that institutional buyers (governments, UN agencies) are typically slow payers, scenario (a) is plausible — but the magnitude of the deterioration demands scrutiny.

> *Buffett: "Only when the tide goes out do you discover who's been swimming naked."*

The balance sheet was a fortress historically but is now deploying capital aggressively. The working capital deterioration needs to reverse for earnings quality to be confirmed.

### Maintenance vs. Growth CapEx Estimate

Cupid's manufacturing facility near Nashik has been operational since 1997 with periodic upgrades. The current capacity is 480M male condoms + 52M female condoms + 210M lubricant sachets per year. The Palava (Maharashtra) expansion will increase capacity by 1.5x — to 1.25 billion male condoms and 125M female condoms annually. A Saudi Arabia FMCG facility is also planned for 2027.

**Estimated Maintenance CapEx:** ₹5-8 Cr per year (roughly equal to depreciation of ₹3-4 Cr plus incremental maintenance). This is a capital-light manufacturing business in steady state.

**Everything above maintenance is Growth CapEx:** The ₹80 Cr (FY2024) and ₹45 Cr (FY2025) spend is almost entirely growth-related — land acquisition, new facility construction, and capacity expansion.

```

If we believe the growth investments will generate adequate returns (the Palava plant expanding capacity 2.5x), then the Owner Earnings figure is the right anchor for valuation. But if the capex runs over budget or the capacity remains under-utilised, those "growth investments" become sunk costs.

### Analysis

Returns on capital have **deteriorated significantly** from the FY2016-FY2017 peak (ROCE 72-79%, ROE 42-50%) to the FY2025 levels (ROCE 17%, ROE 13%). This decline reflects:

1. **A larger equity base** due to retained earnings and bonus issues — the denominator has grown faster than profits.
2. **Lumpy earnings** — FY2022 and FY2025 were weaker profit years relative to the capital employed.
3. **Capital deployed in expansion** — Fixed assets + CWIP have grown from ₹16 Cr to ₹73 Cr, but the new Palava plant is not yet operational.

The current ROE of 13% (FY2025) is mediocre. However, the TTM ROE (₹84 Cr profit / ~₹370 Cr average equity) is approximately 23% — much more respectable. The question is whether the TTM run-rate is sustainable.

> *Buffett: "The ideal business is one that earns very high returns on capital and that keeps using lots of capital at those high returns."*

Cupid has historically earned very high returns on capital (20%+ average over a decade), but these returns are cyclical, not steady. The current FY26 acceleration, if sustained, would restore ROE to the 20-25% range — good but not exceptional by Buffett standards.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Regulatory + Niche Certification Moat (Narrow)**

#### Moat Sources:

1. **WHO/UNFPA Dual Pre-Qualification (STRONG):** Cupid is the ONLY company globally to hold WHO/UNFPA pre-qualification for both male AND female condoms. This is a genuine, quantifiable moat. The pre-qualification process takes years, requires rigorous quality audits, and acts as a significant barrier to entry for institutional procurement. Global health agencies (UNFPA, USAID, DFID) can only procure from pre-qualified suppliers — Cupid has a seat at a very exclusive table.

2. **Female Condom Niche (MODERATE):** The global female condom market has very few manufacturers. Cupid is a leader alongside Veru (US) and HLL Lifecare (India). The company is now entering the premium nitrile (NBR) female condom segment, where there is currently a single global manufacturer — making Cupid a potential second source, which procurement agencies desperately want for supply chain diversification.

3. **Cost Advantage (MODERATE):** Manufacturing in Nashik, India provides a structural cost advantage over competitors in developed markets. India has access to natural rubber and lower labour costs.

4. **Scale & Capacity (GROWING):** Post-Palava expansion, Cupid will have capacity for 1.25 billion male condoms and 125 million female condoms — making it one of the largest condom manufacturers globally. Scale begets cost advantage.

#### Moat Weaknesses:

1. **Tender-Dependent Revenue:** ~90% of revenue comes from institutional B2B contracts. Tender cycles are lumpy, unpredictable, and subject to government budget allocations and geopolitical dynamics. A single large tender delay can destroy a quarter's numbers.

2. **Limited Brand Moat (Domestic):** In India, Cupid competes against entrenched giants — Mankind Pharma (Manforce), Reckitt (Durex), HLL Lifecare (Moods), TTK Healthcare (Skore). These competitors have vastly superior brand recognition, distribution networks, and marketing budgets. Cupid's domestic FMCG push is embryonic.

3. **Commodity Manufacturing Risk:** At its core, condom manufacturing is not highly differentiated. The raw material (latex) is a commodity. Quality certifications provide a moat, but the underlying product is fungible across certified manufacturers.

4. **Competitor Threat — Karex Berhad (Malaysia):** Karex is the world's largest condom manufacturer by volume. They are a direct competitor for institutional tenders. HLL Lifecare (government-owned, 2 billion annual capacity) dwarfs Cupid domestically.

#### Is the Business Susceptible to Change?

This is actually a Buffett POSITIVE. The condom industry has not fundamentally changed in decades. The basic product, manufacturing process, and distribution channels are stable. There is no technological disruption on the horizon that would make condoms obsolete (unlike, say, newspapers or retail). Population growth, STI prevention, and family planning ensure long-term demand.

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change. We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."*

The condom industry is remarkably stable — but Cupid's POSITION within it is what fluctuates (tender wins, capacity utilisation, product mix).

**Moat Verdict: NARROW — Defensible but not Wide.** The WHO/UNFPA certification is a genuine moat, but the business is too dependent on tender cycles and lacks a strong consumer brand moat. The female condom niche and NBR expansion could widen the moat over time.

> *Buffett: "When management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."*

Cupid's management appears competent and aligned (high promoter holding, personal open-market purchases). But the aggressive revenue guidance (₹335 Cr+), combined with the working capital deterioration, requires close monitoring. The investment in GII Healthcare Fund is a minor yellow flag — it suggests available cash was deployed outside the core business.

### Key Observations

**The working capital deterioration is alarming.** The cash conversion cycle has ballooned from 183 days (FY2023) to 570 days (FY2025). 2. **Pre-positioning inventory** for the H2 FY26 demand surge (the record order book)
3. **Aggressive revenue recognition** — the least charitable interpretation

Management must address this in upcoming quarters. If debtor days and inventory days normalise as FY26 revenue is delivered and collected, the concern dissipates. If they persist, it's a structural problem.
