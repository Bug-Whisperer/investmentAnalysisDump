## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

SPML Infra Limited, incorporated in 1981, is an infrastructure development company with over four decades of experience and 700+ executed projects across India. The company operates primarily as an EPC contractor in:

- **Water Supply & Treatment** (core business — ~70% of order book): Bulk water transmission, water treatment plants, pipeline projects, distribution networks, sewage treatment, 24×7 water supply schemes under Jal Jeevan Mission and AMRUT 2.0.
- **Power Transmission & Distribution:** Substations (220 kV, 400 kV GIS), rural electrification, power augmentation.
- **Waste Management:** Municipal solid waste collection, segregation, transportation.
- **Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS):** New strategic pivot via partnership with Energy Vault (USA) for localised manufacturing of B-Vault storage units.

**Buffett Classification: Government-Dependent EPC Contractor / Commodity Business**

This is NOT a toll bridge, a consumer franchise, or a switching-cost business. It is a project-based EPC contractor — fundamentally a **commodity business** where the client (almost exclusively government departments and municipal corporations) awards contracts largely on the basis of the lowest qualifying bid (L1). There is minimal pricing power, minimal recurring revenue (except O&M contracts), and intense dependence on government capex cycles.

> *Buffett: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."*

EPC contracting is a business with structurally difficult economics: lumpy revenues, working capital intensity, execution risk, dependence on government payment cycles, and thin margins. SPML has historically demonstrated these exact characteristics — with revenues swinging wildly from ₹2,276 Cr (FY17) to ₹683 Cr (FY21) and back to ₹771 Cr (FY25), and net margins ranging from deeply negative to single-digit positive.

**Would Buffett understand it?** Yes — it's a simple business (build water/power infrastructure for the government). **Would he buy it?** Almost certainly not in its current form — it lacks durable competitive advantages, predictable earnings, and the margins are razor-thin. However, the turnaround narrative and the "SPML 2.0" story deserve examination.

## 5. CAPEX QUALITY ANALYSIS — Growth vs. Maintenance CapEx

SPML Infra is an **asset-light EPC contractor** — it owns virtually no physical assets (fixed assets = ₹6 Cr in FY25). Traditional CapEx analysis is less relevant here. The company's "capital" is its working capital (receivables, mobilisation advances, retention money locked in projects).

| Metric | FY20 | FY21 | FY22 | FY23 | FY24 | FY25 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | 1,706 | 683 | 952 | 883 | 1,319 | 771 |
| Net Income | 7 | -117 | 0 | 0 | -2 | 48 |
| D&A | 11 | 6 | 4 | 3 | 2 | 1 |
| Estimated Maint. CapEx | ~5 | ~3 | ~3 | ~2 | ~1 | ~1 |
| **Owner Earnings** | ~13 | **~-114** | ~1 | ~1 | ~-1 | ~48 |
| SBC | Negligible | Negligible | Negligible | Negligible | Negligible | Negligible |

**Owner Earnings = Net Income + D&A - Maintenance CapEx**

Because fixed assets are near-zero, maintenance CapEx is trivially small. **Owner Earnings ≈ Net Income** for SPML. The issue is not CapEx quality — it's that Owner Earnings have been near-zero or negative for most of the past decade.

The REAL "maintenance cost" for an EPC business is **working capital** — the cash consumed by the receivables cycle. With 187 debtor days, SPML is effectively providing interest-free financing to the government for 6+ months on every project. This hidden cost doesn't show up in the income statement but devastates cash flow.

> *Buffett on Owner Earnings: "The value of a business is the cash that can be taken out of it during its remaining life."* By this standard, SPML has generated negligible cumulative Owner Earnings over the past decade.

3. **BESS entry** is a high-risk pivot into an entirely new domain where SPML has zero track record.
4. **Technology disruption** is not a major risk for physical water infrastructure, but digital disruption in project management, smart water networks, and IoT-enabled systems is changing the competitive landscape.

> *Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* SPML is the opposite — it's a company undergoing significant change (SPML 2.0, BESS pivot, debt restructuring) in a changing industry. This is not Buffett territory.

**Honest Verdict: The moat is weak and not widening.** SPML competes in a commoditised EPC space where contracts go to the lowest bidder. Its competitive advantages are limited to project pre-qualification experience and government relationships, both of which can be replicated by competitors. The BESS pivot introduces new risk rather than strengthening the moat.
