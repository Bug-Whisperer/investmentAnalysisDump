## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

**Type:** Old-generation Private Sector Bank (Scheduled Commercial Bank)
**Founded:** 1929, Thrissur, Kerala — the first scheduled bank among private banks in Kerala.
**Core Business:** Retail and corporate banking, treasury operations, forex business, para-banking (debit cards, third-party distribution).

**Branch Network:** 948 branches and 1,315 ATMs across India. Majority located in semi-urban areas (460 branches). Heavy concentration in Kerala (53% of customer touchpoints) with South ex-Kerala at 30% and Rest of India at 17%.

**Customer Base:** 75 lakh (7.5 million) customers.

**Loan Book Composition (9MFY24):**
- Corporate Loans: 38%
- Personal Loans: 23% (Housing 29%, Gold 19%, Personal Loan 12%, Mortgage 11%, Vehicle 9%, LAD 8%, Credit Card 8%, Others 4%)
- Business Loans: 20%
- Agriculture: 19%
- 96% A & Above rated Large Corporate Loans

**Deposit Mix (9MFY24):**
- Retail Term: 64%
- Savings: 26%
- Current: ~6%
- Bulk: 4%

**Yield & Cost (Q3FY24):**
- Yield on Funds: 7.53%
- Cost of Funds: 4.71%
- Yield on Advances: 9.28%
- Cost of Deposits: 5.18%

**Would Buffett Understand It?** Absolutely. A bank takes deposits, lends them out at a higher rate, and keeps the spread. It is the oldest and simplest financial business in the world.

**Core Economic Engine:** This is primarily a NIM-driven (spread income) bank with a growing fee income component. The bank earns money by borrowing cheap (deposits) and lending dear (advances). The primary moat question for SIB is whether its deposit franchise — rooted in Kerala — can generate cheap, sticky funding at scale.

Buffett: *"Banking is a very good business if you don't do anything dumb."* The question for South Indian Bank is whether the decade of "dumb" (FY18-FY22 NPA crisis) is truly behind it, and whether the bank has emerged as a genuinely better institution.

**Key Context:** SIB is an old-generation private bank that went through a severe asset quality crisis from ~FY18-FY22, where GNPA spiked above 5% and net profits collapsed to near-zero (₹45 Cr in FY22). The bank has since undergone a dramatic turnaround — GNPA has fallen from 5.48% (Dec 2022) to 2.67% (Dec 2025), and profits have surged from ₹45 Cr (FY22) to ₹1,303 Cr (FY25). This is a classic TURNAROUND story — the critical question is whether the turnaround is durable or temporary.

**Trend Narrative:**

This is a tale of two eras:

**Era 1 — The Crisis (FY18-FY22):** Net profit collapsed from ₹392 Cr (FY17) to just ₹45 Cr (FY22). ROE crashed from 9% to 1%. ROA fell to 0.05%. The bank was essentially earning nothing — all operating income was being consumed by provisions for bad loans. EPS hit a nadir of ₹0.17.

**Era 2 — The Turnaround (FY23-Present):** A dramatic recovery. Net profit surged from ₹45 Cr to ₹1,303 Cr in three years. ROE recovered from 1% to 14%. ROA climbed from 0.05% to 1.08%. EPS went from ₹0.17 to ₹4.98 (FY25) and TTM ₹5.31.

**Critical Question:** Is this earnings recovery driven by genuine operating improvement or simply by lower provisions (which is unsustainable if the credit cycle turns)?

The answer is BOTH. GNPA has improved from 5.48% to 2.67% — this is genuine asset quality improvement that reduces provision requirements. But the Other Income component has also grown significantly (₹1,813 Cr in FY25 vs ₹813 Cr in FY23) — some of this may include one-time recovery income from written-off accounts. Revenue growth itself has been modest (5-year sales CAGR of just 4%) — the profit surge is almost entirely driven by lower provisions and some margin improvement, NOT by top-line growth. This is the hallmark of a recovery play, not a growth story.

Buffett: *"When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* SIB's reputation for bad asset quality (FY18-FY22) is being overcome by improved management — but the turnaround must prove itself through an entire credit cycle before it can be trusted.

## 5. EARNINGS QUALITY & PROVISION ADEQUACY ANALYSIS (BANK/NBFC MODE)

### Normalised Earnings ("Bank Owner Earnings")

| Component | FY22 | FY23 | FY24 | FY25 | TTM |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Income (₹ Cr) | 45 | 775 | 1,070 | 1,303 | 1,390 |
| Add: D&A (₹ Cr) | 84 | 87 | 90 | 102 | ~100 |
| Less: Normalised Credit Cost (est. 1.5% of avg advances) | ~1,100 | ~1,200 | ~1,300 | ~1,450 | ~1,500 |
| Less: SBC (est.) | ~10 | ~10 | ~10 | ~10 | ~10 |
| **= Normalised Earnings (₹ Cr)** | **-981** | **-348** | **-150** | **-55** | **-20** |

**Wait — this produces NEGATIVE normalised earnings?** Let me explain. The 5-year average credit cost for SIB during FY18-FY22 was extremely high (reflecting the NPA crisis). Using that as "normalised" would unfairly penalise the bank — it would imply the bank can NEVER earn positive normalised profits, which is clearly wrong if asset quality has genuinely improved.

**Better approach:** Use FORWARD-LOOKING normalised credit cost. If SIB sustains GNPA at ~3% and PCR at ~80%, a normalised credit cost of 0.5-0.8% of advances is more appropriate (in line with industry averages for banks with ~3% GNPA). Let me recalculate:

| Component | FY25 | TTM |
|---|---|---|
| Net Income (₹ Cr) | 1,303 | 1,390 |
| Add: D&A (₹ Cr) | 102 | ~100 |
| Less: Forward Normalised Credit Cost (0.7% of ~₹85,000 Cr avg advances) | ~595 | ~620 |
| Less: SBC (est.) | ~10 | ~10 |
| **= Normalised Earnings (₹ Cr)** | **~800** | **~860** |
| **Normalised EPS (₹)** | **~3.05** | **~3.28** |

**Analysis:**
- **Actual credit cost in FY25** was significantly LOWER than historical normalised levels — the bank is in a benign credit environment where recoveries from previously written-off accounts are boosting profits.
- **Current reported earnings (₹1,303 Cr) are HIGHER than normalised earnings (~₹800 Cr)** — this means about ₹500 Cr of current profits come from provisioning being BELOW normalised levels or recoveries. This is unsustainable if the credit cycle turns.
- **PCR trend** is RISING (from ~42% to ~83%) — this is conservative and builds buffer. Rising PCR = the bank IS building reserves, not releasing them. This is a positive sign.
- The gap between reported and normalised earnings will narrow over time as the NPA recovery tailwind fades. Investors should use normalised EPS of ~₹3.05-3.28 (not reported EPS of ₹4.98-5.31) for valuation to be conservative.

### ROA-to-ROE Bridge (The Quality Check)

**FY25:** ROE 14% = ROA 1.08% × Leverage 12.3x ≈ 13.3% (close to reported 14%)

**This is QUALITY ROE.** The ROE of 14% is driven by an ROA of 1.08% with MODERATE leverage of 12.3x. Compare this to:
- A bank with 14% ROE from 0.7% ROA × 20x leverage = DANGEROUS
- SIB with 14% ROE from 1.08% ROA × 12.3x leverage = QUALITY

The leverage has actually DECREASED while ROE has increased — this is the gold standard. ROE is improving because the BUSINESS is getting better (higher ROA), not because the bank is taking more risk (higher leverage).

### Quality Tier Assessment

| Quality Tier | ROA | ROE | Deserved P/B |
|---|---|---|---|
| Elite | >2.0% | >18% | 3.0-4.0x |
| Excellent | 1.5-2.0% | 15-18% | 2.5-3.5x |
| **Good** | **1.0-1.5%** | **12-15%** | **1.5-2.5x** |
| Mediocre | 0.5-1.0% | 8-12% | 0.8-1.5x |
| Poor | <0.5% | <8% | <1.0x |

**SIB currently sits in the "Good" tier** with ROA of 1.08% and ROE of 14%. The "deserved" P/B for this tier is 1.5-2.5x. The stock trades at 0.90x — a SIGNIFICANT discount to its quality-tier-implied multiple.

**HOWEVER — the 5-year average ROA is only 0.58%, which is "Mediocre" tier.** The question is whether the current 1.08% ROA is the new normal or a cyclical peak. If ROA reverts to 0.7-0.8% (which would happen if credit costs normalise), ROE would drop to ~9-10%, and the deserved P/B would be 0.8-1.5x — close to where it currently trades.

Buffett: *"A great bank earns high ROE primarily through high ROA (not excessive leverage), maintains this through credit cycles, and can reinvest retained earnings at the same high ROE to compound book value."* SIB earns ROE through improving ROA with declining leverage — the right formula. The unanswered question is sustainability through the next credit cycle.

### Fair P/B Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)
Where:
  ROE = 12% (conservative sustainable — below current 14% to account for credit cycle normalisation)
  g   = ROE × Retention = 12% × 92% = 11.0%
  CoE = 12% (higher than 10-11% for a small bank with higher risk)

Fair P/B = (0.12 - 0.11) / (0.12 - 0.11) = 0.01 / 0.01 = 1.0x
```

At 12% sustainable ROE, the Fair P/B is approximately **1.0x Book Value.**

If ROE sustains at 14%:
```
g = 14% × 92% = 12.9%
Fair P/B = (0.14 - 0.129) / (0.12 - 0.129)
```
Note: g (12.9%) > CoE (12%), so the Gordon model breaks down — implying the fair value is theoretically very high. This happens when ROE significantly exceeds cost of equity AND the retention is high. Practically, for a bank with SIB's risk profile, a Fair P/B of 1.0-1.5x is reasonable.

### Buffett's Bank Valuation Rules of Thumb

| ROE | Deserved P/B | SIB's Current P/B (0.90x) |
|---|---|---|
| ROE ≈ 11% (= CoE) | ~1.0x Book | Slight discount — reasonable |
| ROE = 13-14% | 1.5-2.0x Book | **Significant discount — cheap** |
| ROE = 15-16% | 2.0-3.0x Book | Very significant discount |

**At 0.90x P/B with 14% ROE, SIB is trading at a meaningful discount to its quality-tier-implied P/B of 1.5-2.5x.** If ROE is sustainable, this is a bargain. If ROE reverts to 9-10% (as the provision tailwind fades), then 0.90x is fair.

This is the central tension of the investment thesis: **Are you buying a 14% ROE bank at 0.9x book (a steal), or are you buying a 9% ROE bank at 0.9x book (fair value)?**
