## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

### Business Model

International Conveyors Ltd (ICL), incorporated in 1973, is India's **only listed player** in PVC Conveyor Belting — a critical consumable used in underground and above-ground mining and bulk material transportation (coal, potash, salt, cement, fertiliser). The company manufactures fire-resistant, antistatic solid woven conveyor belts (Type-3 to Type-12) at two plants in Aurangabad with combined installed capacity of ~11.25 Lac meters per annum.

**Revenue Breakdown (FY24):**
- PVC Fire-Resistant Antistatic Solid Woven Coal Conveyor Belting: ~62%
- Fittings and accessories: ~27%
- Trading (steel cord conveyor belts and fasteners): ~11%
- Wind power generation (5 wind turbine generators): minor

**Geographical Split (FY24):** Exports ~62% (primarily USA, Canada) | Domestic ~38%
**Industry Split (FY24):** Coal ~85% | Others ~15%
**Client Concentration (FY24):** 6 customers account for ~71% of revenue

**Would Buffett understand it?** Absolutely. This is a simple, tangible business — the company makes rubber-and-PVC belts that move coal and minerals on conveyor systems. It is a **consumable** product (belts wear out and must be replaced), giving it recurring demand characteristics. It is closer to a **toll bridge / approved supplier** model than a commodity business because of the dual-stage approval process required to become an approved supplier, long-term contracts, and safety/regulatory requirements in underground mining.

> *Buffett: "I try to invest in businesses that are so wonderful that an idiot can run them. Because sooner or later, one will."*

The business is simple to understand, within the circle of competence for any industrial investor, and has niche characteristics that Buffett would appreciate — it is a small, unglamorous, essential consumable business with high entry barriers.

However, there is a **significant caveat**: the company has a large treasury/investment segment that generates substantial "other income" from equity investments, mutual funds, and inter-corporate deposits. In FY25, other income (₹105 Cr) was **larger than operating revenue (₹152 Cr)**. This makes ICL part industrial manufacturer, part investment holding company — a complexity Buffett might frown upon.

### Trend Narrative

**Revenue story is deeply cyclical and volatile.** Sales peaked at ₹215 Cr in FY23, collapsed 38% to ₹134 Cr in FY24, and partially recovered to ₹152 Cr in FY25. The 10-year sales CAGR is a mere 5%, and 3-year CAGR is actually -10%. This is NOT a secular growth story on the top line.

**Operating margins have improved structurally** from the disastrous FY16-FY19 period (negative to breakeven) to a much healthier 14-19% range in FY23-FY25/TTM. This suggests the company has gained some pricing power or moved up the value chain.

**The elephant in the room is "Other Income."** In FY24, other income was ₹67 Cr against ₹134 Cr in sales. In FY25, it ballooned to ₹105 Cr against ₹152 Cr in sales. Stripping out other income and adjusting for tax, core operating earnings are far more modest.

**EPS growth has been extraordinary** (from -₹1.10 in FY18 to ₹14.47 in FY25), but this is driven almost entirely by the investment portfolio, not by the core manufacturing business scaling up.

> *Buffett: "Earnings can be as pliable as putty when a charlatan heads the company reporting them."* While there's no suggestion of charlatan behaviour here, the blending of manufacturing and treasury income makes it essential to separate the two.

The factory asset is almost fully depreciated and still producing goods — that IS capital-light. But the growth story is absent.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Assessment

**Moat Type: Niche Monopoly / Approval-Based Switching Costs (Narrow)**

ICL claims to be India's **only listed player in PVC Conveyor Belting**. The moat sources are:

1. **Dual-Stage Approval Process:** Becoming an approved supplier for mining companies (especially for underground coal mining) requires rigorous safety testing and certification. This creates real switching costs — mines cannot simply switch to an unapproved belt.

2. **Long-Term Contracts:** The company operates on long-term supply agreements with major mining companies globally (Nutrien, Mosaic, Glencore, Peabody, Coal India affiliates).

3. **Safety/Regulatory Requirements:** Underground mining belts must be fire-resistant and antistatic — failure can be catastrophic. This creates high product specifications and quality barriers.

4. **Installed Base / Replacement Cycle:** Conveyor belts are consumables that wear out. Once a mine installs a system using ICL's belts, replacement belts tend to come from the same supplier (compatibility, testing history).

### Moat Durability Assessment

| Factor | Assessment | Rating |
|---|---|---|
| Brand / Reputation | Established in niche; 50+ years | Moderate |
| Switching Costs | High for approved suppliers | **Strong** |
| Cost Advantage | India-based manufacturing | Moderate |
| Network Effects | None | None |
| Regulatory Barrier | Approval process | Moderate |
| Overall Moat | **Narrow but real** | 6/10 |

### Competitive Threats

**Key Competitors:** Fenner (now Michelin group), Continental AG, Bridgestone, Yokohama, and various Chinese manufacturers. These are all MUCH larger than ICL.

- **Existential Threat:** Chinese PVC belt manufacturers offering lower prices. However, the approval process and quality requirements for underground mining provide some protection.
- **Manageable Threat:** Large global players (Fenner/Michelin, Continental) compete on quality/brand but operate at a much larger scale and focus on different segments (steel cord, textile belts).
- **Technological Disruption:** Low risk. Conveyor belt technology is mature and evolving slowly. There is no "Uber for conveyor belts."

### Susceptibility to Change

**This business is remarkably resistant to change** — and that's a good thing from Buffett's perspective. Conveyor belts have been used in mining for over a century. The basic technology (PVC woven fabric coated with rubber/PVC compounds) has not fundamentally changed. Underground coal mining continues globally. The product will look essentially the same in 10-20 years.

> *Buffett: "We do not get enthused about change... we're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money and even more in the future."*

The risk is NOT that the product becomes obsolete. The risk is that coal mining itself declines due to the energy transition. With 85% of revenue tied to the coal industry, ICL is fundamentally a **bet on the longevity of coal mining**, particularly underground coal mining.

**Honest Moat Verdict:** The moat is **narrow and industry-dependent**. Within the niche of underground PVC conveyor belts, ICL has real competitive advantages. But the overall market is small, the customer base is concentrated (6 customers = 71% of revenue), and the business is hostage to coal mining activity. If coal mining declines materially, no moat will save the business.

## 20. TEST OF SIMPLICITY — Can a 10-Year-Old Understand This?

### The 10-Year-Old Test

**"This company makes big rubber belts that carry coal and rocks in mines. They're the only company in India listed on the stock market that makes this type of belt. Their belts have to be really safe because they go underground where there could be fires. Because of this, it's hard for other companies to compete with them.**

**But here's the thing — this company also uses a lot of its money to buy other companies' stocks, like a mini stock market investor. Actually, they make MORE money from buying stocks than from selling belts! So you're kind of buying two things: a belt factory and a stock portfolio."**

### Does the Math Work on a Napkin?

- Market Cap: ₹465 Cr
- Investment Portfolio: ₹299 Cr
- **You're paying ₹166 Cr for the belt factory**
- Factory generates ~₹15-24 Cr/year in core profits
- That's a 9-14% yield on your ₹166 Cr — reasonable

**Yes, the napkin math is simple and compelling.** You're paying about ₹166 Cr for a factory that makes ₹15-24 Cr/year, and you get a ₹299 Cr stock portfolio thrown in. The risk is that the stock portfolio can go down and the coal business can shrink.

> *Buffett: "If you need to use a computer or a calculator to make the calculation, you shouldn't buy it."*

The napkin math works. But the uncertainty around the investment portfolio and coal demand keeps this from being a no-brainer. A no-brainer would be if the stock traded at ₹45-50 (0.75x book or less) — then you'd be getting both the factory AND the portfolio at a meaningful discount.

ICL has a narrow moat in a niche industry. The moat is real but not wide. The reward is moderate. For a small capital base investor willing to be patient and contrarian, this is a decent position at the right price — ₹55-60 or below. At ₹72, it's a hold, not a buy.
