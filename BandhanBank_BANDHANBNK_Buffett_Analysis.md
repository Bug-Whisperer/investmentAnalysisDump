## 1. THE BUSINESS — Understanding the Economic Engine

**Buffett: "Never invest in a business you cannot understand."**

Bandhan Bank is a microfinance-turned-universal bank, headquartered in Kolkata. It started as an NGO in 2001, became an NBFC-MFI in 2006, received a universal banking license from the RBI in 2015, and listed on exchanges in March 2018. It acquired housing finance company GRUH Finance in October 2019 via a share-swap deal.

**Type of Financial Institution:** Universal commercial bank with deep microfinance roots. The bank's core business model is lending to the underbanked — primarily women borrowers in rural and semi-urban India — through its Emerging Entrepreneurs Business (EEB) segment, while progressively diversifying into secured retail (housing, vehicle, personal, gold loans), SME, and commercial banking.

**The Economic Engine:**

The business is primarily **NIM-driven (spread income)**. Bandhan earns a wide spread because its microfinance loans carry yields of 17-20%+, far above its blended cost of funds (~6-7%). This microfinance-heavy yield is the source of the bank's historically high NIMs (~7-8%), among the highest in the Indian banking sector.

**The Moat Question:**

Bandhan's moat WAS its unmatched rural distribution (6,300+ banking outlets), deep relationships with 3+ crore women borrowers, and the difficulty of replicating this grassroots network. However, this moat has been **eroding** due to: rising competition from other SFBs, fintechs, and MFI-NBFCs; persistent asset quality problems in the microfinance book; and declining CASA ratio which weakens the funding moat.

**Buffett's Circle of Competence Test:** A microfinance-focused bank serving rural India's poorest is NOT a simple business model Buffett would naturally gravitate toward. The credit risk of unsecured micro-loans to vulnerable populations, the geographic concentration (historically heavy in East India/Bengal), and the cyclical devastation caused by events like demonetisation, Assam MFI crisis, COVID, and cyclones make this a volatile, hard-to-predict business. **Buffett would likely struggle with the unpredictability of credit cycles in this segment.**

> *Buffett: "Risk comes from not knowing what you are doing."* — The microfinance credit cycle has surprised even seasoned management teams. This is a high-skill, high-risk banking model.

### Trend Narrative

**NIM is compressing sharply.** From elite levels of 8-9% in FY17-FY20 (reflecting the high-yield microfinance book), NIM has declined to ~5.9% in Q3 FY26. This is driven by: (a) the deliberate shift toward lower-yielding secured loans (housing, auto, personal), (b) rising cost of deposits as the CASA ratio deteriorates (from ~43% in FY21 to ~27% in Q3 FY26), and (c) competitive pressure on lending rates.

**Cost-to-income ratio is elevated and volatile.** Per the bank's press releases (which separate pure operating expenses from provisions), the true C/I ratio improved to ~47% in FY25 from a peak of ~77% in FY22. However, this remains above best-in-class private banks (40-45%). The Bandhan 2.0 transformation (new core banking system, branch expansion, hiring) is absorbing costs that have not yet translated into proportionate income.

**Earnings are wildly cyclical.** FY22 was a near-wipeout year (₹126 Cr profit, ROA of 0.09%) due to massive COVID-related provisioning on the MFI book (~₹2,524 Cr in total credit cost, consuming ~95% of PPOP). FY23-FY25 showed recovery, but 9M FY26 is deteriorating again with provisions spiking. **This volatility is the hallmark of a microfinance-heavy bank — exactly the kind of business Buffett warns about.**

> *Buffett: "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for bad economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."* — The microfinance credit cycle has repeatedly overwhelmed Bandhan's management, regardless of their operational skill.

### Buffett's Financial Fortress — Adapted for Banks

**1. Capital Adequacy — PASS (Adequate but declining)**

CRAR of 18.7% (FY25) is well above the regulatory minimum of 11.5%. However, the trajectory is declining from ~25-28% at IPO-era to ~16% in Sep-25 (including profits). This is natural as the bank leverages up, but it means the capital buffer is shrinking. **The bank has NOT raised equity since the GRUH Finance share-swap in FY20** — shares have remained steady at 161.1 Cr since then. This is a positive sign for capital discipline.

**2. Asset Quality — FAIL (Persistent NPA problem)**

**This is Bandhan's Achilles' heel.** GNPA has ranged from 3.8% to 7.2% over FY20-FY25, far above the <3% threshold for a quality bank. The microfinance (EEB) book is inherently volatile — every external shock (demonetisation, COVID, Assam crisis, cyclones) causes a spike in NPAs. The improvement to GNPA 3.3% in Q3 FY26 was driven significantly by large technical write-offs (₹1,270 Cr in Q3 FY25; similar levels continued in FY26 quarters), not just genuine recovery. **Net NPA of 1.0-1.4% looks better but is masked by aggressive write-offs.**

**3. Funding Stability — DETERIORATING**

The CASA ratio has collapsed from 43% in FY21 to **~27% in Q3 FY26**. This is a critical red flag. Low CASA means the bank is increasingly reliant on expensive term deposits and bulk funding, which compresses NIMs and makes the funding base less sticky. For context, best-in-class private banks like HDFC Bank have CASA ratios of ~38-40%. Bandhan's 27% is among the lowest in the private banking sector.

**4. Hidden Risks — MODERATE**

The restructured book post-COVID has largely resolved, but slippages remain elevated (₹1,310 Cr in Q3 FY26). The microfinance book continues to be the primary source of stress. The bank's shift toward secured lending (now 57% of the book) is the right strategic move but will take years to meaningfully change the risk profile.

> *Buffett: "It takes 20 years to build a reputation and five minutes to ruin it."* — For banks, one bad credit cycle can destroy a decade of accumulated profits. Bandhan's FY22 proved this — years of profits were wiped out in a single year of provisioning.

## 7. COMPETITIVE POSITION & MOAT ANALYSIS

### Moat Type: **Narrow and Eroding**

**1. CASA Ratio & Cost of Deposits — DETERIORATING (was a moat, now a weakness)**

CASA has collapsed from 43% to 27%. This directly increases cost of funds and compresses NIM. For context, HDFC Bank (~38%), Kotak (~50%), ICICI (~42%) are far ahead. **Bandhan is losing its funding moat.**

**2. Distribution & Branch Network — MODERATE MOAT**

6,300+ banking outlets is impressive, particularly in underbanked areas of East and Northeast India. This is difficult to replicate quickly. However, digital channels and fintech competition are reducing the value of physical distribution.

**3. Underwriting Skill — WEAK**

The repeated NPA spikes (FY21, FY22, FY25-FY26) across multiple credit cycles suggest underwriting skill in the MFI segment is average at best. The bank has not demonstrated through-cycle resilience that would constitute a moat.

**4. Fintech Disruption Risk — MODERATE**

UPI, digital lenders, and platforms like PhonePe/Paytm are increasingly serving the same customer segment. Micro-loans are being offered through apps, reducing the need for physical Bandhan doorstep agents.

**5. Regulatory Moat — MODERATE**

The banking license is a genuine barrier to entry. However, RBI has given licenses to several SFBs (AU, Ujjivan, Equitas) that compete directly with Bandhan in the MFI/small-ticket lending space.

**6. Susceptibility to Change — HIGH**

The microfinance business model IS changing. Digital lending, UPI-linked credit, and RBI's evolving MFI regulations are reshaping the landscape. Bandhan's core franchise (doorstep lending to women's groups) may look very different in 10 years.

> *Buffett: "We're looking for the absence of change to protect ways that are already making a lot of money."* — Bandhan's core microfinance model is facing significant structural change. This is NOT the kind of business where you can confidently predict the next 10-20 years.

### Key Competitors

## 8. SCALABILITY CHECK — Can This Business Scale and Is It Scaling?

### Part A: Unit Economics of Scaling

**Revenue Scalability:**

The microfinance business has modest scalability — each new loan requires a physical agent visiting the borrower's doorstep for collection. This is labour-intensive and doesn't scale like digital businesses. The shift toward secured retail (housing, auto) improves scalability as these products have lower marginal servicing costs and can be sourced through DSA channels. However, the transition is still in progress.

**Profit Scalability — Incremental Margin Analysis:**

| Year | Revenue (₹ Cr) | Rev Growth | Net Profit | NP Growth | Incremental NP Margin |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY20 | 12,434 | 61% | 3,024 | 55% | — |
| FY21 | 14,546 | 17% | 2,205 | -27% | **Negative** |
| FY22 | 16,694 | 15% | 126 | -94% | **Deeply negative** |
| FY23 | 18,374 | 10% | 2,195 | 1,642% | 123% (recovery) |
| FY24 | 21,041 | 15% | 2,230 | 2% | 1.3% |
| FY25 | 24,915 | 18% | 2,745 | 23% | 13.3% |

The incremental margins are volatile and often negative — driven by credit cycle swings. **This is anti-scaling behaviour.** Revenue grows but profits are wiped out periodically by provisioning surges.

**Capital Scalability:**

Internal capital generation rate of ~10-11% can support ~10-12% advance growth. For faster growth, the bank needs external capital. This is a constraint.

### Part B: Scaling Runway

TAM for financial inclusion lending in India remains enormous — only ~25-30% of India's adult population has access to formal credit. Bandhan has ~3 Cr customers out of a potential ~50 Cr underbanked adults. The runway is 15-20x if the bank can execute.

**S-Curve Position: Growth Phase (but with scaling friction)**

### Fair P/B Multiple Calculation (Gordon Growth Model)

```
Fair P/B = (ROE - g) / (CoE - g)

Where:
  Sustainable ROE = 12% (conservative, assuming normalised credit cost and NIM compression)
  g (growth rate)  = ROE × Retention = 12% × 90% = 10.8%
  CoE              = 12% (higher than typical 10-11% due to elevated risk)

Fair P/B = (12% - 10.8%) / (12% - 10.8%) = 1.0x
```

**With 12% ROE and 12% CoE, Fair P/B = 1.0x Book.** This means at a 12% ROE, the bank is just earning its cost of equity and is worth exactly book value.

If ROE improves to 14% (achievable if credit costs normalise and NIM stabilises):
```
Fair P/B = (14% - 12.6%) / (12% - 12.6%) = Not applicable (g > CoE, model breaks)
```

Using a simpler framework with g = 8% (more realistic):
```
Fair P/B = (14% - 8%) / (12% - 8%) = 6% / 4% = 1.5x
```

**Fair P/B range: 1.0x (bear, 12% ROE) to 1.5x (base, 14% ROE) to 2.0x (bull, 16% ROE)**

**At CMP ₹160 and BVPS ₹153, the stock trades at ~1.05x book — essentially at the BOTTOM of the fair value range.**
